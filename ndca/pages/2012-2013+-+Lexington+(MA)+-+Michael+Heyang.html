Additional warrants for utilitarianism.

First, respect for the equality of persons mandates consequentialism.
CUMMISKEY
[Cummiskey, David. Kantian Consequentialism. Published by Oxford University Press. 1996. (p.142).]
If I sacrifice some for ... may sacrifice some to save many.

Second, deontological side constraints assume a distinction between acting and choosing not act. Otherwise, those side constraints could apply to mutually exhaustive options and would not tell us which action is moral. This distinction is flawed because
A. The choice of inaction is an act in itself. When we will a particular end with our inaction, we are causally responsible for the resulting harms even if we were not the primary causal factor.
B. A natural state of affairs does not exist. The world is constantly changing, so events can be described only in relation to each other. We cannot say that an act disrupts the universal baseline of being while an omission lets nature take its course, because there is no such thing as nature’s course. It is just as accurate to say that your head collided with my fist as it is to say that my fist hit your head. So, failing to do good is morally equivalent to causing the harm that results from their state.
C. Negative rights require positive obligations to establish the institutions and infrastructure to protect them in the first place. Thus the existence of negative rights requires the existence of positive obligations as well.



Third, absolute standards are logically incoherent since they produce insolvable conflicts between moral duties. For instance, if I assume a debt that I become unable to pay, I can either break my promise to pay the debt, or steal the money from someone else, both of which would violate moral rules, making action both required and forbidden, which is contradictory. Consequentialist standards let us hierarchically order any list of potential acts in terms of some desired outcome.

Fourth, all moral systems collapse into an evaluation of end states, since end states are the only way to classify acts into moral categories. For instance, deontology claims that murder is always wrong, but what counts as murder versus justified killing is determined contextually by examining the outcomes of the act, most importantly whether it prevented death or serious injury to the killer.

Fifth, there’s no relevant distinction between persons because we’re all reducible to states of affairs.
PARFIT
[Reasons and Persons. Oxford University Press: 1984.]
In this imagined case, [Suppose] each half of my brain ... different descriptions of the same outcome.
Since grouping experience by individuals thus makes no sense, all that’s left to be morally relevant is the aggregate of all sensations. This commits us to utilitarianism because each individual’s personal identity or self-interest does not matter. Three implications: A) Absent the negative providing a warrant that there framework is consistent with Parfit’s proof of personhood or a different biological conception of personhood, presume the AC framework is true because the conclusion is correct even if defensive responses deny possible warrants and B) Frameworks premised on a collective group are non-sensical because we can only aggregate sensations.

C) Lack of personal identity undermines theories of Desert.
DRESSER
[Rebecca Dresser, J.D. Associate Professor, School of Law and Center for Biomedical Ethics, School of Medicine, Case Western Reserve University. “PERSONAL IDENTITY AND PUNISHMENT” Boston University Law Review]
