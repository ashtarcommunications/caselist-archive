1AC States

Contention 1: The Border

The US has deliberately resisted investing in transportation infrastructure at the Mexican Border due to the status quo focus on border security being the driving force behind all border policy.
Wilson, 11, Christopher E., Mexico Institute, Woodrow Wilson International Center for Scholars “Working Together,” Wilson Center, November 2011,

No additional funding for border transportation is coming.
Balido, 12 (Nelson, “Trade Makes Some Gains in New Transportation Bill, but Long-Term Plan Still Needed”, Border Trade Alliance, Http://Www.Thebta.Org/Tag/Canamex SW)
but Let’s Give Credit Where … up a Long-Term Transportation Plan.

This focus on security is rooted in the border’s traditional social function as the place where society defines itself. In enforcing the limits and conditions of entry, the image of the self and its relationship to the other are defined.
Salter 6 [associate professor of political studies at University of Ottawa, “The Global Visa Regime: Borders, Bodies, Biopolitics”, Alternatives 31]

This process of inclusion and exclusion that takes places at the boarders cements a very specific understanding of the Other as one whose welcome must be conditional.
Btihaj 06; AjanaPhD candidate in Sociology at Macquarie University; “Immigration Interrupted,” Journal of Cultural Research 10:3, July, p. 259-260

This conception of the Other is what justifies the states attempt to regulate its own internal makeup based on self-reflexive standards for being American.
Yegenoglu 03; MeydaAssociate Professor in the Department of Sociology at the Middle East Technical University; “Liberal Multiculturalism and the Ethics of Hospitality in the Age of Globalization”; Postmodern Culture Volume 13, Number 2

Attempts to manage alternatiy within the American body politic inevitably results in xenophobic violence.
JaneJuffer; 2009; Associate Professor of English at Cornell University; Compassion and Rage: The Face of the Migrant ; South Atlantic Quarterly 108:1

Our ability to draw lines between acceptable and unacceptable members of both local and global society is the key internal link to American violence both inside and outside our borders.
Hoffmann ‘7 (Kasper, International Development Studies at Roskilde University, May, “Militarised Bodies and Spirits of Resistance”, http://diggy.ruc.dk:8080/handle/1800/2766)

Plan

As an act of affirming unconditional hospitality my partner and I demand the United States federal government should substantially increase its investment in transportation infrastructure serving ports-of-entry along the United States-Mexico border

Contention 2: Solvency



Affirmation of unconditional hospitality radically changes our understanding of the body politic. The politics of exclusion and violence that come with protecting the group identity are transformed into a politics of openness.

Maurice Hamington;2010; Professor of Women’s Studies at the Metropolitan State College of Denver ;”Toward a Theory of Feminist Hospitality”; Feminist Formations, Volume 22, Number 1


The 1AC is the only possible starting point. Only immigration politics force us to confront Otherness on a national level, which is key to challenging the violence of the American body politic.

Gibson ‘03; Sarah Lecturer in the Department of Culture, Media and Communication, University of Surrey; “Accommodating strangers: British hospitality and the asylum hotel Debate”; Journal for Cultural Research, Vol. 7, No. 4


It’s true; this single act of openness won’t tear down the walls of US oppression in one fell swoop, but that isn’t the point. Ethics is only possible when we attempt to actualize the impossible. This ruptures the dominant system of ideology and creates space for new politics of Otherness.

Yegenoglu; ‘03; MeydaAssociate Multiculturalism and the Ethics of Hospitality in the Age of Globalization”; Postmodern Culture Volume 13, Number 2


Making each act a question of absolute solvency makes any ethical act impossible because no one action could ever solve all violence. Vote Affirmative to engage in a continuous struggle towards ethics, anything else is complicit in violence.

Bulley; ‘06; Dan “Negotiating ethics: Campbell, Ontopology, and Hospitality”; Review of International Studies; Vol. 32


Ethics can only be rooted in openness and responsibility to the Other. Only the face of the Other challenges the ability of the self to know and regulate the external world, starting anywhere else only furthers this.

Zylinska 05; JoannaReader in New Media and Communications at Goldsmiths, University of London; THE UNIVERSAL ACTS Judith Butler and the Biopolitics of Immigration; Cultural Studies Vol. 18, No. 4



Rhetoric matters- the way in which we rhetorically construct the places we advocate for shapes what they become.

Danielle Endresand Samantha Senda-Cook, Associate Professor @ Communication Location Matters and Assistant Professor @ Creighton University, The Rhetoric ofPlace in Protest ,Quarterly Journal of Speech, Vol. 97, No. 3, August 2011, pp. 257282



In round rhetoric matters. Public utterances are a sign of conviction. That means you have to be responsible for the ethics of what you advocate, means our impact turns stand on all flows.

Ronald Walter Green and Darrin Hicks ‘5 (Cultural Studies, Vol. 19, No. 1, pp. 100-126, “Lost Convictions: Debating both sides and the ethical self-fashioning of liberal citizens”, January 2005) (this card paraphrases arguments from Murphy, R. (1957) ‘The ethics of debating both sides’, Speech Teacher , vol. 6, no. 1. JSTOR wouldn’t give us the actual article, it was annoying)


1AC Valley

Contention 1: Right To The Streets

Life in the city is defined by density and by heterogeneity. Urban space, more so than any other mode of community, forces individuals to coexist continuously with others in dense and limited space. These aspects of urban life demand a unique approach to the production of urban space. We call this approach The Right To The City.
Don Mitchelle, Geography Prof @ Syracuse, The Right To The City, New York: Guilford Press, 2003, P.17-18

“There are several... to the city” (Lefebvre 1996 [1968], 174).

The Right To The City recognizes that urban dwellers, not the state or the market, must have the right to appropriate and dictate the production of urban space in a way that addresses their daily needs and experience.
Mark Purcell, Geography Prof @ U of Washington, Citizenship and the Right to the Global City: Reimagining the Capitalist World Order, International Journal of Urban and Regional Research, September 2003

“Everyday life...political community.”

Unfortunately, the vast majority of urban public space serves one purpose: the orderly and convenient use of an automobile.
Ainsley Judge, Twin Cities Bike Activist, Honor’s Thesis Submitted To The Macalester Geography Department, "Designing More Inclusive Streets: the Bicycle, Gender, and Infrastructure" , 2011, http://digitalcommons.macalester.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1028&context=geography_honors

““Share the Road”... function democratically (Mitchell 2003, 132).”

By cutting off each motorist from others and from the space, automobile dominated space becomes a space of isolation from others and from the city space. This destroys the social nature of most of the city’s public space, rendering it depoliticized.
Matthew Wietse Talsma, Geography Prof @ U Toronto, Technologies of the City and Technologies of the Self: Lessons from the Road, 2010, Google It

“The freedom... against traffic.”

“As in all the... state apparatus.”
This transition away from the city as a sight of vibrant public social spaces forces urban dwellers to cede control over the production of urban space. Only public space can provide the forum for the formation and expression of demands on urban space that are essential for exercising Right to the City.
Simon Springer, Geography Prog @ University of Victoria, Public Space as Emancipation: Meditations on Anarchism, Radical Democracy, Neoliberalism and Violence, Antipode Vol. 43 No. 2, 2010
“Such spatialized notions...recurring performances (Valentine 1996).”

The result is the violent disenfranchisement of countless urban populations. Ceding control of the production of urban space to the external forces renders whole populations disposable.
David Harvey,Professor of Anthropology at the Graduate Center of the City University of New York, Right To The City, New Left Review 53, September-October 2008

“Surplus absorption...its production.”

Plan


Thus my partner and I, as an act of reclaiming right to the city, demand federal investment in bicycle infrastructure along urban roads.

Contention 2: Solvency


Cycling, as an alternative mode of transportation, repoliticizes the streets as a public space by allowing individuals to interact with both other’s and the urban environment.
Zach Furness, Prof of Cultural Studies @ Columbia College, One Less Car: Bicycling And The Politics’ Of Automobilist, Temple University Press, 2010

“At the most basic level,... my lovely bicycle”

This in turn makes the promotion of the bicycle a key step in the actualization of the Right To The City. By forcing the individual to actively engage with space during transportation, bicycling produces the type of urban subjects necessary to shift the production of urban space back to the urban dwellers themselves.
Jen Petersen, Ph.D. in Sociology from New York University, “Pedaling Hope,” Magazine for Urban Documentation, Opinion and Theory, no. 6, 2007, http://www.monumagazine.com/monu6/Petersen_final.pdf

“This and other experiences...seeing and knowing.”

Federal investment in in bike infrastructure drastically increases biking. Test programs prove.
PRI (Public Radio International), “New study shows federal investment in bike infrastructure paying off”, 22 May, 2012, http://www.pri.org/stories/business/the-bright-side-of-biking9962.html

“Across the..the future."

Infrastructure investment is the key starting point. Everything else follows.
Ainsley Judge, Twin Cities Bike Activist, Honor’s Thesis Submitted To The Macalester Geography Department, "Designing More Inclusive Streets: the Bicycle, Gender, and Infrastructure" , 2011, http://digitalcommons.macalester.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1028&context=geography_honors

“While the...of transportation. “

We ask that you use your ballot to affirm our vision of urban space by joining us in our demand for federal investment in bicycle infrastructure. No one here controls the state and we think that’s fine, the production of urban space must begin with deliberation and politics among the people.
Mark Purcell, Geography Prof @ U of Washington, Excavating Lefebvre: The right to the city and its urban politics of the inhabitant, GeoJournal 58: 99–108, 2002

“Lefebvre’s notion...citizen enfranchisement.”
