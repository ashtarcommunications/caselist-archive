I affirm. Part 1 is the resolutional interpretation. Since New Oxford American Dictionary defines “pursuit” as “the action of following or pursuing someone or something,” the resolution is a question of which actions the US ought to take, rather than adopting mindsets or philosophical outlooks.

National security objectives concern protecting US interests and institutions.
Larsen 02 [Jeffrey A. Larsen. EXCERPTED FROM “Arms Control: Cooperative Security in a Changing Environment.” Copyright © 2002. Lynne Rienner Publishers] AJ


[WEIGHING] Prefer my definition since the word “objectives” creates a term of art since it deals with long-term goals rather than case-by-case evaluation. This also indicates a question of policy choices, not operational frameworks, since the resolution questions current objectives of the US as opposed to philosophical debates about security.

Digital privacy concerns the protection of data and confidentiality in digital media contexts.
Hung and Wong 09 [Humphry Hung and Y.H. Wong. Department of Management and Marketing, Hong Kong Polytechnic University, Kowloon, Hong Kong. “Information transparency and digital privacy protection: are they mutually exclusive in the provision of e-services?” Journal of Services Marketing. 23/3 (2009) 154–164. © Emerald Group Publishing Limited. Accessed 7/1/13] AJ


[WEIGHING] Prefer the definition since it draws on the philosophical foundation of “privacy” rather than just defining digital privacy in terms of a policy interpretation, so it best captures the resolution in terms of the philosophical conflict.
Thus the aff defends status quo government surveillance programs such as NSA wiretapping or strategic data mining. The neg must defend a competitive policy option that prioritizes digital privacy of citizens over national security.

PART 2 IS FRAMEWORK:
Since ought concerns moral obligations, I value morality.

The value criterion is maximizing expected well-being.

First, Util better explains the moral intuitions that ultimately guide our moral decision-making.
Sinnot-Armstrong [Sinnott-Armstrong, Walter, "Consequentialism", The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy (Winter 2012 Edition), Edward N. Zalta (ed.), URL = <http://plato.stanford.edu/archives/win2012/entries/consequentialism/. Accessed 7/2/13] AJ


[WEIGHING] Explaining intuitions is key since our intuitions are the starting points for ethical theorizing, so theories that have major conflict with intuitions are useless since they deny their own necessary starting points.

Second, public disagreement over what is best for the government means we can’t adopt complex theories like the categorical imperative, since people would inevitably disagree about such theories. Forcing our moral theory upon citizens, even if it is technically correct would create a tyranny of the majority and citizens would not comply. The only unifying aspect of citizens is their common security interest, since every person empirically continues to attempt to live and so we can reasonably assume people care about their well-being.
