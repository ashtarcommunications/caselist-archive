Contention 1: Call of Duty

Let me tell you a story about my Uncle Sam. He’s pretty famous. You’ve probably heard of him, even if you haven’t met him in person. I get along with him alright, but I’m not blind to his major character flaws either. He tends to get a bit belligerent with his opinions sometimes. And yes, he’s not exactly the most inclusive or tolerant person. But I think that everyone will agree that my Uncle Sam’s biggest problem has always been his proclivity towards violence. That was before he bought Call of Duty. Now he can’t get off the couch. He just sits in the dark, in his underwear, eating cheetohs and drinking diet coke. His eyes are red and glazed from the ever-present glow of the television screen. He barely even blinks. He never goes outside. He channels all that aggression into the war games he plays on his Xbomb. It’s not violent. It’s a simulation. It’s a game. He’s not killing people. He’s eliminating targets. Screens, not screams. Buttons, not bombs. Royakkers and van Est explain:

Contention 2: War Games

Now that I think about it, I don’t think Uncle Sam is better off the way he is. Where do we draw the line between reality and virtual reality? According to Peter Singer, “when faced with a dispute or crisis, policymakers have typically regarded the use of force as the “option of last resort.” Unmanned systems might now help that option move up the list, with each upward step making war more likely”. In a world where generals can kill people through a computer screen, I stand resolved:

THE UNITED STATES FEDERAL GOVERNMENT SHOULD PERMANENTLY DISCONTINUE THE USEAGE OF MILITARIZED DRONES IN IRAQ, AFGHANISTAN, SOUTH KOREA, JAPAN, TURKEY AND KUWAIT.

Our plan represents a shift in the way that we, as individuals, relate to violence. We think that the United States Federal Government’s use of militarized drones is a symptom of a larger problem within the dominant discourse of policymaking. This discourse privileges cost-benefit analysis over ethics, policy objectives over empathy and abstract calculation over human casualties.

Contention 3: You’re a drone too.

Introducing the debaterbot 2.0—the ultimate in policy-making technology. It’s not necessarily any smarter than the debaterbot 1.0, but it certainly reads faster and it’s definitely predictable. Predictability—that’s what you wanted, right? Program our debaterbot 2.0 with a hypothetical scenario and it’s guaranteed to calculate at least three ways that your scenario will result in nuclear war. That’s is what debate is supposed to be about, isn’t it? Reducing nuclear war to an abstract simulation, a scenario, a mere image that we can distance ourselves from through a reliance on repetition and hypothetical’s?

Don’t believe me? Just listen to this testimony from satisfied customer and former debater, Nick Schweitzer:

(2006) (Nick, Software Consultant in the Milwaukee area. Among various geeky pursuits, he also likes to think he’s a political pundit. “Everything Causes Nuclear War” Janary 24, 2006 http://www.nickschweitzer.net/2006/01/25/EverythingCausesNuclearWar.aspx)



“I have another geeky ..... leads to nuclear war damnit!


What Max and I are really trying to say is that the threat of nuclear war isn’t any more real to us than missiles are to the playstation warriors that drop them on Kabul. It’s not about bombs, it’s about balluots. It’s about winning the race to nuclear war. We can’t afford these kind of simulations anymore—not in debate, and certainly not in the real world. The dominant discourse of policymaking, in war, in peace, in policy debate, makes violence less real. There is no face to face interaction with people from other places. The topic countries are little more than coordinates on a map. Have you ever played Risk? Moving your soldiers from one brightly colored country to the next? Doesn’t it remind you of the resolution this year? I’m not sure that’s a comparison we should feel comfortable with. After all, militarism isn’t a game.

At the end of the day, debate is nothing more than a space in which we accept or reject certain values, ideas and truth claims. We will always win that debate is a place where a person can construct their political and ethical relationship to the world. If this is the primary purpose of debate, then we think that this debate should be about which team best relates to violence. We think that the dominant discourse of policymaking renders violence abstract and benign, making it more likely to occur and escalate. In contrast, the affirmative rejects mediatized versions of war that distance the killer from the killed and make large-scale violence easy to commit with a clean conscious. Your ballot might not “solve” problems in the traditional debate-sense, but we don’t really think that’s what debate is about. Even if we did decide to pretend to be policymakers, this debate would never change anything with regards to nuclear war, poverty, bird flu, terrorism, economic collapse or global warming. But it can change how we think about violence—we can choose to ignore it, or, we can choose to see it.
