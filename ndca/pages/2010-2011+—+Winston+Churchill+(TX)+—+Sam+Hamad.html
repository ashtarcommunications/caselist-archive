I affirm: States ought not possess nuclear weapons.

The affirmative can advocate the state abolition of nuclear weapons, but does not have to defend implementation of this policy. This is true for a few reasons,
1) Regardless of definition, "Ought" is a modal, auxiliary verb.
2) The only for me to evaluate the consequences of an action is if I am guaranteed a world where states don’t possess nuclear weapons.
3) Nations that currently possess nuclear weapons are very different in their structures, obligations and motives.

Ought implies moral obligation. Because ‘ought’ has a strictly positive connotation, ie there is a moral obligation to do something, ‘ought not’ means that there is no moral obligation.

The affirmative, then, can prove the resolution true in two ways: Either a) by proving that states have no moral obligations or b) by proving that states have a moral obligation to not possess nuclear weapons.
The obligations of states aren’t defined by morals. George Kennan explains:

[Professor Emeritus at the Institute for Advanced Study, Princeton; Morality and Foreign Policy; Foreign Affairs; 1986]

“Certain distinctions should be made before....needs no moral justification, nor need it accept any moral…”



Whether ought is a moral obligation or desirability, the best policy option is one that evaluates consequences. James Wood Bailey explains, `
Bailey, James Wood (Lecturer in Politics, Princeton University) “Utilitarianism, Institutions, and Justice” Page 9. Oxford University Press. 1997


A consequentialist moral theory can... dismiss them with moral sophistries or to ignore them as if we lived in an ideal world.


And, we decide which action is better based on the affect it has on human beings. A policy that decreases suffering is the best policy. Jamie Mayerfeld describes,
Jamie Mayerfeld, Associate Professor of Political Science at the University of Washington. Suffering and Moral Responsibility, Oxford Press. 1999. ISBN 0195154959, 9780195154955
We have a prima facie duty to relieve suffering...suffering cries out for its own abolition or cancellation.

Therefore the standard is the reduction of human suffering.

My advocacy is that states ought not possess nuclear weapons because of the threat they pose to millions of lives.

Negative


I negate: States ought not possess nuclear weapons.

I negate the resolution, resolved: States ought not possess nuclear weapons.

The word ought implies evaluating action through various considerations to ultimately find the absolute best thing to do. Ralph Wedgwood writes,

Conceptual Role Semantics for Moral TermsAuthor(s): Ralph WedgwoodSource: The Philosophical Review, Vol. 110, No. 1 (Jan., 2001), pp. 1-30Published by: Duke University Press on behalf of Philosophical ReviewStable URL: http://www.jstor.org/stable/2693596Accessed: 25/08/2010 01:09

"As I understand it then, the question 'What is the best thing for me to do?' ...what we have most reason to do', rather than 'best'."

In the context of a debate round the debaters take the roll of the advisor. If a world where states don’t possess nuclear weapons is the best option, you negate.

Evaluating the consequences of an action is the best way to determine whether or not it is the best option. Walter Armstrong writes,


An Argument for Consequentialism Author(s): Walter Sinnott-Armstrong Source: Philosophical Perspectives, Vol. 6, Ethics (1992), pp. 399-421 Published by: Ridgeview Publishing Company Stable URL: http://www.jstor.org/stable/2214254 Accessed: 06/07/2010 00:26

"A moral reason to do an act is consequential ...All such reasons are consequential reasons."

All of the harms associated with nuclear weapons have consequences in one form or another. The majority of literature on this topic discusses the benefits and harms of states possessing nukes. Thus, when decided whether they should or not, we ought to evaluate the ends.

The standard is minimizing suffering. Jamie Mayerfield explains the importance of reducing suffering,
Jamie Mayerfeld, Associate Professor of Political Science at the University of Washington. Suffering and Moral Responsibility, Oxford Press. 1999. ISBN 0195154959, 9780195154955
"We have a prima facie duty to relieve suffering...suffering cries out for its own abolition or cancellation."
