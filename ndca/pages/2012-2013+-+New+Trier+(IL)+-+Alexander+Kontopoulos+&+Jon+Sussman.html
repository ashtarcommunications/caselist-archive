Initial Email:

Jon Sussman on November 5th:¶ Hello Dr. Furness,¶ My name is Jon Sussman and I am a High School policy debater from New Trier High School in Winnetka Illinois.¶ This year we are debating about whether or not to affirm a resolution about Transportation Infrastructure Investment.¶ The wording of the resolution is "Resolved: The United States federal government should substantially increase its transportation infrastructure investment in the United States"¶ When my partner and I affirm the resolution, we defend that it is a good idea for the federal government to increase bike lane investment.¶ I have read your "Put the Fun Between Your Legs" partial dissertation and enjoyed it quite a bit. In fact we cite the paper in our affirmative argument.¶ Our argument is based around the idea that because the federal government focuses on highway investment and pretty much disregards bicycle lane investment, that it re-entrenches an auto-mobile dependent culture.¶ An argument that is frequently ran against us is that the State governments, rather than the federal government should be the ones investing in the bicycle lanes.¶ We normally make the argument that it is a federal responsibility to invest in the lanes, as well as that it is not any longer a radical critique of the relationship between the government and the oil companies if the state governments were to invest. Our argument in the end comes down to the assertion that federal investment is more indicative of a national priority to enable the cycling counter-culture than state investment.¶ I was wondering if you had any thoughts that you would be willing to share on the issue of federal vs. state investment, and if you would mind if I used your response as quoted evidence in a debate round?
His Answer:

Hey Jon!
Great to hear from you. It's funny because another debater from Texas got in touch with me recently to talk about bikes too...I love the random encounters that bring people together over ideas.

Well, first off, I'm flattered by you reading my diss but my book is dramatically better, if I do say do myself. I express my thoughts about some of what I think needs to happen with respect to prioritizing transportation and I'll send you a PDF of my book in a few minutes. But the short and sweet is that, historically speaking, leaving spending decisions to states--usually in the name of 'state's rights'--has often been a way for politicians and their parties to avoid implementing measures that are seen as 'liberal', particularly anything having to do with the environment. While some states like California, Oregon, Minnesota etc tend to take the lead over the fed on a lot of bike trans policy issues, they are the exception to the rule....and even in those cases they aren't doing anything to really undermine auto development & planning, rather, they simply make use of funds that are supposed to be utilized for non-automotive transportation. My point is that states already have way too much flexibility in terms of what they are allowed to do with trans planning which is why most states not only routinely redirect fed allotted $ for bicycling/pedestrian infrastructure into auto-based trans projects, they also tend to literally let money go to waste that is designated for bike-ped projects. More to the point, most states have never even taken the first steps to comply with ISTEA (legislation), which is to conduct studies to determine how, where or whether specific kinds of bike-ped projects should be carried out (I quote someone on this in Chap 3 of One Less Car, I think). Put simply, conservative politicians have been highly successful in making non auto-based development seem like its an envirofascist/hippie/liberal plot to force people out of their cars and onto bicycles and horses-and-buggies (see the first paragraph in my conclusion). While the fed tends to botch tons of important things, there's really no hope for a sustainable transportation future (ie a mix of public transit, rail, and pedal-powered trans technologies) unless the fed not only forces states into compliance with existing bike-ped-transit laws (which are insanely benign, and favored by most people in the US, regardless of political persuasion) but also initiates an unprecedented shift in priorities that would put such an agenda at the center of American policy debates (which, unfortunately, will never happen cuz of oil corporations are the wealthiest institutions in the history of history and they have bought both political parties). The only discretion states should have, IMHO, is 1) to work with their constituencies and the fed to decide where projects should be built, and 2) to have the flexibility to go above and beyond what the fed recommends as far as committing resources to sustainable trans (this would seem like a no-brainer given the habitual 'states' rights' discourse, but the courts also ruled, for ex, that CA didn't have the right to raise efficiency standards on cars a number of years ago...and the states' rights/energy independence repubs applauded without irony cuz in actuality all they really care about is appeasing the 1%, even if it means promoting policies that habitually make both state govts and economies less stable, less efficient, and ever more dependent on both 'foreign oil' and 'Washington'). Do you really think, for example, that Illinois would intentionally devote $ to transit or cycling unless the fed kicked down money? Maybe in Chicago because there's always been support for mass trans and bicycling....but the latter was only nurtured because old man Daly happen to like bikes and made the city build some infrastructure several decades ago (which his son continued). But aside from that, I doubt anything would happen in Illinois, or Indiana, or most other states if all decisions about trans spending were made by state govts. We can't wish for the occasional bike-friendly politician to act as savior, and we can't rely on states to decide whether or *if* they will commit to the kinds of trans systems increasingly found in every other advanced country.
Besides, states are almost all broke right now so it's literally a moot point to suggest that states are even capable of doing their own thing on trans policy.

And if the fed didn't spend millions of dollars a minute (or more) on war, prisons, and corporate bail outs, there would never have to be debates over where trans $ got allocated because there would be enough to go around (and building all that infrastructure would also create those 'jobs' that I always hear politicians yammering about....weird how that works, huh?)
