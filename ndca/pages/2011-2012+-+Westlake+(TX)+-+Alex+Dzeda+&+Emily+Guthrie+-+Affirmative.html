Avatar Aff UT 1ac


When I was younger, I wanted to be a cowboy. As I’ve grown, I’ve wanted to be many things. President, lawyer, physicist, cowboy, astronaut. The only one where limits do not exist is the astronaut. The possibilities were to be endless. I could have the world at my fingertip! I was not alone. Millions of pieces of literature about space exist, each giving a new perspective on the unknown. Unfortunately, there is a trend towards using space as a tool for expansion of the military and culmination of humanity, leaving hope of celebration of difference at the launch pad, as DeWitt Kilgore explains: [1]
Popular narratives that rely on the reflected glory of military adventure... It is through this kind of imaginative work that we develop the tools we need to change the future.


Perhaps the most well-known example of astrofuturisms is in the movie Avatar. For insight on the thought process of many while viewing the movie, Ryan Croken states: [2] [START PRESENTATION]
You enter the theater, Avatar begins, and you are catapulted... your spirit whispers to you, but there is no garden, it appears, to get back to, and, what’s worse, it’s all your fault.

But what happens when we can’t explain those emotions? Why does going to a movie make an entire species feel guilt? Guilty of what? Or is it guilt at all? It appears that only the mother knows, but she is gone. The images and messages in Avatar encourage viewers to take a stance of mastery over an other, even if the other is not known. It forces us to love ourselves and feel power over others because of it. Because we can never truly become one with the other, humanity is locked into a cycle of domination and mastery that prevents ontological valuation, as Stephen Norton explains: [3]
While a fundamental sense of lack is the state of Avatar's protagonist... Thus, the plot of Avatar functions as the latest reiteration of a myth of totality which attempts to remedy this lack with its symbolic ointment; a closer look at Avatar reveals a poison in the salve.


This desire for unity is a recipe for disaster. Every historical attempt to create harmony has ended in devastating genocide and destruction across all boundaries of identity. Instead of attempting to overcome the system, we must come to terms with it and acknowledge the impossibility of perfection, as Slavoj Zizek explains: [4]
Psychoanalytic 'essentialism' is paradoxical in so far as it is precisely psychoanalysis... totalitarian terrorism — only afterwards can the state limit its disastrous effects.


The question is, how do we stop it?

Join us in an inquiry into the psychological underpinnings of why we want to ride horses on Pandora. The experience of being confronted with the underside and the horrors behind the spectacles in Avatar force a confrontation with the mirror stage and allow for the celebration of culture and difference. Contemporary society is founded upon dictating how something is to be understood. Instead, we allow for people to learn how to look. Instead of looking at Pandora and seeing unobtanium and profit, there is an appreciation for beauty, respect for the Na’vi culture, and no human presence. This is peace. Mayra Gomes explains: [5]

However, in Avatar, in the game between 3D, movie, heroic saga... Through identification, a utopia of identity lives in the mirror, although the inflexion of disparate elements.

Call us crazy, but you’re just shocked. You don’t want to hear things that go against what you think is right. Hint hint: that’s a formula for getting nothing done. ONLY through endorsing a non-hegemonic idea can we allow for practical change to occur. It is insanity to repeat the same action and expect a different outcome. Slavoj Zizek explains:[6]
A short circuit occurs when there is a faulty connection... aware of another—disturbing—side of something he or she knew all the time.
