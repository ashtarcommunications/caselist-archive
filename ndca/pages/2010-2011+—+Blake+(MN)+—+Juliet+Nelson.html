The law is not good in itself—analyzing whether or not there is an obligation to follow it comes prior to defining a moral obligation as to what it is

Marmor( Andrei Marmor. Professor of Law at USC. The Nature of Law. Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy. 2007.
http://plato.stanford.edu/entries/lawphil-nature/ )

From a moral point of view, the rules of recognition, by themselves, cannot be regarded as sources of obligation to follow the law. Whether judges, or anybody else, should or should not respect the rules of recognition of a legal system, is basically a moral issue, that can only be resolved by moral arguments (concerning the age old issue of political obligation). And this is more generally so: the existence of a social practice, in itself, does not provide anyone with an obligation to engage in the practice. The rules of recognition only define what the practice is, and they can say nothing on the question of whether one should or should not engage in it. But of course, once one does engage in the practice, playing the judge, as it were, there are legal obligations defined by the rules of the game. In other words, there is nothing special in the idea of a legal obligation to follow the rules of recognition. The judge in a soccer game is equally obliged to follow the rules of his game, and the fact that the game is conventional poses no difficulty from this, let us say, ‘internal-player's’ perspective. But again, the constitutive rules of soccer cannot settle for anyone the question of whether they should play soccer or not. Similarly, the rules of recognition cannot settle for the judge, or anyone else for that matter, whether they should play by the rules of law, or not.




And, while harms of actions are important, ends-justified arguments about punishment and deterrence do not sufficiently warrant criminalization. Agency and active intent to harm others are necessary standards to use the criminal sanction. Luna writes

Erik Luna. [associate prof at University of Utah College of Law] “Overcriminalization: the politics of crime.” The American University Law Review. 2005. Lexis /TJ
When society designates as "crime" particular acts accompanied by a sufficient degree of subjective awareness or intent, it makes a critical moral judgment about the wrongfulness of such conduct, the resulting harm caused or threatened to others, and the culpability of the [*714] perpetrators. n53 And when society punishes an individual for having committed a crime, the magnitude of the sentence represents a concomitant decision about the degree of wrongfulness, harmfulness , and culpability, all in service of the legitimate goals of punishment - namely, to prevent future criminality and/or to justly punish individuals for past misconduct. n5 4 Each of the assumptions underlying the denomination of crime and the determination of punishment can be deemed necessary but insufficient criterion to invoke the full power of the criminal justice system. Having the common cold may be potentially harmful in some sense - after all, who wants to catch another person's illness? - but it seems ridiculous to claim that the "perpetrator" harbors the degree of responsibility and his "conduct" evinces the type of wrongdoing necessary to justify labeling him a criminal subject to punishment. n55 Instead, the criminal sanction should be reserved for specific behaviors and mental states that are so wrongful and harmful to their direct victims or the general public as to justify the official condemnation and denial of freedom that flow from a guilty verdict. In fact, these consequences for the individual distinguish criminal justice from all other areas of law . The state authority to deprive freedom or even life itself - the most potent action any government can take against the governed - is sui generis.

Contention 1 is addiction

a. The abuse of drugs is a brain disease
Alan I. Leshner. “Addiction is a brain disease, and it matters.” Frontiers in Neuroscience: the Science of Substance Abuse. Science, vol 278, no 5335 October 1997.

Although each drug that has been studied has some idiosyncratic mechanisms of action, virtually all drugs of abuse have common effects, either directly or indirectly, on a single pathway deep within the brain. This pathway, the mesolimbic reward system, extends from the ventral tengmentum to the nucleus accubens, with projections to areas such as the limbic system and the orbitofrontal corntex. Activation of this system appears to be a common element in what keeps drug users taking drugs. This activity is not unique to any one drug; all addictive substances affect this circuit. Not only does acute drug use modify brain function in critical ways, but prolonged drug use causes pervasive changes in brain function that persist long after the individual stops taking the drugs. Significant effects of chronic use have been identified for many drugs at all levels: molecular, cellular, structural, and functional. The addicted brain is distinctly different from the nonaddicted brain, as manifested by changes in brain metabolic activity, receptor availability, gene expression, and responsiveness to environmental cues. Some of these long-lasting brain changes are idiosyncratic to specific drugs, whereas others are common to many different drugs. The common brain effects of addicting substances suggest common brain mechanisms underlying all addictions. That addiction is tied to changes in brain structure and function is what makes it, fundamentally, a brain disease. A metaphorical switch in the brain seems to be thrown as a result of prolonged drug use. Initially, drug use is a voluntary behavior, but when that switch is thrown, the individual moves into the state of addiction, characterized by compulsive drug seeking and use.

b. The abuse of drugs is rooted in genetics
Paul R. Buckland. [dept of Psychological Medicine, Cardiff University, UK] “Will we ever find the genes for addiction?” Addiction. Horizons Review. October 2007.

For many years studies have been carried out into the genetic association of DRD2 and alcoholism. A recent meta-analysis [11] and a larger-than-average study [12] (1215 total subjects) of the association with the Taq1A1 allele both showed a significant association, albeit one with a small effect size, which may vary between popula- tions, hence the many negative results [13]. A number of other SNPs in the DRD2 gene region have also been studied and many positive results obtained (e.g. [14]). Similarly, cocaine addiction has also been found to be associated with dopamine system genetic variants [15], although not the Taq1A polymorphism [16]. If there is an association between the dopaminergic system and addiction, then there must be one or more functional changes in this system associated with the polymorphisms in question and this may be detectable before or following drug administration. There is a body of evidence that suggests that polymorphisms within and close to the DRD2 gene lower its expression or stability [17,18]. Indeed, the Taq1A polymorphism is associated strongly with a lowering of D2 receptor numbers in the human caudate nucleus [18] and lowering of D2 recep- tor availability expression in the ventral striatum [19]. A lowering of D2/3 receptor availability has been shown to be related to increased intravenous cocaine self- administration in rhesus monkeys [20]. However, this lowering is also related to impulsivity and it may be the impulsivity which causes the increase in cocaine self- administration [21]. This suggests that rather than being part of the addiction process, dopaminergic variation may be related to the initiation of drug taking due to the impact on behavioural traits, not on any intrinsic process involved in the pathophysiology of addiction. Genetic effects can, therefore, be divided into two cat- egories: those which directly affect the process which is perturbed during drug addiction and those which modify the known environmental effect, i.e. the liability to take drugs in the first place. Thus, while genetic factors may contribute 50% or so of the propensity to develop drug abuse (see below), a significant proportion of this, perhaps most of it, may be due to genetic factors which predispose an individual to use a drug for the first time or for reasons not related to reinforcement or dependency, and thus not related to the addiction process. Genetic differences may affect liability to initiate drug taking in other ways. Much work with alcohol has high- lighted the role of factors such as stress and stress-related genes in the propensity for alcoholism to run in families.

Immanuel Kant likens just desert to a debt. Citizens of a certain community enjoy the advantages gained from the rules imposed. The principle of fair play states that citizens must do their part to preserve the law. A person who wants the benefits of law without following it and exercising self-control is called a free rider. It justifies punishment for any wrongful act or injustice committed. The main idea is that the wrongdoer has done something that gave him undue advantage over the victim, thus just desert or the punishment for such wrongdoing restores the balance of justice.
