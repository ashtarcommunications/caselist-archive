B0RGz v2.0

The feminist movement began as a way to rediscover written women’s history and culture, initially through the exploration of old communities that had nurtured female creativity. Aside from the goal of finding neglected or forgotten women writers, the movement strove to forge an alternative literary tradition that could better represent the female perspective. From Christine de Pizan’s first publication about the relation of the sexes in the 15th century to the modern so-called ‘Third Wave’ of feminism, the movement has striven to explore language, biology, roles, development, and history.
In the modern era, with the successful recovery of many of these women’s texts, attention has turned to a question first raised in the late 70s: Shouldn’t feminist criticism formulate its own theory of practice?
The justifications seem to be sound. A central analysis of gender would directly address the meaning of body, sex, and gender. It would label gender differences. It would suggest possibilities for social change. Reliance on various assumptions, categories, and strategies, after all, serve only to become associated with non-feminist schools of thought which would weaken feminist theory.
The question then becomes what would comprise that central analysis. What ideologies are considered feminist and non-feminist? How can liberal, radical, separatist, post-structural, ecological feminism be brought together? Which would take precedence and which would be insignificant?
Diquinzio 93 (Exclusion and Essentialism in Feminist Theory: The Problem of Mothering. Patrice Diquinzio)
