Affirmative

Act 1

we begin our discussion
with an ambiguous message
the opening of Heidegger’s
essay on “Language.”
(Poetry, Language and Thought, 1971, p. 189)
Man speaks. We speak when... it is to be called – man.

we'd like to begin with
a rather clear suggestion
a moment of break
of new direction

we’d like to enact
a new mutation
an infrastructural rupture
a new kind of station

we’d like to give up
on an old formal structure
though we’re too wide to fit through
this needlepoint puncture

we have the resolve
to get off the train
stare back at our words
their legends of pain

n ‘o my body
make of me [one] who questions’
who builds the world of the YOU
in all insurrections

let’s invest in the building
an architecture of theoretics
to transport ourselves
through a new autopoetics

resolved, resolute
that the word is the structure
inside the transportation
from this dwelling to its rupture

we’re a federation of bodies
governing words
neither major nor minor
a scale of blue thirds

the time to be building
draws ever near
let’s invest in this infrastructure
the buck stops here

invest this time
this fleeting being
in words that lie
never quite agreeing

each little word
makes us through praxis
let’s not take two cabs
let’s make parataxis

words don’t matter
that’s the common perception
but not so fast
Sylvia Winter has a new conception
(Sylvia Wynter, prof. emeritus of Spanish/Portuguese and African/Afro-American Studies at Stanford, in Greg Thomas, “Proud Flesh Inter/Views: Sylvia Wynter.” Proudflesh: A New Afrikan Journal of Culture, Politics & Consciousness: Issue 4, 2006. http://proudfleshjournal.com/issue4/wynter.html, pp. 12-15)

Our second act
Makes the reasons clear
Why investing in poetry
Is nothing to fear

Confined by western language
The “savage” stops to chuckle
The jokes on western thought
For its self-deteriorating struggle

Ms. Wynter tells it straight
That our cognition is not free
Lest we change how the game is played
A side of ourselves, we will never see
(Sylvia Wynter, chair of African-American Studies at Stanford, “Ethno or Socio Poetics,” Alcheringa 2:2, 1976, p. 83)
The idea of the savage black, writes .... OF THE MAJORITY PEOPLES OF THE THIRD WORLD.


What technocracy has wrought
Is to make debate a home
For a single mode of thought
Detailed here in Heidegger’s poem
(Poetry, Language and Thought, 1971, pp. 1-6, Opening to “The Thinker as Poet” by)

Way and weighing
Stile and saying
On a single walk are found.

Go bear without halt
Question and default
On your single pathway bound.

When the early morning light quietly
grows above the mountains ....

The world's darkening never reaches
to the light of Being.
We are too late for the gods and too
early for Being. Being's poem,
just begun, is man.

To head toward a star - this only.

To think is to confine yourself to a
single thought that one day stands
still like a star in the world's sky.

The way we think
Has been years in the making
Enacting change
Will be no easy undertaking

Diverting from the single pathway bound
Will take a lot of building
The tool to pave us new paths
Karen Gagne argues is poetry

(Karen Gagne, doctoral candidate in Sociology at SUNY Binghamton, “On the Obsolescence of the Disciplines: Frantz Fanon and Sylvia Wynter Propose a New Mode of Being Human.” HUMAN ARCHITECTURE: JOURNAL OF THE SOCIOLOGY OF SELF-KNOWLEDGE, SPECIAL DOUBLE ISSUE, SUMMER 2007, 251-253)
“What is to be done?” and “What can we do?” we ... contribute to “the needs of mankind” (45- 46).


And since words transport
And our mobility’s up to us
We agree with Ruth Forman
That poetry should ride the bus
By Ruth Forman (Poems need to come from a deep connection to the people. This poem is the most beloved poem of “Poetry for the people.” Forman is trying to articulate a version of poetry that is for the people. Poetic knowledge should be from living with oppressed people.)

poetry should hopscotch in a polka dot dress
wheel cartwheels
n hold your hand
when you walk past the yellow crackhouse
poetry should wear bright red lipstick
n practice kisses in the mirror
for all the fine young men with fades
shooting craps around the corner
poetry should dress in fine plum linen suits
n not be so educated that it don’t stop in
every now n then to sit on the porch
and talk about the comins and goins of the world
poetry should ride the bus
in a fat woman’s Safeway bag
between the greens n chicken wings
to be served with Tuesday’s dinner
poetry should drop by a sweet potato pie
ask about the grandchildren
n sit through a whole photo album
on a orange plastic covered La-Z-Boy with no place to go
poetry should sing red revolution love songs
that massage your scalp
and bring hope to your blood
when you think you’re too old to fight
yeah
poetry should whisper electric blue magic
all the years of your life
never forgettin to look you in the soul
every once in a while
n smile
