Contention 1: Right To The Streets

Life in the city is defined by density and by heterogeneity. Urban space, more so than any other mode of community, forces individuals to coexist continuously with others in dense and limited space. These aspects of urban life demand a unique approach to the production of urban space. We call this approach The Right To The City.
Don Mitchelle, Geography Prof @ Syracuse, The Right To The City, New York: Guilford Press, 2003, P.17-18

“There are several... to the city” (Lefebvre 1996 [1968], 174).

The Right To The City recognizes that urban dwellers, not the state or the market, must have the right to appropriate and dictate the production of urban space in a way that addresses their daily needs and experience.
Mark Purcell, Geography Prof @ U of Washington, Citizenship and the Right to the Global City: Reimagining the Capitalist World Order, International Journal of Urban and Regional Research, September 2003

“Everyday life...political community.”



Unfortunately, the vast majority of urban public space serves one purpose: the orderly and convenient use of an automobile.
Ainsley Judge, Twin Cities Bike Activist, Honor’s Thesis Submitted To The Macalester Geography Department, "Designing More Inclusive Streets: the Bicycle, Gender, and Infrastructure" , 2011, http://digitalcommons.macalester.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1028&context=geography_honors

““Share the Road”... function democratically (Mitchell 2003, 132).”

By cutting off each motorist from others and from the space, automobile dominated space becomes a space of isolation from others and from the city space. This destroys the social nature of most of the city’s public space, rendering it depoliticized.
Matthew Wietse Talsma, Geography Prof @ U Toronto, Technologies of the City and Technologies of the Self: Lessons from the Road, 2010, Google It

“The freedom... against traffic.”

This transition away from the city as a sight of vibrant public social spaces forces urban dwellers to cede control over the production of urban space. Only public space can provide the forum for the formation and expression of demands on urban space that are essential for exercising Right to the City.
Simon Springer, Geography Prog @ University of Victoria, Public Space as Emancipation: Meditations on Anarchism, Radical Democracy, Neoliberalism and Violence, Antipode Vol. 43 No. 2, 2010
“Such spatialized notions...recurring performances (Valentine 1996).”

The result is the violent disenfranchisement of countless urban populations. Ceding control of the production of urban space to the external forces renders whole populations disposable.
David Harvey,Professor of Anthropology at the Graduate Center of the City University of New York, Right To The City, New Left Review 53, September-October 2008

“Surplus absorption...its production.”

Thus my partner and I, as an act of reclaiming right to the city, demand federal investment in bicycle infrastructure along urban roads.

Contention 2 is Solvency


Cycling, as an alternative mode of transportation, repoliticizes the streets as a public space by allowing individuals to interact with both other’s and the urban environment.
Zach Furness, Prof of Cultural Studies @ Columbia College, One Less Car: Bicycling And The Politics’ Of Automobilist, Temple University Press, 2010

“At the most basic level,... my lovely bicycle”

This in turn makes the promotion of the bicycle a key step in the actualization of the Right To The City. By forcing the individual to actively engage with space during transportation, bicycling produces the type of urban subjects necessary to shift the production of urban space back to the urban dwellers themselves.
Jen Petersen, Ph.D. in Sociology from New York University, “Pedaling Hope,” Magazine for Urban Documentation, Opinion and Theory, no. 6, 2007, http://www.monumagazine.com/monu6/Petersen_final.pdf

“This and other experiences...seeing and knowing.”

Federal investment in in bike infrastructure drastically increases biking. Test programs prove.
PRI (Public Radio International), “New study shows federal investment in bike infrastructure paying off”, 22 May, 2012,http://www.pri.org/stories/business/the-bright-side-of-biking9962.html

“Across the..the future."

Infrastructure investment is the key starting point. Everything else follows.
Ainsley Judge, Twin Cities Bike Activist, Honor’s Thesis Submitted To The Macalester Geography Department, "Designing More Inclusive Streets: the Bicycle, Gender, and Infrastructure" , 2011, http://digitalcommons.macalester.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1028&context=geography_honors

“While the...of transportation. “

We ask that you use your ballot to affirm our vision of urban space by joining us in our demand for federal investment in bicycle infrastructure. No one here controls the state and we think that’s fine, the production of urban space must begin with deliberation and politics among the people.
Mark Purcell, Geography Prof @ U of Washington, Excavating Lefebvre: The right to the city and its urban politics of the inhabitant, GeoJournal 58: 99–108, 2002

“Lefebvre’s notion...citizen enfranchisement.”
