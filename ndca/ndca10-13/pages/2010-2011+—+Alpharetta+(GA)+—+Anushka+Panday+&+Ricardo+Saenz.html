The last component of Bar On’s ethics, which Irigaray’s ethics complements, is hospitality. Hospitality is one of the key elements to an ethics of sexual difference because hospitality involves welcoming of others into one’s space while maintaining one’s identity. Hospitality takes place with the cultivation of breath. When the subject balances exhalation and inhalation, she carries that balance into the relation with the other. Through exhalation and speaking, the subject welcomes the other. Through inhalation and silence, the subject gives the other space to accept or refuse the invitation. One can fail the measure of hospitality in two ways: hostility toward others and allowing the other to overtake the self. The first danger, that the subject is hostile to the presence of another, occurs when a subject narrowly protects herself, her interests, or her loved ones. In this case, the subject views herself as belonging to a world in which her subjectivity has already been defined and the presence of another threatens that subjectivity. The subject jealously guards subjectivity. The second danger happens when the subject interprets hospitality as giving oneself over to the other who approaches her. In this case, the subject hastily gives herself over to the other’s control. Within an ethics of sexual difference, the subject must engage the other through hospitality. The subject in an ethics of sexual difference is always negotiating between the natural and the cultural, the present (which arises from the past) and development, and in order to continue that development she must encounter the other and return to the self. Hallie’s “From Cruelty to Goodness” and Bar On’s example of hospitality reveal how hospitality can be part of an ethics of sexual difference. According to Hallie, hospitality, “lies not only in something negative, an absence of cruelty or of imbalance; it lies in unsentimental, efficacious love” (1981, 26). Hospitality is more than abstract concepts, rules, or beliefs; hospitality is concrete action, it is effective. For the ethics of sexual difference, the concrete opportunities that present themselves are the many ways in which sexual difference presents itself: racial, religious, economic, and cultural. Within each of these manifestations, subjects have the opportunity to collapse the other into someone “like me” or to develop individually and together in peace (Irigaray 2005, 139). If the subject ignores the other, exploits the other, or makes the other into her own image, then hospitality fails. If the other encounters this difference in the spirit of compassion or kindness as outlined above then hospitality is enacted as well.
