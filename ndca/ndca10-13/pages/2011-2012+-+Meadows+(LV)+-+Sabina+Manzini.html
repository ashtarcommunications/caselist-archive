Moral Status AC


The resolution is a question of whether animals posses the qualities necessary to be considered under the scope of Justice. Justice refers to the proper distribution of goods and rights but prior to determining how to allocate rights, it is necessary to identify which beings are entitled to them which requires an evaluation of the being’s moral status.

Proving that justice requires any species or category of animals be given rights textually affirms the entirety of the resolution because in order for those rights to exist justice would require the recognition of the concept of animal rights. In addition, requiring the affirmative to defend that all animals deserve rights would impose an impossible burden due the diversity of beings with different capacities and qualities identified as animals.

Rights are not definitionally inviolable which is evident from the numerous occasions in which they are superseded by competing interests or conflicting rights claims. Rather, rights exist as requirements that an agent’s interests be considered and deemed important. Therefore, because having moral status implies one is entitled to certain protections or considerations, it is equivalent to possessing some type of right. Chandola writes.
Chandola, MV. “Dissecting American Animal Protection … legal rules over another. n78

In addition, “rights” is a diverse category that include a wide variety of protections and requirements. Because it is impossible to determine from the resolution which rights and their corresponding requirements ought to be evaluated, the round must focus on a determination of the more general possession of moral standing.

And, the core affirmative literature advocates equal consideration of animals without recourse to rights discourse, instead they identify rights as “moral standing”, which means requiring the affirmative to prove animals have rights excludes significant affirmative positions, decreasing topic education and putting the aff at an unfair structural disadvantage through the removal of key ground. This interpretation prevents negatives from running tangential critiques of rights discourse while conceding that animals ought to be given equal consideration, thereby increasing education through preservation of the central resolutional debate of human obligations towards animals.

Because there are a large number of theories of justice that deal with diverse categories of desert, such as theories of compensation, distribution of goods, and fairness, isolating any particular theory of justice for use in a framework fails to address the entirety of the resolution. If a being possesses moral standing, justice must require the recognition of it because if justice were to disregard certain relevant characteristics of beings, it could not properly assign due. As such, a sufficient affirmative burden is to demonstrate that nonhuman animals have moral standing.

I defend that justice requires the recognition of the rights of companion animals. The ASPCA defines companion animals.
