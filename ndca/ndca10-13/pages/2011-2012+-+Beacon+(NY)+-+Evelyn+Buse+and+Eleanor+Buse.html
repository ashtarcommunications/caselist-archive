The Planet of Junior Brown - 1AC at the New York City Invitational

Contention One: “The Planet of Junior Brown”

[The Planet of Junior Brown by Virginia Hamilton pages 5-7]

"What the astronomers saw through the telescope," said Mr. Pool, "changed their lives completely and changed completely the whole order of things. However, the asters were not men to blow their cool. They had a sense of humor. "'This might just stop the war for a while,' one of them said. Another said, 'And stop the population explosion.' "'And riots,' they boy had said, getting into the swing of it, 'and poverty and going to the Statue of Liberty every field trip day.' "After that," said Mr. Pool, "Junior Brown was always welcome at the planetarium. The astronomers never caught what his name was and never knew how they happened to call the amazing planet, Junior Brown. But they were glad to have a name for it right off like that. Now, they wouldn't have to name it My Old Lady, which had been the suggestion of one graduate assistant." "Oh, sure, man," Buddy Clark said, taking up the story from Mr. Pool. Buddy's voice was a soft, high purr. "Those astronomers measured and experimented and even ate their meals looking through that telescope. They had to admit that the ten-planet solar system after about fifty thousand years was stable and up there to stay for the rest of time." In the feeble light of the miniature solar system, Big Buddy's hands flipped over, palms down. His hands crossed and slowly uncrossed, while in the room the planets slowed into gliding, peaceful revolutions. There were three speeds to Mr. Pool's and Buddy Clark's solar system, the same as there were to any decent record player. It was Mr. Pool who had changed the pace of the planets while Junior watched Buddy's hands. But for Junior the slowing down of the planets had seemed like magic."


The Planet of Junior Brown's reconstruction of the solar system is a metaphor for power systems in our social universe that are responsible for the displacement and marginalization of individuals unable to occupy positions of power. This year's exploration of space in an educational setting invites us as debaters, and readers to recognize the dominant universal values of both systems to change them.
Lesley, 11 (Naomi, SDSU alum from the graduate program in Children's Literature; Solar Systems and Power Systems: Decentering the Naturalized Universe in Virginia Hamilton's The Planet of Junior Brown, Children's Literature Association Quarterly, Vol. 36, No. 2, pages 125-126)
"Virginia Hamilton, a distinguished...replicating oppressive values."


The underlying macrocosm that dictates social construction is whiteness, where social order is determined through departure of the norm. Whiteness as a polity permits the domination of nonwhites and reinforces racial privilege as a power structure at ease because of its exclusion from political forums.
Mills, 97(Charles W., Associate Prof of Philosophy at University of Illinois, Chicago 1997; The Racial Contract; p. 1-3)
"White Supremacy is...franca of our times."


The Planet of Junior Brown offers a new global theoretical framework needed to strategize forms of resistance to whiteness through the creation of a new model for the solar system which internally restructures the registers of our status quo value system for society.
Lesley, 11 (Naomi, SDSU alum from the graduate program in Children's Literature; Solar Systems and
Power Systems: Decentering the Naturalized Universe in Virginia Hamilton's The Planet of Junior Brown, Children's Literature Association Quarterly, Vol. 36, No. 2, pages 143-144)
"By moving toward...nature of society."

Thus Eleanor and I endorse the exploration of the Planet of Junior Brown and the invisible solar system.

Contention Two: “The Invisible Solar System”

[The Planet of Junior Brown by Virginia Hamilton page 13]

“But I lost heart, thought Mr. Pool. I could no longer teach in so rigid a regime. Maybe I was wrong. This change—has any of it really changed? Only now, through his work with Buddy Clark and the example of Buddy's devotion to Junior Brown, did Mr. Pool slowly begin to believe in himself again. He could no longer remember when he arrived at the curious notion that the two-legged beings on earth were only disguised as men. Working with Buddy, he sensed a whole new being lying in wait within the boy. Perhaps the human race is yet to come, thought Mr. Pool We must make life ready.”


Invisibility of spaces augment racial binaries through a racing of space: the partitioning of areas by their relation to dominant forms of civil society. Areas that cannot appropriate are negated and assist the design and standards of whiteness.
Mills, 97(Charles W., Associate Prof of Philosophy at University of Illinois, Chicago 1997; The Racial Contract; p. 74-75)
"Globally, the Racial Contract...cannot be followed."


The Planet of Junior Brown teaches us that disturbing the universe requires that we abandon our current tools of thought in order to engage in the invisible solar system disconnected from authoritative societal nature. Only then will we be able to recognize the invisible presences of the unknowable.
Lesley, 11 (Naomi, SDSU alum from the graduate program in Children's Literature; Solar Systems and
Power Systems: Decentering the Naturalized Universe in Virginia Hamilton's The Planet of Junior Brown, Children's Literature Association Quarterly, Vol. 36, No. 2, pages 141-143)
"Self-erasure is...in science lessons."


Deploying the disruption of the solar system as a metaphor works to restructure the systemic norms that hinder the capacity of the invisible to attain self agency while introducing the merits of alternative engagements in education.
Lesley, 11 (Naomi, SDSU alum from the graduate program in Children's Literature; Solar Systems and Power Systems: Decentering the Naturalized Universe in Virginia Hamilton's The Planet of Junior Brown, Children's Literature Association Quarterly, Vol. 36, No. 2, page 139)
"The challenge for...upon Junior involuntarily."


The metaphor of reconstructing the solar system also instills self authority and maintains autonomy in the powerlessness of those deemed socially invisible through the power of imaginative representations and a different order of rules.
Tribunella, 10 (Eric L. Tribunella, Associate Professor of English at the University of Southern Mississippi, “Children’s Literature and the Child Flâneur”, Children’s Literature, Volume 38, pages 85-87)
"Buddy teaches other...boys financially."


Contention Three: “The Hook”

[The Planet of Junior Brown by Virginia Hamilton page 210]

“The sound of Buddy's voice fell in a pattern of musical notations. 'We are together,' Buddy told them, 'because we have to learn to live for each other.' So that was it, he told himself. That was what he had forgotten all these years, or changed with the passage of time to fit with his loneliness. No, his Tomorrow Billy had taught him much more than life as mere survival.”


Institutional power and individual autonomy meet where teaching practice and education are valued within schooling and rhetoric. The Planet of Junior Brown exhibits the denigration of student autonomy that occurs in those who don't comply with the pedagogical norms reinforced by American culture, and American political values.
Jipson and Paley, 94(Janice Jipson, Ph.D in Education, works in the the Developmental Education Department at the National-Louis University, Nicholas Paley, Ph.D in Latin American Literature, Professor of Educational Leadership at George Washington University, “Literature/Curriculum/Authority/Absence: An Axiomatic Conversation”, English Education Vol. 26, No.4, pages 227-228, JSTOR)
"Nick: Listening to...Goals 2000."


These systemic norms are socially predetermined and structurally inculcated as a standard of whiteness in both written and spoken institutional criteria of 'common sense' and education. The “rigid regime” of schooling advances a project of assimilation that works to invalidate forms of intelligence and culture that withdraws from whiteness.
Lesley, 11 (Naomi, SDSU alum from the graduate program in Children's Literature; Solar Systems and
Power Systems: Decentering the Naturalized Universe in Virginia Hamilton's The Planet of Junior Brown, Children's Literature Association Quarterly, Vol. 36, No. 2, page 129)
"At the beginning...for professionalism."


Reading aloud and participating in The Planet of Junior Brown as a communal education allows varying facets of knowledge that work to free individuals suffering the repercussions of autonomous negation.
Lasevoli, 07(Dave Lasevoli, Teaches in the English Education Program at CUNY/Queens College. He is currently working on a study of educational initiatives in New York State prisons. “Behind Education: How Can You ‘Be the Book’ Behind Bars?”, The English Journal, Vol. 96, No. 6, Page 22-23, http://www.jstor.org/stable/30046747, JSTOR)
"The principal at...freer children."

This postmodernist relativism that does away with empirics. In the past, this theoretical rubbish meant intellectuals gave only empty resistance to demagogues that arose in Lenin’s Russia and Hitler’s Germany. Embracing postmodernism in modern society will lead us like swine to perdition.

Disease

Disease stands in for the projection of racism and xenophobia onto the postcolonial Other.
Schell 97 (Heather Schell ‘97 is a Ph.D. candidate in Modern Thought and Literature at Stanford University, Configurations 5.1 (1997) 93-133 “Outburst! A Chilling True Story about Emerging-Virus Narratives and Pandemic Social Change”)
"The self/non-self...a competitor."

Virus discourse posits the virus as the Other that demands the decontamination of Western imperial violence towards the subaltern.This is structurally tied to killer virus stories.
Dougherty 01 (Steven Dougherty, Spring 2001 The Biopolitics of the Killer Virus Novel, Cultural Critique 48)
"At the same time...it head on."


Their rhetorical strategy is both incorrect and oppressive. Racism and colonialism are external DAs to their plan. Vote against their representation to reject the racist baggage of disease fear scenarios.
Schell 97 (Heather Schell ‘97 is a Ph.D. candidate in Modern Thought and Literature at Stanford University, “Outburst! A Chilling True Story about Emerging-Virus Narratives and Pandemic Social Change”)
"Our current fascination...a different metaphor."


Virus fears represent transplanted anxieties over transgression of imperialism – containing the virus translates into containing and disciplining the Other. Vote neg to break the pollution taboo.
Schell 97 (Heather Schell ‘97 is a Ph.D. candidate in Modern Thought and Literature at Stanford University, “Outburst! A Chilling True Story about Emerging-Virus Narratives and Pandemic Social Change”)

Invisibility (vs. Millard South AC)


“The hibernation is over. I must shake off the old skin and come up for breath.

There’s a stench in the air, which, from this distance underground might be

the smell either of death or of spring—I hope of spring. But don’t let me trick

you, there is a death in the smell of spring and in the smell of thee as in the

smell of me. And if nothing more, invisibility has taught my nose to classify

the stenches of death. (Invisible Man page 580)”


We should embrace the alternative of invisibility as a means to deconstructing whiteness. Making blackness visible enables white culture to commodify it.

Sadomasochism

Sadistic and masochistic thinking governs the political. The sadistic thinker demands immediacy, quantity, objectivity, turning from victim to victim without consideration for any larger patterns or meaning. The masochist, in suspended anticipation of punishment, sees at the world in a grain of sand and lives eternity in an hour. This deeper reflection underpins true politics.
Reynolds 6 (Jack, Lecturer in Philosophy at University of Tasmania, ‘06, Parrhesia, "http://www.parrhesiajournal.org/parrhesia01/parrhesia01_reynolds.pdf" www.parrhesiajournal.org/par rhesia01/parrhesia01_reynolds.pdf, p. 96-98)
"While a concern...of more)."


The United States is enthralled in sadism today; crusading in a meaningless paradox against evil for the sake of hegemony.
Baudrillard 02 (Jean, Professor of Philosophy of Culture and Media Criticism at the European Graduate School in Saas-Fee, Switzerland, French sociologist, cultural critic, and theorist of postmodernity, The South Atlantic Quarterly, Volume 101, Number 2, Spring 2002, Duke University Press, p. 404-405)
"All the speeches...catastrophic process."


Thus Eleanor and I advocate to embrace the status quo.

"He coins a...worth playing."

Security

Security ensures apocalypse from now on – constructions of existential risk ensures annihilation.
Coviello 02 (Pever Coviello, Professor of English at Bowdoin, Queer Frontiers, p. 39-40.)
"Perhaps. But to...be done without."


The attempt to explore space reflects an urge to dominate. Space exploration expands the destructive potential of our worst impulses.
Bormann and Sheehan, 2009 (Natalie Bormann, Department of Politics, Northeastern University, Boston, and Michael Sheehan, Professor of International Relations at Swansea University, Securing Outer Space, 2009, p. 1-3)
"That day in October...the same order."
