Biopolitics AC

The criterion for the round is reducing government biopolitical control.

My sole contention is that nuclear weapons reinforce the biopolitical state.


Subpoint A is the Links

First, nuclear weapons are manifestations of a security perspective of foreign policy, reinforcing biopolitical control. Burke writes in 2007:

Anthony, Senior Lecturer in Politics and International Relations at UNSW, Sydney, “Ontologies of War: Violence, Existence and Reason”, Theory and Event, 10.2, Muse

Second, the destructive capabilities of nuclear weapons give states power over death, allowing extensive government biopolitical control.
Hardt and Negri write:

Michael Hardt and Antonio Negri, “Multitude: War and Democracy in the Age of Empire”, page 18, http://books.google.com/books?id=LM2leHxCCiIC&pg=PA18&dq=biopower+nuclear+weapons&hl=en&ei=FNJpTMCsCIK88gaggdWyBA&sa=X&oi=book_result&ct=result&resnum=1&ved=0CCgQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&q&f=false

Weapons of global…perhaps indeed of all being.

Subpoint B is the alternative

The Affirmative endorses a national defense consisting of civilian resistance.

Civilian resistance is an appropriate solution and substitute for nuclear deterrence. Churchill explains:

Robert P. Churchill, Annals of the American Academy of Political and Social Science, Vol. 469, Nuclear Armament and Disarmament (Sep., 1983), pp. 46-57, “Nuclear Arms as a Philosophical and Moral Issue” < http://smartaffs.googlegroups.com/web/Churchill+-+Nuclear+Arms...+Moral+Issue.pdf?gda=OPMAP14AAAD7QNf6gJDkzn4DQw5JLzn612LTU_NqqzQmkZ3rNLThC1q7BUMmJQ2_tEAMgoEojRxoNE7DtZLxoFPHfoJzHO3AmaXskJz9MiPYO5N101iKyuOwpdWz5ftt1dlzlu5J-bE>

A program of civilian resistance is preferable for multiple reasons.

First, it substitutes a nuclear arsenal and thereby does not promote a biopolitical state.

Second, civilian resistance is an effective means of preventing a foreign invasion, as well as a domestic oppressive government. It actively rejects a biopolitical government. Caplan writes:

Bryan Caplan, Humane Studies Review Volume 9, Number 1, Summer 1994. “The Literature of Nonviolent Resistance and Civilian-Based Defense”. < http://www.carolmoore.net/articles/nv-action-article.html>

Weaponitis NC

I value morality.

The standard is preserving life.

The Affirmative identifies nuclear weapons as the problem and cause of the impacts in the AC. This is unwittingly counterproductive for three reasons.

Contention One is OUR HISTORY.

The Affirmative’s focus on nuclear weapons only deals with a specific incarnation of our cultural obsession with super weapons. This ignores that nuclear weapons are not unique, but only part of a long historical progression dating back to the discovery that rocks kill more efficiently than fists.
Wagar writes:

Wagar, 1989 (W. Warren, Distinguished Teaching Professor of History at SUNY Binghamton, “Truth and Fiction, equally Strange: Writing about the bomb,” American Literary History, 1:2, Summer)

the real and imagined…weapons, but the vision.

This functions as a turn to the AC. The Affirmative focuses on criticizing nuclear weapons, instead of engaging in a discussion of our cultural addiction to the current “superweapon”. This allows for even greater weapons of mass destruction to be built in the future, wreaking more havoc and destruction.


Contention Two is MASKING MILITARY ADVANCES.
The Affirmatives comfortable anti-nuclear politics get co-opted and used as a tool to hide interventionism, allowing for more and more military action.

Schwartz and Derber write:

(William A. Schwartz and Charles Derber, 1989, “ The Nuclear Seduction: Why the arms race doesn’t matter – and what does”)

weaponitis has an irresistible … this threat is coopted.

This impact links into my standard, because it the criticism of nuclear weapons allows for militarism to expand. All the wars we fight now have only been allowed to occur because the peace movement is too busy blaming WMDs for violence and destruction.

Contention Three is BLAMING THE BOMB.
The Affirmative’s focus on weapons implies the existence of legitimate weaponry and constructs them as independent actors outside of human control.
Beier writes in 07:

(J. Marshall Beier is an Assistant Professor of Political Science at McMaster University, “Disarming Politics: Arms, Agency, and the (Post)Politics of Disarmament Advocacy,” http://www.cpsa-acsp.ca/papers-2007/Beier.pdf)

the idea of bad weapons… individualized agency to weapons.


This implicates the debate on two levels:
First, it functions as another turn to the AC. Assigning responsibility to these so called “bad weapons” ignores the fact that people are the root cause of the problem, and only allows for continued use.
Second, it at least cuts away solvency from the AC. The construction of weapons as independent actors outside of human control makes reduction impossible or seemingly unnecessary.
