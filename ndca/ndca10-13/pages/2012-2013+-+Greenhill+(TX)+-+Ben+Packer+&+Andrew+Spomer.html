During the Cold War, the continuous modernization of U.S. ICBMs resulted in sustainment of design skills and industrial expertise; however, this pattern is long over. The last ICBM design team was dissolved in the 1980s.94 Based on air force data, the Defense Science Board concluded that a serious decline in ICBM design capability would occur within five years [2011] and, with sustainment efforts within 10 years [2016].95 In 2006, the DoD Threat Reduction Advisory Committee had reached a same conclusion: In May 2006, a study of civil infranstructure for strategic strike concluded that the current low level of effort on new or modified strategic strike systems is insufficient to maintain the health of the infrastructure and to provide strategic options for the nation.96 In 2008, the DSB pointed out that, Industrial base skills . . . are in danger of significant further erosion in the areas of ballistic missiles (emphasis in the original).97 This report was regarded as so significant that it was included as an annex to one of the reports of the U.S. Strategic Commission. According to the 2009 report of the U.S. Strategic Commission, The infrastructure that supports two thirds of the strategic deterrent triadthe SLBMs and ICBMsis not being sustained . . . Assuming the United States is not ready to abandon these kinds of missile systems, it needs to preserve the option to replace them when required . . . The skills being exercised today for nuclear deterrent forces are almost exclusively related to the less demanding sustainment of systems first deployed many years ago.98 The limited effect of the Minuteman III life-extension program in preserving design expertise is rapidly coming to an end. The Minuteman III guidance replacement program was completed in 2008.99 The life extension program on the post-boost vehicle has also been completed.100 The impact of this on the industrial base is made worse by the overall situation in the U.S. aerospace industry which, as summarized by Aviation Week, is one in which many beleaguered U.S. aerospace manufacturers are trimming back amid continuing uncertainty over the nations long-term goals . . . 101 In a paper written for the U.S. Strategic Posture Commission, Robert Barker, former Assistant to the Secretary of Defense (Nuclear & Chemical & Biological Defense Programs) stated that, In the not too distant future, the infrastructure unique to strategic missiles will not be available for any new programs or to respond to major problems, should they develop, in deployed systems. Any reconstitution of capability will take years and will inevitably be accompanied by schedule slips and cost overruns.102 There has also been loss of expertise in the air force itself. No one in the air force project office has experience developing, or managing the development, of a new type of ICBM.103 In the 1990s, the air force abolished the Air Force Ballistic Missile Organization. This organization, which was formed in 1954, had managed the development of new ICBMs for approximately four decades. No significant design work on a new ICBM has been undertaken since the 1980s and the program office was downsized in 1997 to a small office supported by a prime contractor, Northrop Grumman, to manage and integrate the Minuteman life extension program.104 By 2005, the air force lost its only relatively modern ICBM, the Peacekeeper. Thus, the air force is losing experience in handling more modern ICBM systems. Under these circumstances, the 2008 mandate from Congress directing that the government itself perform systems integration functions should be reevaluated. If this mandate is applied to Minuteman replacement, the results may not be desirable. Even in maintaining the existing ICBM force, there has been a ten-fold increase in requests for engineering support from the ICBM Systems Division.105 The main reasons, according to the Defense Science Board, are the age of the support equipment and the reduced experience in units maintaining the equipment.106 New support equipment is not scheduled to become available until 2017, an obvious mismatch with the stated importance of the nuclear mission and the state of the existing equipment.107 The suggestion that theAir Force ICBMoffice should copy the Navy Strategic Program office is not nowa good idea. For decades, the navy has had a program office with operational responsibilities. Its missile, the Trident II (D-5), is still in production and is a much more modern missile to sustain. The navy has a much larger resource base than the air force from which to draw skills. This resource base supports the attack submarine force, the continued development of a new attack submarine, the Virginia-class, and initiation of the Ohio-class replacement program. The air force has none of these advantages. At this point, the air force cannot emulate the navy operation because of these fundamental differences. The navy does not operate refurbished 1960 vintage missile submarines carrying life-extended Polaris missiles using 4050-year-old support equipment and facilities. The naval bases that support the Trident were built specifically for this deterrent system. The air force , on the other hand, does operate 1960s vintage missile silos and launch control facilities (although the electronics have been upgraded). The 40-year-old Minuteman III missile has two stages that are even older, since they come from the older Minuteman I and II. Unlike the nuclear navy, the ICBM force has ceased to be a unique career field in the air force. The Defense Science Board has also pointed out that unlike the Navy SSP, the ICBM SPO has not worked with its industrial base to indentify and then fund incremental sub-system developments that would specifically retain critical skills.108 The impact of this is that, if a major problem with Minuteman develops, the root causes of design failure, should one occur, could be difficult to determine and correct.109 We do not know if a major problem will develop with the Minuteman III in the next twenty years, but like any other weapons system we must be prepared to deal with one if it does. Sustainment plans cannot be based solely on best case assumptions. To move in the direction of the navy model, the air force must first build up its in house technical expertise and retain the critical industrial expertise that will be necessary to sustain the ICBM force to 2030 and beyond. This will be difficult for a number of reasons: (1) increasingly tight defense budgets, (2), hiring freezes, and (3) the long-term effects of eliminating the ICBM force as a career field in the air force, diminishing valuable air force expertise. These factors must be taken into account. In the short term, there is a critical need to maintain the industrial engineering and design expertise that still exists. No one who was involved in the original design of the Minuteman III is still involved in the program today. The 2008 Defense Science Board report on Nuclear Deterrence Skills strongly urged an advanced development program for ICBM application . . . 110 Even more egregious, Expertise that provided designs for hardened and survivable launch control facilities, silos, communications, launch systems, reentry systems, and offensive countermeasures is not now available.111 Moreover, The declining trend of Air Force nuclear expertise has not been effectively addressed.112 Within the air force itself, ICBM officers are leaving after a single tour of duty and enlisted personnel rarely volunteer for ICBM services.113 The March 2011 OSD report on solid-rocket motors industrial capabilities recognizes that the ability to produce large solid motors is not in and of itself sufficient to sustain our ability to design new large solid-fuel ballistic missiles. It states that, Research and development programs, such as theAir Force ICBM Demonstration andValidation program, are required to preserve SRM [Solid-Rocket Motor] science and technology, engineering and design teams and their critical skills.114 This is a useful step, but it is not at all clear that a program this small will preserve essential design expertise. The implication of this as recorded in the 2006 Defense Science Board report is that, Current skills may not be able to cope with unanticipated failures requiring analysis, testing and redesign.115 In its 2009 report, the U.S. Strategic Commission noted the critical importance of ICBMs: The force is also immediately responsive in a highly controlled manner. And for the foreseeable future, there is no prospect that a significant portion of the ICBM force can be destroyed by a preemptive strike on the United States by small nuclear powers, including China.116 To sustain this capability, there is a critical need to get research and development activities underway on a follow-on system. If this does not happen the prospects of an ICBM force after 2030 will become illusory, with grievous implications for U.S. national security.
