Derive 1AC- State



OUR CENTRAL IDEA is the construction of situations, that is to say, the concrete construction of momentary ambiances of life and their transformation into a superior passional quality. We must develop a systematic intervention based on the complex factors of two components in perpetual interaction: the material environment of life and the behaviors which it gives rise to and which radically transform it.
--Guy Debord

Here is the situation…

We were in Boston to attend the Harvard debate tournament. We had left a couple of days early. This meant that we had time to be the typical tourists. Not that we are ever entirely typical. We went to the important sights. Went to see worshippers touch the highly polished shoe of the Harvard founder that some mischievous college student likely pissed on the night before. We went to several cool bookstores…perused the newsstands astonished at the wide selection. We walked the streets stopping for a moment to watch a street artist turn spray paint into a vision of space from another planet complete with Panda…thoughts about debate circulate through our heads and we cut short our tourist vacation and get down to business…back on the bus…red line…the bus-stop by Starbucks…get off and hike through the cold to the Super 8…do not disturb…we have debate work to do…


We aren’t going to talk about the tournament except to say that there was plenty of talk about space… we went 3-4…our worst tournament of the year…we should have been debating out rounds…we should have been talking about space…but very shortly space would the farthest thing from our minds…
By this point in the trip everyone wanted to leave…one more day…Dana says next year we are going to Cali…I won’t be here next year…Jake and I had to make Boston matter…


We spent the first part of the day walking the city with our entire group…


Did I know what a Derive was then?


No…


But we went on a derive…


Jake and I left the group behind…we took to the city with no purpose other than to wander…go to the places that drew us in and damn the rest…there was no purpose to this exploration…no ultimate goal…we were not seeking some product or place but rather seeing what this place had to say to us…
Guy Debord said of derivement that there is a:

“…sudden change of ambiance in a street within the space of a few meters; the evident division of a city into zones of distinct psychic atmospheres; the path of least resistance that is automatically followed in aimless strolls (and which has no relation to the physical contour of the terrain); the appealing or repelling character of certain places — these phenomena all seem to be neglected. In any case they are never envisaged as depending on causes that can be uncovered by careful analysis and turned to account.”

Simulations hide in our thoughts like secret militias peering out of street corners waiting to suck us dry...pulling at our already dwindling souls. They slip in without us noticing but like any machine there are glitches … it was a Monday night in Harvard square… We had been exploring the ins and outs of Boston in the beginning it was like our tourist romp. We wandered again in astonishment… My fantasy had become reality. Elite scholarship, great legacies… We walked through the city not knowing if we could find our way back…

But then that sudden change Debord speaks about on a Derive began to happen like a modified vision…or perception…somehow our unproductive walk had turned our eyes away from the Non-Stop-Ivy-League-Red-Brick-Façade to the people and things that lived in the city but were also apart from it…they didn’t participate the same way the rest of these people did…some were starving artists strumming guitars and singing with happy eyes and open guitar cases, others sold paintings or crafts they had made from found objects on the streets or bought with money they panhandled for the night before. Still the only piece of art I remember was not for sale and it didn’t belong to a starving artist with happy eyes…the art was drawn on a cardboard box opened up lengthwise so it could act as a large blanket or shelter. On it was drawn a face with a mouth carved into a long smile like a Cheshire grin teeth blacked out like broken factory windows. (SHOW THE ART WE DID ON THE PLANE) Under the masterpiece that would never hang on a museum wall like the Rothko’s Jake visited at the beginning of the year was a man half fallen off of a park bench…still asleep…vomit from the night before frozen fast to his chin…we left him sleeping…he still hasn’t left us…
It was like a door into a world I knew existed but never took a walk through…

We came across many men and women sleeping on the streets clenching on to their blankets pulling them up over their heads…one man had been reading a book... We gave money when we could but change couldn’t help…even a bandage couldn’t repair the shallowest of wounds. They were silent…dead in the eyes with sunken in faces hiding families and childhoods behind them just like the rest of our memories…but at some point they were thrown to the streets…not a place where the rest of society were people but ghastly spectators convincing them that they are the ghosts of the city. Hideous poverty and undeniable ignorance had been revealed…but somehow we were taken back in by the machine’s trick…keep wandering…soon enough the mythological Harvard would be shattered… we spent the evening walking through the streets watching the sun to go down while the artificial lights went up. It was frigid… we thought that we should go back but continued to walk and take the red line in …we found a paper on the train and flipped through it seeing the usual articles about Santorum calling someone a whore along with Iran’s nuclear news and horrifying success for Newt Gingrich giving us a better picture of the world we didn’t want to see…we arrived back in Harvard Square there was people surrounding the center, we peered around the news-stand…someone was yelling…we thought that there was some kind of Shakespearean play happening, maybe some street theatre. We had seen a camera crew in the square earlier…we didn’t want to ruin their shot, we didn’t want to walk through the play and ruin the entertainment for the audience. So we walked the other way around the newsstand, the same newsstand we had been in for half an hour, marveling at how many different literature journals it carried. As we came to the play, we saw a crowd, someone lying on the ground, someone standing and yelling…what WAS this? I asked a man nearby…he looked normal enough, he replied “Don’t worry about it…it’s just a hobo fight”….just a hobo fight. It was just a hobo fight. Just a hobo fight…nothing more than a cockfight, two animals tearing each other apart. As I looked, still trying to figure out what was happening, I noticed the man on the ground was bloody…unconscious…the man standing shouted “DON’T EVER FUCKING TOUCH ME AGAIN!” and he stalked towards the one on the ground…picked him up by the front of his bloody jacket…yanked him into a sitting position…drew back his leg…there was a dull, moist thud as boot met face, another as back of skull met pavement…there were gasps but mainly silence, the man lay on the ground not breathing. Our exploration was over. The city was no longer a wonderland for us to explore…we didn’t wander around the streets…we ran. We ran like our lives depended on it, they very well might have…I called 911, the first time in my privileged life I have ever called 911, reported what was a likely murder…called in the violence I had seen in a city I supposedly had grown to know so well, but I couldn’t figure out the address to tell the operator…the fantasy was broken, we had seen past the red-brick academic buildings, the stone carvings around the windows, to the beach beneath the street…we shivered as if it were a blizzard as we descended below to the subway street and boarded the red line.

No more space. We have had enough… if our dream lines are continually cut down on earth and tied to the will of the invisible hands of power we must first confront the spaces down here; the spaces where violence, the mundane, repetition all come into a distant concentration of power relations; the space where children are subject to the harsh reality of the streets. The space where we can no longer live fulfilling lives without flogging ourselves to death waiting, not for a savior to swoop down and take us away from all of it, but for a paycheck to feed ourselves. We certainly think that exploration is good…even when it forces us to confront horrible truths about ourselves…and in this sense perhaps we should explore space in the same way we explored Boston…but this debate is not about exploring space…it is about being caught in a situation that doesn’t allow us to escape until we resolve our position in relation to what we have experienced…as we rode the bus shaking there was no talk of space…or debate…I didn’t for one moment think about satellites that monitor racist weather…or the dumb asteroid that was coming to wipe us out…this situation had trapped us…swallowed us whole…
The bus-stop by Starbucks…get off and hike through the cold to the Super 8…
I laugh with tears in my eyes at the do not disturb sign…

We walk in tell the story for the first time…
It puts the adults around us in tears…we hold onto each other like the family we are…


We told the story and they were thrust into the situation…we realized that our experience could place others in the situation as well…and while it is not comfortable…in fact while it is horrifying it is really the first time that structural violence wasn’t just a concept for me…it was REAL…this for me was the most profound realization that structural violence is a problem we must struggle with before we can extract ourselves from this situation…we must stop being spectators who only speak on behalf of power


“it’s just a bum fight”…please still shop at our news stand…please don’t lose your appetite and abandon eating at our fine establishments…don’t alarm…it is only a bum fight…look at Harvard over there twenty yards away…the world must be ok… “It’s only a bum fight…” be another spectator here or move along there is nothing to see.

But we know there was something to see…there were real people there and we are real people as well. We need to give up on being spectators and become “Livers” who are a part of the situations we find ourselves in and then take those situations with us to others whose own situations will resonate with the space we occupied in Boston on that fateful Monday. This is the most portable form of education debate offers and our method for this debate.

This is Guy Debord-

The powers of a situation will certainly expand considerably in both time and space with the realizations of unitary urbanism or the education of a situationist generation. The construction of situations begins beyond the ruins of the modern spectacle. It is easy to see how much the very principle of the spectacle — nonintervention — is linked to the alienation of the old world. Conversely, the most pertinent revolutionary experiments in culture have sought to break the spectators’ psychological identification with the hero so as to draw them into activity by provoking their capacities to revolutionize their own lives. The situation is thus designed to be lived by its constructors. The role played by a passive or merely bit-part playing “public” must constantly diminish, while that played by those who cannot be called actors, but rather, in a new sense of the term, “livers,” must steadily increase. We have to multiply poetic subjects and objects — which are now unfortunately so rare that the slightest ones take on an exaggerated emotional importance — and we have to organize games for these poetic subjects to play with these poetic objects. This is our entire program, which is essentially transitory. Our situations will be ephemeral, without a future. Passageways. Our only concern is real life; we care nothing about the permanence of art or of anything else. Eternity is the grossest idea a person can conceive of in connection with his acts. Situationist techniques have yet to be invented. But we know that a task presents itself only when the material conditions necessary to its realization already exist, or at least are in the process of formation. We have to begin with a phase of small-scale experimentation. It will probably be necessary to prepare plans or scenarios for the creation of situations, despite their inevitable inadequacy at the beginning. To this end we must develop a system of notations, which will become more precise as we learn more from the experiences of construction. We will also need to discover or verify certain laws, such as that according to which situationist emotions depend on extreme concentration or extreme dispersal of actions (classical tragedy giving a rough idea of the former, dérives of the latter). In addition to the direct means that will be used for specific ends, the positive phase of the construction of situations will require a new application of reproductive technologies. One can envisage, for example, televised images of certain aspects of one situation being communicated live to people taking part in another situation somewhere else, thereby producing various modifications and interferences between the two.


Arbornauts 1AC - Fremont-Mills



The Earth...is our creator...it is the point of origin for all of us...
Primordial creatures crawl nameless through a sludge onto an endless featureless plain brought to life by some distant spark...many recall a God...others recall some magnificent bolt of lightning...crude matter is brought to life. I try to think back to the moment of creation...the truth is I can’t remember anything except for the sound my television makes when it turns on. All memory is lost with the exception of this bare earth. It is this humble dirt that binds us to the earth and creation. Toes plunge deep into tilled soil...fingers penetrate reaching out for the source of life like hands pleading to the sky asking favors from a silent god...they reach into the ground expecting to pull up a beating heart but they come up only with a handful of cool dirt and the faint scent of matter turned to decay. The magic of life is no longer a spectacle of Gods and lightning...it is the most mundane of things...dirt...water...and the scent of death. Somehow what it means to be alive is trapped in this earth like a cypher built into nature and yet despite this awesome gift we are still trapped here. Life it seems can only struggle...bowing its back out against the forces not of death, which is a condition of life, but of never having been in the first place...there are voids that aren’t this empty...in fact the void is the matter of this confrontation. How can we take the fragile act of living and remove it from the soil from whence it came? How do we leave our creator? How can life be uprooted and cast into the permanent vertigo of deep space?

In space there is no up and down...no right or left...it is only confusion...
Amidst the confusion the sound of a television turning on...
This is Eraserhead by David Lynch.
Eraserhead is the drain into which vertigo collects after it runs down from the high places circulating its madness like a flock of blackbirds. Madness is present everywhere in Eraserhead but for the moment we are not interested in some central aspect of the narrative of the film but rather an image that appears in the background. An ancillary detail...an image never intended to be the center circles with the blackbirds inside my head where ideas consolidate before they slip through the grate into irredeemable loss...erasure...This idea manages to kick itself loose and through its own brand of mundane magic has brought itself life and grown the necessary legs (freak legs anyone?) to travel to this plot of Earth in Fremont-Mills Iowa. This is the image of a tree in space. A tree removed from the Earth. Uprooted. There it sits on a common nightstand...in an average bedroom...almost forgettable and yet just strange and wild enough to cast its spell...

This is no longer going to be the common in-door house plant that some forlorn housewife whispers her deepest regrets to while listening to an episode of Regis and Kelly blare in the background like the sound of static from jet engines firing. That tree is trapped just like the people that live around it. It is bound firm to earth by the presence of a pot...roots gnarl and twist against the limits...in space there are no limits...there it sits in the matrix of the world at its place and time dreaming of space...it called out to us...there is no coincidence in the world of magic...it found its way here...the potted tree mimics the sick astronaut who believes because they have gone outside the boundaries of the earth’s mesosphere they have experienced the feeling of being in deep space...in space there are no limits...the unbound life energy of the astronaut turns slavishly back upon itself once it hits the slick impermeable surface of the spacesuit. The only thing the astronaut smells is the technology of recycling and its own stench. This is not life exploring space but rather life slammed into artificial stasis and launched into space...the equivalent of a fish pushing an aquarium onto land and claiming that it has explored land...Thanks Bill!

The tree like the human must go into space naked...without limits...no boundaries...face to face with death and the unimaginable expanse of space.

The sane or logical person at this point might raise an objection...but the tree cannot survive in space...it will freeze, or starve...this is true for the meantime, but this Tree will never become so tired of where it has been that it will lose its motivation to live...it will never give up on life because it has been choked to death by the forced proximity to itself...it can leave its creator and still live...it knows the cold rush of the void...it has been starved and neglected and still clings to life...it knows the loneliness of freedom...it has seen the darkest of nighttimes and been thrust into the scorching proximity of nebula that give birth to the furnaces at the hearts of stars. Any one of which contains revelations that are worthy of trading ones life for and yet still the presence of life is so unique in the vastness of space that the tree should hold on...and so should we for in space is the next stage of human freedom and the expansion of human experience to the edges of absolute extremity.

But we cannot go into the void like a march to our death. We must come up with a way of going to space that preserves the life of the being in space and likewise affords them the ability to touch infinity. Remember the magic of life is not a spectacle...there are no Gods and no need for lightning. We are interested in the magic present in life that is more ordinary...soil...water...the scent of death and a touch of some unexplainable intent. Trees incidentally have always had a special purpose...they are houses of spirits...cites of rituals...places where we come to turn our human intent into reality. Many cultures have taken up the practice of driving a nail into a sacred tree as a way of affirming some hope they have for the world or themselves or as a means to ward of some evil. In a practice born of slavery and colonialism a magic named Palo Mayombe was produced that would see objects covered in nails...We came to this place with one nail in the tree whose wish no one gets to know...even us. The second nail is a prayer that must come to pass if we are ever to come in contact with space and free ourselves from the trappings that keep us as we are. It is a prayer for safe travel...a prayer for survival...a prayer for freedom. The tree then becomes a magical object covered in nails. It is proof that true exploration of space is possible. It is a reminder of the fact that even the most meager of objects can find its way to space given the correct intent. It is evidence that human beings can become something more wild and free. It is all here with us now...the tree of life...the dark immanence of death...somewhere between these poles there is a secret locked in nature...the exact content of the secret that sparks life is not relevant...we don’t need to know...fortunately, this tree is a fountain from which the secrets of nature spring without reason...this tree holds the possibility of life turned loose into space...it has no limits...it rushes out to look upon the face of death and the universe...it has come back to us here to hold out the possibility that we could do the same.

Upon the return of the tree and the ritual complete we will place this tree back into the ground where it can bury its roots in the earth...we may plant a dead tree...that is a risk that we must take if we are willing to risk to look upon the face of the infinite...we are not afraid to die...this tree may be wounded but it is from those wounds that is learns how to live in space...away from its creator...it should remind us that we too can crawl out of our pots and with the kind of legs that you can’t see learn to walk in a world that might wound us but can never determine our fate...

There is one more film that flashes before the sound of the television powering down...it is from a movie called Offret by director Andrey Tarkovskiy. The opening scene of this movie connects back to the vision of the unpotted tree by some invisible strand of energy. The movie depicts a father and son on the shores of a shining sea planting a dead tree. When the child inquires about the intelligence of planting a dead tree the father remarks to him about the importance of ritual. He tells his child if you do something with the proper intent everyday for a long enough time it must change the world. The father then dutifully waters the tree and cares for it. He dreams one day it will come back to life...


Broken Burroughs Adding Machine 1AC - Blake



This is a 1908 Burroughs Company adding machine. It was invented, patented, and distributed by the Grandfather of Astronaut William Seward Burroughs; it was broken on Saturday December 10th during our mission to liberate the control center designated Dowling by a fucking cop masquerading as a Marxist Revolutionary. The ugly death suckers thought the machine too dangerous and thus sent in their secret agent to destroy it in the name of control and offer us a cash payment to kill it forever, keep your money but strike us down and we will become more powerful than you can possibly imagine.

Nietzsche himself claimed to be born posthumously. What does it mean to be born after your death? How could such a thing ever happen? Perhaps a demonstration…

This adding machine spent its first life in the bowels of capital…eating money and shitting out bank notes. We are here with the purpose of speaking this machine back to life. We invoke the same mutant force that caused William to realize his biological function and leave the gravitational pull of Earth, and a comfortable life as a maker of banking equipment for outer space. The adding machine likewise gives into the force of mutation…the forces of control only broke a syndicate calculator…it too, like Nietzsche, has been born after its death as a control panel for our spaceship. This diversion turns the machine against its original purpose liberating it from the slavery of control forever…

Our experience is falling to pieces about our ears, and its disintegration is a direct consequence of the development of consumer society. The phase of devaluation, and thus the possibility of diversion, is the work of contemporary history. Diversion has become part of the tactics of supersession; an essentially positive act. While the abundance of consumer goods is hailed everywhere as a major step forward in evolution, the way these goods are used by society, as we know, invalidates all their positive aspects. Because the gadget is primarily a source of profit for capitalism and the socialist bureaucracies, it cannot be used for any other ends. The ideology of consumerism acts like a fault in its manufacture, it sabotages the commodity coated in it; it turns what could be the material equipment of happiness into a new form of slavery. In this context, diversion broadcasts new ways of using commodities; it invents superior uses of goods, uses by which subjectivity can strengthen itself with something that was originally marketed to weaken it. The problems of tactics and strategy revolve around our ability to turn against capitalism the weapons that commercial necessity has forced it to distribute. Methods of diversion should be spread as an ABC Of The Consumer Who Wishes To Stop Being So. Diversion, which forged its first weapons from art, has now become the art of handling every sort of weapon. Having first appeared amidst the cultural crisis of the years 1910-25, it has gradually spread to every area touched by social decomposition. Despite which, art still offers a field of valid experiment for the techniques of diversion; and there's still much to be learnt from the past. Surrealism failed because it tried to reinvest dadaist anti-values which had not been completely reduced to zero. Any other attempt to build on values which have not been thoroughly purged by a nihilistic crisis will end in the same way; with recuperation. Contemporary cyberneticians have taken their 'combinatory' attitude towards art so far as to believe in the value of any accumulation of disparate elements whatsoever, even if the particular elements haven't been devalued at all. Pop Art or Jean-Luc Godard, it's the same apologetics of the junk-yard. Diversion, self-critical language, is our only possible means of communication. There are no limits to creativity. There is no end to diversion.

Immortality is prolonged future, and the future of any artifact lies in the direction of increased flexibility capacity for change and ultimately mutation. Immortality may be seen as a by-product of function: "to shine in use." Mutation involves changes that are literally unimaginable from the perspective of the future mutant. Coldblooded, non-dreaming creatures living in the comparatively weightless medium of water could not conceive of breathing air, dreaming, and experiencing the force of gravity as a basic fact of life. There will be new fears like the fear of falling, new pleasures, and new necessities. There are distinct advantages to living in a supportive medium like water. Mutation is not a matter of logical choices.

Recent dream research has turned up a wealth of data but no one has assembled the pieces into a workable field theory. By far the most significant discovery to emerge from precise dream research with volunteer subjects is the fact that dreams are a biological necessity for all warm-blooded animals. Deprived from REM sleep, they show all the symptoms of sleeplessness no matter how much dreamless sleep they are allowed. Continued deprivation would result in death. All dreams in male subjects except nightmares are accompanied by erection. No one has proffered an explanation. It is interesting to not that a male chimpanzee who did finger dab paintings, and was quite good too, went into a sexual frenzy during his creative acts. Cold-blooded animals do not dream. All warm-blooded creatures including birds do dream. John Dunne discovered that dreams contain references to future time as experienced by the dreamer. He published his findings An Experiment With Time in 1924. Dream references, he points out, relate not to the event itself but to the time when the subject learns of the event. The dream refers to the future of the dreamer. He says that anybody who will write his dreams down over a period of time will turn up precognitive references. Dreams involve time travel. Does it follow then that time travel is a necessity? I quote from an article summarizing the discoveries of Professor Michel Jouvet. Jouvet, using rapid eye movement techniques, has been able to detect dreaming in animals in the womb, even developing birds in the egg. He found that animals like calves and foals, who can fend for themselves immediately after birth, dream a lot in the womb and relatively little after that. Humans and kittens dream less in the womb and are unable to fend for themselves at birth.
He concluded that human babies could not walk or feed themselves until they had enough practice in dreams. This indicates that the function of dreams is the train the being for future conditions. I postulate that the human artifact is biologically designed for space travel. So human dreams can be seen as training for space conditions. Deprived of this vital link with our future in space, with no reason for living, we die.
Art serves the same function as dreams. Plato’s Republic is a blueprint for a death camp. An alien invader, or domestic elite, bent on conquest and extermination, would rapidly immobilize the earth by cutting dream lines, just the way we took care of the Indians. It is the relation of that element, that object, to a system in which it cannot be given a stable position that makes it “rotten.” Its excluded rottenness is necessary in the coherence of the system. As Bataille puts it: “rottenness is not just a middle term between the ear and the wheat. The inability to envisage, in this case, rottenness as an end in itself is the result not exactly of the human point of view but of the specifically intellectual point of view (to the extend that this point of view is in practice subordinated to a process of appropriation)” (OC, 2: 65; VE, 99).
Rottenness is an end in itself: it does not “go anywhere,” but instead wanders in the labyrinth, throwing off energy, disorder. Heterological science studies it, but not as a means to something else. Bataille’s science attains not a higher knowledge but a failure point of knowledge (just as to eat is always to vomit): “a burst of laughter is the only imaginable, definitively terminal, outlet- and not the means- of philosophical speculation” (OC, 2: 62; VE, 99.).
A matter that is an end in itself, leading nowhere; but this matter is always prior, an unformed, or definitively formless, “stuff,” mana which circulates through entities and communities, which opens their possibility, but which is never reducible to them. It is the charged, energy-laden material that draws together, and sets apart. Verneuil and his victims; andin another context, it is the junk that ties together people in primitive religions at the moment they expel it- when “it,” for example, is the mutilated corpse of a sacrificial victim. It is the energy of their violent communication. “What [formless] designates has no rights in any sense and gets itself squashed everywhere, like a spider or an earthworm” (OC, 1:217; VE, 31).
Thus for Bataille this sacred, charged, formless matter entails a religious experience, if one could use that word, but it is an “experience” that is not to be associated with established, Christian religions. (Bataille writes, “ WE ARE FEROCIOUSLY RELIGIOUS, and, to the extent that our existence is the condemnation of everything that is recognized today, an inner exigency demands that we be equally imperious” [OC, 1: 443; VE, 179].) Christianity proposes a homogeneous sacred, one that attempts to assimilate all evil, all rottenness, to its own system: evil is only the absence of God, it is not a positive entity, and one can work one’s way out of it on the way to a sacred belonging (Dante). The Christian sacred is ineffable, pure, holy, elevated above the rotten matter it must distance itelf from in order to continue itself. But Bataille notes the existence of early, now banned and cursed religions that maintained a cult of “bad matter”: Gnosticism.
In practice it is possible to give as a leitmotiv of Gnosticism the conception of matter as an active principle having an external, autonomous existence, which is that of darkness (which would not be the absence of light but the monstrous archons revealed by that absence), that of evil (which would not be the absence of good, but a creative action). (OC, 1: 233; VE, 47; emphasis Bataille’s)
Religion returns as a cult of materialism; not useful matter, to be sure, but “formless” matter that communicates its energy (“creative action”) between members of a cult. An elevated, pure God is doubled by a dead God or a multiple cursed divinity (such as the archons of Gnosticism). And along with this matter there is an energy, given the Sadean context of Bataille’s writing of this period, that is inseperable from a death drive. In some ways this “religion” is more rigorously atheist than was Sade’s; Sade, as is clear enough in La Philosophie dans le boudoi, needed God: The Sadean orgasm could only reach its peak intensity when the libertine was enraged in blasphemy. Sade was in effect already developing a model of sacrifice avant la letter: God’s sacrifice was integral to the transgressive act. The sacrifice of God and the production of “sacred matter” (the expelled, murdered, formless mess left over after the orgy) were inseperable. Sade’s dead God and his fiction are coterminious.
Bataille’s religion, then, must take up from where Sade’s necessarily blasphemous nonreligion ends. In a sense Bataille must be conscious of the religious double bind in which Sade find himself, the necessity of positing God only to blaspheme and murder him. But where Sade, locked in his fiction, hits a dead end, with Dolmance regretting that he cannot believe in the God he blasphemes, Bataille affirms the virulence of the dead God erupting in and against society. This is the practice, if one can call it that, of Bataille’s heterological science, a science that ends not in self-satisfied knowledge but in the horror of a revolting sacrifice God exists only in his death, his expulsion, his violation. It seems that Bruno’s immanent, material God has come back in Bataill, but with his status as uncontrolled eruption of energy now made explicit: endlessly opening the violent duality of matter, God does not “exist,” but he incessantly dies, since the matter he inaugurates is the very destruction of any coherent principle- and God is the ultimate principle. If laughter is the terminal point of scientific activity, then God is the terminal point of laughter: “When God realized he was an atheist he died laughing.” Bataille’s dead God is not mere fiction, but myth and tragedy (tragedy being the highest myth, that of the death of the king, the leader, the most high).

(Peter Dickens, Visiting professor of Sociology at the University of Essex, “The Cosmos as Capitalism’s Outside,” The Sociological Review, 57: 66–82)

The imminent conquest of outer space raises the question of ‘outside’ and ‘inside’ yet again. Capitalism now has the cosmos in its sights, an outside which can be privately or publicly owned, made into a commodity, an entity for which nations and private companies can compete. As such the cosmos is a possible site of armed hostilities. This means, contra Hardt and Negri, that there is an outside after all, one into which the competitive market can now expand indefinitely. A new kind of imperialism is therefore underway, albeit not one attempting to conquer and exploit people ‘outside’ since there are no consumers or labour power to exploit in other parts of the solar system. Ferrying wealthy tourists into the cosmos is a first and perhaps most spectacular part of this process of capital's cosmic expansion. Especially important in the longer term is making outer space into a source of resources and materials. These will in due course be incorporated into production-processes, most of which will be still firmly lodged on earth. Access to outer space is, potentially at least, access to an infinite outside array of resources. These apparently have the distinct advantage of not being owned or used by any pre-existing society and not requiring military force by an imperializing power gaining access to these resources. Bringing this outside zone into capitalism may at first seem beneficial to everyone. But this scenario is almost certainly not so trouble-free as may at first seem. On the one hand, the investment of capital into outer space would be a huge diversion from the investments needed to address many urgent inequalities and crises on Earth. On the other hand, this same access is in practice likely to be conducted by a range of competing imperial powers. Hardt and Negri (2000) tell us that the history of imperializing wars is over. This may or may not be the case as regards imperialism on earth. But old-style imperialist, more particularly inter-imperialist, wars seem more likely than ever, as growing and competing power-blocs (the USA and China are currently amongst the most likely protagonists) compete for resources on earth and outer space. Such, in rather general terms, is the prospect for a future, galactic, imperialism between competing powers. But what are the relations, processes and mechanisms underlying this new phenomenon? How should we understand the regional rivalries and ideologies involved and the likely implications of competing empires attempting to incorporate not only their share of resources on earth but on global society's ‘outside’? Social crises, outer spatial fixes and galactic imperialism Explanatory primacy is given here to economic mechanisms driving this humanization of the universe. In the same way that they have driven imperializing societies in the past to expand their economic bases into their ‘outsides’, the social relations of capitalism and the processes of capital-accumulation are driving the new kind of outer space imperialisms. Such is the starting-point of this paper (See alsoDickens and Ormrod, 2007). It is a position based on the work of the contemporary Marxist geographer David Harvey (2003) and his notion of ‘spatial fixes’. Capitalism continually constructs what he calls ‘outer transformations.’ In the context of the over-accumulation of capital in the primary circuit of industrial capital, fresh geographic zones are constantly sought out which have not yet been fully invested in or, in the case of outer space, not yet been invested in at all. ‘Outer spatial fixes’ are investments in outer space intended to solve capitalism's many crises. At one level they may be simply described as crises of economic profitability. But ‘economic’ can cover a wide array of issues such as crises of resource-availability and potential social and political upheavals resulting from resource-shortages. Furthermore, there is certainly no guarantee that these investments will actually ‘fix’ these underlying economic, political and social crises. The ‘fix’ may well be of a temporary, sticking-plaster, variety.

If we retain this original sense of “radical evil” as it appears in Kant’s ethical thinking, the thinking that is the source of contemporary ethics of human rights according to Badiou (p. 8), then it turns out that Badiou’s own proposals look very Kantian indeed. Notice, first of all, that for Badiou as for Kant the ethical imperative does not derive from the experience of being or of a multitude of empirical facts about human beings, but from something immortal and transcending. The truth event, although prepared somehow in the order of being, comes to the subject, or rather, somehow transforms the human animal into a subject, from a horizon that is outside of space, time, and causality. Badiou could easily affirm Kant’s contention that it is “a contradiction to look for the temporal origin of the moral constitution of a human being.”9 Kant stresses pure reason as an immortal source of the moral law, while Badiou is content to leave nature of the revelatory power of eternal truth open and undefined. But for both this immortal calls for an allegiance to itself on the part of a subject who is disinterested in anything but the requirements of universal truth. For Badiou, a truth event generates a single, formal imperative: “the sole maxim of consistency (and thus of ethics): ‘Keep going!’ Keep going even when you have lost the thread, when you no longer feel ‘caught up’ in the process, when the event itself has become obscure, when its name is lost…” (p. 79). (Does this not look like another version of the categorical imperative that obligates the will independently of any material content)? Precisely in the way that Kant describes radical evil, Badiou too speaks of the human being falling from grace by betraying the formal imperative of consistency, by putting self-interest and self-love ahead of the immortal truth. Evil emerges for Badiou, not because of the effects of human action on others, but because of a disorder in the way the subject responds to a revelation of truth. Evil is measured in other words, not by what is done to others, that is, by the horrible suffering even well-intentioned men cause (that would smack too much of the ideology of rights for Badiou), but by failures in what Kant would call the subjective will. Badiou does not speak of freedom the way Kant does, but one would have to surmise that for him, this fall or swerve from the truth is freely undertaken: one willfully relaxes back in to the status quo, one gives up on one’s principles, and one chooses totalizing power and contingencies, rather than the concrete universality of truth. Evil represents a contamination of the purity of one’s insight, whether it is political, artistic, religious, or amatory. So, my claim here is that Badiou shares with Kant 1) a formalistic ethics grounded in a subjectivity transcending the ordinary universe of cause and effect and 2) a conception of evil that stems from false choices or from a will that deranges the primacy of truth. To this we can add another resemblance: 3) a non-gradualist approach to moral reform. For Kant, radical evil stems from the will’s original distorted ordering of incentives that puts self love ahead of a disinterested allegiance to the moral law. The only way to change that root orientation or propensity to sin in the human species is by an act of will. The gradual acquisition of virtue, for example, will not change the will since that acquisition itself could have “evil” motives. True virtue, for Kant, represents an efect and not a cause of a change of heart; it is the result of a pure resolution to stay true to the demands of reason and the moral law, come what may. Citing John 3:5, Kant says, “And so a ‘new man’ can come about only through a kind of rebirth, as it were a new creation and a change of heart.”10 From an empirical, phenomenal standpoint such a change is as utterly unpredictable, as inexplicable as evil itself. Badiou shares Kant’s belief that a commitment to truth requires a total transformation of the human subject and not a gradual reform. In an interview with Peter Hallward, the translator of his Ethics, Badiou uses words like “seizure,” “irruption,” and the “incalculable,” (109, 125), expressing his appreciation for the French Revolution and the Maoist Cultural Revolutions for their faithfulness to their truth events. Badiou has no patience for the detail work of legal and political institutions of liberal democracies that strive to bring about gradual reforms in the human condition. Rather, as Kant or indeed Kierkegaard does, Badiou seeks a conversion in being that makes man new in an instant, or at least in an instant places him on a righteous path. Elsewhere, Hallward notes that Badiou embraces what we could call the puritanical virtues of a dedicated revolutionary: "Criteria of rectitude and 'purity' certainly play an important, perhaps fundamental, role in many aspects of Badiou's work." 11 For both Badiou and Kant, then, the emphasis is on the subject as an agent of moral good and evil. The sufering that is brought to the world as a consequence of the subject’s behavior is for both thinkers secondary. Thus, the absolute good or evil for Kant “could be only the maxim of the will and consequently the acting person himself as a good or evil person.”12 This is a crucial passage. We are still under its spell in modern ethics, as signaled by Maeve Cooke’s claim: “To be congruent with the normative self-understanding of late modernity, therefore, any account of moral evil must conceive moral disposition as something for which the individual subject ultimately has to bear responsibility.”13 This, however, sidesteps any account of moral evil as that which those who are in subjection have to bear, as though victims were merely contingent conditions, while vicious hearts were necessary conditions for the existence of evil.
