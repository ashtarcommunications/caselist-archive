Further, issues of due process come prior to normative questions of desirability because of the nature of justice.

And, There is no morally relevant distinction between human animals and non-human animals in terms of due process. Social interaction and the implied rules that result are not unique human experiences. Gruen Explains,
Gruen, 10 (Lori Gruen – 2010 [Professor of Philosophy, Wesleyan University] The Moral Status of Animals, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, The Metaphysics Research Lab. GW)

Perfect solvency not a requisite obligation for rights in a justice system.
Observation 2: The affirmative advocacy is that all animals, regardless of species be granted access to due process rights when being punished of a crime. I reserve the right to clarify in CX.

Contention 1: In the status quo, trials for non-human animals are anomalies. Instead, the norm for punishment is a silent execution often obfuscated with the rhetoric of humane “euthanization”. No legal obligation exists to report the use of capital punishment on non-human animals. Girgen 03 Explains,
Girgen, 03 explains (Jen Girgen, “the historical and contemporary prosecution and punishment of animals”, http://www.animallaw.info/journals/jo_pdf/lralvol9_p97.pdf 12-may-2003, date accessed sept 6th, 2011 O.G.)

Kniaz, 95 (Laura G. Kniaz, J.D., University at Buffalo, Copyright (c) 1995 Buffalo Law Review (: Animal Liberation and the Law: Animals Board the Underground Railroad) 1995 (lexis))

Dunayer, 04 (Joan Dunayer, Animal Rights Activist, 2004, Speciesism, p. 140)
Amendment 5 and 14… life, liberty, or property.

And, Legal rights protect the individual from the interests of the greater society. These rights are not subject to be weighed and denied. This is not exclusive to human animals. Rollin Explains,
