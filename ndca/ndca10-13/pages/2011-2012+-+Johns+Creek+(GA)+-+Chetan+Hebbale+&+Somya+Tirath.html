Observation Two: The Dispossessed

Ursula K. Le Guin’s novelThe Dispossessedtells a story of Urras and Anarres, a double planet system. Urras is dominated by a competitive libertarian worldview. The Urrasti are obsessed with status, wealth, and power-- the society has descended into an insatiable quest for material possession in spite of the vast inequality and poverty plaguing the planet.

Anarres is a barren moon, colonized by those who have left Urras to escape the greed, war, and oppression. Here they have constructed a society without government, completely decentralized and founded on principles of mutual aid, altruism and self-sacrifice. There is no poverty on Anarres. Despite the scare resources the Anarresti have ensured that all are treated equally - no one will eat while the other starves.

The Dispossessedtells the story of Shevek, a brilliant Anarresti physicist who travels to Urras to develop a General Temporal Theory. Over the course of the novel, Shevek witnesses first hand how profit and the wish for power corrupt every person and every action – but he also realizes that both the Urrasti and Anarresti society have stagnated because of the walls built up between them.

Terra, Earth, is also represented in the novel, but only as a dead world spoiled by humanity, now governed by total centralization.

The story is a warning- Terra and Anarres are both possible futures for the United States. Anarres is a not a blueprint for a perfect static utopia, but offers the hope as an ongoing process of freedom and mutuality. However, Anarres cannot come to us- the United States must initiate the journey of exploration on its own.
Stillman 5 [Peter G. Stillman is Professor of Political Science at Vassar College “ The New Utopian Politics of Ursula K. Le Guin’s The Dispossessed” – pg. 68-69, Chetan]

In the face of those difficulties, The Dispossessed gives readers a warning..... and make the attempt.

The Dispossessed disrupts the idea of time as a linear progression towards the “end of history”— the story of the past is never finally told and the future is never fully closed.

Only uniting past, present, and future can save either world. The wall built up between the real world of Urras and the utopia of Anarres prevents change in both societies- Anarres loses its connection with past history on Urras, and Urras loses the utopian hope represented by the possible future of Anarres.
Davis 2005 [Laurence, PhD in Politics @ Oxford, The New Utopian Politics of Ursula K. Le Guin’s The Dispossessed, Ed. Davis and Stillman, p. 3-9 GAL]
