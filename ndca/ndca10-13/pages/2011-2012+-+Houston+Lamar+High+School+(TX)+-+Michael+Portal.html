Contractualism w/ a little skepticism

I negate the resolution; resolved: Justice requires the recognition of animal rights

I value Justice, as demanded by the resolution.

Take a second and ask your self “What is the definition of justice?”

Now, ask yourself if your conception of justice is the same as those in other countries?

Or how about other states? Or cities? Or households? Or even in your own home? Or even among us three?

You should agree that morality is a subjective and variable entity. These variations mean that our conceptions of moral obligations, or normative reasons to act, are solely reflections of our society, not of objective truths. J.L. Mackie[1] explains,

The argument from ... of objective values.

You may say: My justice is not that different from yours; there must be some universal morality. It seems logical, there must be some baseline for truth. The problem is, there isn’t. We would be incapable of understanding such truths, even if they did exist. We can doubt the validity of moral obligations, because it is illogical for us to be penalized for not acting on a moral principle we don’t understand. Because as Mackie explains,

Even more important,... meaningless but false.

Mackie’s problem with a universal morality would appear to allow for murder. This is not true, though it does give us reasons to be a skeptic. We can doubt morality but still be good citizens. The answer is found in contracts. A mutually agreeable hypothetical contract binds the individual to action, but also doesn’t infringe upon their will. We can make contracts between ourselves to have normative reasons to act, but not pretend to have a full understanding objective moral truths. Thus the standard is Contractualism, which does not demand or forbid too much but does create a mutually agreeable moral contract.

Contention 1: Animals cannot enter into the contracts that create morality

David Gauthier[2] writes:
We have already n....rationally grounded morality.

Carl Cohen[3], Professor of Philosophy at the University of Michigan, concludes my position,

The holders of ... and do possess rights.

I am not advocating for animal abuse, for we can be nice to animals if it pleases us, but I am saying there is no morally compelling reason to give rights to animals for which they cant understand and reciprocate.

I negate the resolution; resolved: Individuals have a moral obligation to assist people in need.

Observation 1: The resolution is a descriptive statement, insofar as it questions whether or not an individual has a specific moral obligation. This is distinct from a prescriptive statement where one may prove we should have a moral obligation. Arguments dealing with comparative value are dismissing the question of the resolution. Thus the affirmative must prove that individuals have this obligation and the negative must prove that they do not.

The value is Morality

We create morality, denying this fact denies the original premise of our reasons to do anything; we are the moral actors, we are the moral creators, the reflective self is the source of moral obligations and in turn morality.

Korsgaard[1] explains

Thus, the value criterion is consistency with being human

Contention 1: Free will is a pre-requisite to moral obligations

A moral obligation must be possible for an individual to fulfill, since an obligation to do the impossible is going to unreasonably demand too much of the individual. Along the same lines, the obligation must be agreeable to the individual, since if it is not the individual may not obey it. Thus, free will is a precondition for moral agency and moral obligations. Our actions are just the culmination of other factors out of our control. Our lack of free will prevent us from having moral obligations, merely legal obligations or communal obligations.
