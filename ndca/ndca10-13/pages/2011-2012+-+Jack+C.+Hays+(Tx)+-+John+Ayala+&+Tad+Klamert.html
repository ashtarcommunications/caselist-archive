Affirmative - Spectral Forces 1AC

The history of American space exploration is filled with fiery political rhetoric that calls on our ‘frontiersman’ roots to promote feelings of adventurism in the name of getting there first. Since Kennedy, we’ve called on our nationalistic roots to restore American confidence; beating the enemy to space, comprehending, mastering it first. This Cold War era mentality has far from left us and we still view space exploration through a lens of the ‘new sea’ and just like that frontier, it must be mastered
Jordan 2003(John W, Assistant Professor of Communication at the University of Wisconsin-Milwaukee, Kennedy’s Romantic Moon and its Rhetorical Legacy for Space Exploration, pg. 209-212, Muse)
This American, ‘frontier’ mentality has divided the world into the sacred and the profane, the Good and the Evil. The frontier is posited as a challenge that we as Americans must face in order to reify our greatness, to fulfill our errand into the wilderness. This logic has empirically resulted in genocidal violence in the name of civilization
Spanos 2011(William V, Professor of English and Comparative Literature at Binghampton University, The Exceptionalist Sate and the State of Exception: Herman Melville’s Billy Budd, Sailor)
Not only that, but the continuation of an American National Identity rooted in the frontier produces spectral forces that risk annihilation of the planet
Spanos 8(William V, Professor of English and Comparative Literature, American Exceptionalism in the Age of Globalization: The Specter of Vietnam, pg. 95-97)
Thus, we affirm the resolution from a different standpoint. Our affirmation that the United States federal government should substantially increase its exploration of space beyond the Earth’s mesosphere comes from not a collective national identity, but from a collection of counter-memories. In looking at history through the perspective of those who have been at the receiving end of the American frontier myth, we reject the dominant ontology
Clifford 2001 (Michael, Associate Professor of Philosophy at Mississippi State University, Political Genealogy After Foucault: Savage Identities, pg. 134-137)
We must engage in debates that relate to space exploration’s perpetuation of the frontier mentality – only through these intermingling of discourses can we challenge the proliferation of violence
Siddiqi 2010(Asif A, assistant professor of history at Fordham University and member of advisory board at Shahjalal University of Science and Technology, Sputnik and the Soviet Space Challenge and The Soviet Space Race with Apollo. Competing Technologies, National(ist) Narratives, and Universal Claims: Toward a Global History of Space Exploration, Technology and Culture, Volume 51, Number 2, April 2010, pg. 438-440)

Our pedagogical approaches must encompass a challenging of dominant narratives of history – to engage in a universal narrative that forecloses other perspectives is to allow an understanding of space exploration that divides the world into the sacred and the profane and perpetuates the American frontier myth
Trofanenko 5 (Brenda, Professor in the Department of Curriculum and Instruction at the University of Illinois, The Social Studies, Sept/Oct)

We believe that politics in the status quo has forgotten the ontological question. We bring those questions into the realm of the political and policy discussion. The 1AC can be understood as an onto-political stance that fuels oppositional movements against the triumph of the frontier
Spanos 8 (William V, Professor of English and Comparative Literature at Binghampton University, American Exceptionalism in the Age of Globalization: The Specter of Vietnam, pg. 23-24)
