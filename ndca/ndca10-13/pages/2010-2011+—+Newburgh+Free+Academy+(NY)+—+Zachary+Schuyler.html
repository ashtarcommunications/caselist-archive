Theory standards:

-Reciprocal Burdens: The affirmative and negative burdens set up by this affirmative are fair. Fair burdens allow equal ground on each side and allows for optimal clash. This framework gives the negative and affirmative an equal chance of winning and allows for a lot of clash on the issue of whether nuclear weapons should have been created. Reciprocal burdens are key to fairness because it allows both sides an equal chance at victory and alleviates topic bias.

-Depth: The framework set-up by the affirmative allows for a deeper level of discussion by taking the topic a step further. It now analyzes the original purpose of nuclear weapons and the intention for creating them. This level of depth allows for more education, another theory voter.

Observation 3: Affirmative’s Choice: The affirmative should be allowed to choose the framework and definitions for the round.

Theory Standards:

-Time Skew: The affirmative has a time disadvantage when it can’t choose the framework. If you allow for the negative to choose the framework the affirmative only has four minutes to adapt to the negative’s framework. This wastes minutes of the 1ACs time and makes the 1AR horrible. Time is key to fairness because time determines a debater’s ability to make arguments and win the round. Even a period of 30 seconds could be 3-5 arguments meaning any amount of time skew is enough to vote off theory.

-Strategy Skew: Without affirmative choice the negative has an unfair advantage. They can nit-pick any part of the framework and win the round. The negative can ruin the entirety of the 1AC just by changing the definition of ought. With affirmative choice the negative must take on the entirety of the affirmative, allowing some affirmative strategies to be effective. This would allow both sides an equal chance of winning the round. Strategy skew is key to fairness because it allows for affirmatives to have a fighting chance against the nit-picky negatives. This prevents negatives from winning a majority of rounds because of luck.

-Substantive Education: The “affirmative choice” method allows for more discussion on resolution relative literature. It allows the debaters to discuss nuclear weapons and its intents instead of an argument on which ethical theory would be best to evaluate nuclear weapons with one or two mentions of nuclear weapons. Substantive Education is the core of the Education voter because it is what the topic wishes to educate us about.

We conducted a computerized literature review using the keyword `needle exchange' for English language articles published from 1993 through to the end of 1997. We examined the references in articles identified by our literature search and contacted NSEP experts to identify additional articles. Books with articles on NSEP were also included, but abstracts from conferences were not. Articles that were only available in abstract form at the time of our previous report were included if they presented at least some new data.
We included only articles that addressed the impact of NSEP upon drug use rates, injection-risk behaviors, hepatitis and HIV (Table 1) [12-35]. Serial cross-sectional studies addressing the impact of NSEP upon the IDU community were included as this a key question about the effect of NSEP Otherwise cross-sectional studies were excluded, as they cannot address trends over time and serial cross-sectional studies of particular cities were included only if the data for NSEP participants and nonparticipants were analyzed separately. All changes reported are statistically significant at P < 0.05. If there was more than one statistically significant finding, representative findings are reported. Some studies appear more than once in Table 1 because they addressed more than one outcome or used more than one analytic approach; each is considered a separate study. Studies of cost-effectiveness, changes in sexual risk behavior (there were few of these), and the impact upon referrals to drug treatment were not included. We assumed that successful publication was a rough proxy for quality and did not use a quality scale as in our previous report.
We identified a total of 24 articles that have been published since our previous report. The impact of NSEP on drug use rates was addressed in 10 articles, on injection risk behaviors in 12 articles, on hepatitis B and C in one article, and on HIV in 11 articles. The studies were generally of a higher quality than those available in 1993 with a wider range of evaluation designs. In particular, ecological and case-control designs were more common, and prospective studies of HIV incidence had greater statistical power and were therefore analyzed using more sophisticated methods than was possible with the small prospective studies that were available in 1993. Control groups were more often used in the studies measuring NSEP impact upon injection risk behaviors and blood- borne pathogen transmission. Half of the studies were conducted in the USA.

Of the 10 studies diseases rates, four serial cross-sectional studies examined the impact of NSEP on drug use rates in the IDU community and the remaining six assessed NSEP impact on drug injection rates of IDU using the NSEP Some studies used mean IDU age to investigate whether young people had begun injecting as a result of the NSEP Seven studies reported reductions in measures of drug use, two reported no change, and one an adverse effect associated with NSEP The latter finding, in a drug treatment cohort, was a greater decline in the percentage of IDU who never used the NSEP and who continued to inject, compared to IDU who ever used the NSEP This observation has been made once before [36], and likely represents self-selection: IDU who expected to continue injecting drugs may have been more likely to select NSEP use. Of the 12 studies examining the impact of NSEP upon blood-borne infections, six reported beneficial effects for NSEP, four reported neither a beneficial nor an adverse effect, and two reported an adverse effect.
