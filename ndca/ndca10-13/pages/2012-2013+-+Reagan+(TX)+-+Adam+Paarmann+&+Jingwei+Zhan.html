1ac suicide bomber


Debate puts Bobby and I in a dilemma, a paradox of responsibility: we have to be affirmative - configured by a linguistic frame. The resolution we are hailed by an indeterminate other, to justify road, planes, trains and automobiles as relief to a particular type of oppression and asked to choose: to attend to a particular huddled mass or the entire huddled mass to save from impending doom… but we are just people in a room pleading for a ballot, using the currency of oppression as justification as a win the moment that we decide to decide. While this is not specific to debate, the structure of a tournament and the game has the affirmative debaters communicate their justification for their affirmation.

A brief story might help illuminate our discussion: The call of god to Abraham to sacrifice his son Isaac presents Abraham with the conflict of his particular love of his son and the love of his God. This is the problem of sacrifice, of decision, of having to choose. Every choice to respond to a particular other is the decision to not to respond to every other other. The world is finite, or choices constitute who we are, but how can we act, know if our actions are correct—how are we supposed to be ethical in a world that is indeterminate and detached from a telos? To choose to not to choose, that is a choice as well: to ignore the call of the other entirely.
Caputo, David R. Cook Prof. of Philo. @ Villanova U., 1997
(John D., The Prayers and Tears of Jacques Derrida, pg. 203-5)
The alterity of every other, of friends or … others, the faces of whom we see daily on the evening news.

Still, responsibility is fickle, it would be easy to just pick an ethical frame: to defend a calculation, to rationalize a decision to respond—to claim responsibility for the consequences of our actions based on a code, religion, morality and fake it. We could choose to respond like Agamemnon—who responds to the call of his brother: for revenge against Paris with constant justification. Stuck without wind, unable to help his brother Menalauous—Agamemnon couldn’t leave for Troy. In order to set sail and gain favorable winds he offered his daughter, Iphigenia, up as a sacrifice. When the ships finally sailed to Troy it was assumed that the gods were appeased. But this economy of sacrifice doesn’t end here. When Agamemnon returned home, his wife Clymestra and her lover Aegisthus killed him to avenge Iphigea. Later, Agamemnon’s son killed his mother and her lover in order to avenge his father. Perhaps this all started with a boast, hubris, the killing of one of Artemis’s deer, or the rape of Helen, but the economy of sacrifice is unending as long as the sacrifice is able to be justified and moralized. Agamemnon’s actions serve as a way to moralize morality- that in order to fulfill his ethical duty to his brother a sacrifice is needed and is justified by the overall good. It is this unproblematized idea of morality that facilitates war.
Tracy Strong Prof. of PoliSci @ Harvard, explains in 2003.
[Tracy, “Nietzsche and Legal Theory (Part 1): Where are We when We are Beyond Good and Evil,” Cardozo Law Review 24:2, January)

The problem is that we aren’t given a rulebook to life, aren’t given a telos to chain our existence to. One day we will be gone and it will be over before we realize we ever lived. We have several choices as affirmative debaters: we can respond to the call of the resolution by not debating, by not attending to anyone impacted by transportation infrastructure, we could act like Agamemnon, or we could act line Abraham. We choose father Abraham. While Many Sons had father Abraham, father Abraham also attempted to kill his son Isaac, but his decision to turn his back on his community and secretly sacrifice his son to god without question, transgresses the narcissism of the sacrificial economy of calculation that is ethics. Unlike Agamemnon though, there is no justification—just the aporia of responsibility. Every decision to act in the name of a universal ethic entails the sacrifice of all those not within the world view of that ethic or the reduction of the singularity of an other to a narration that fits with a simplistic understanding of their particular situatedness, this effacement is the erasure of that which makes us human—our absolute singularity. The only response to the particular other is the gift of death—of irresponsibility—and this isn’t easy—but the sacrifice of ethics: of an action absent of all justification for the sake of responsibility to the singularity of the absolute other—is the only option.

Our argument isn’t for inaction: quite the opposite, our argument is a call for an ethical aporia—of an instantaneous affirmation of action that radically accepts the responsibility of our actions—that accepts the intended and unintended consequences of our actions: it is a genuine decision absent any justification—the only decision.
