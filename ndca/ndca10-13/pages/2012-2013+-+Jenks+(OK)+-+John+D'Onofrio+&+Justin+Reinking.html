Camus Aff

PART ONE IS THE ROAD
Pre-Socratic philosophers amused themselves with the essence of existence. Thales believed that water made up everything. Anaximander believed that from nothing and the undefined there sprung qualities and opposites; that out of chaos springs order. Heraclitus thought the world was change and flux—the universe danced with the fire of the logos. This matter, this unifying principle to explain everything has no explanation for otherness, for the World that moves day after day, haunting philosophers with the unknown. Western philosophy has been the procession of a search for a single great answer, a cosmological principle to discern actual reality. Albert Camus explains this desire for knowledge writing that
Camus 1957 (Albert, French writer & nobel prize winner in literature, The Myth of Sisyphus and Other Essays, pg. 19-22[DR])
Of whom and of what indeed ... to know beforehand if thought can live in those deserts.


PART TWO IS THE TRAVELER
“I woke up as the sun was reddening; and that was the one distinct time in my life, the strangest moment of all, when I didn't know who I was — I was far away from home, haunted and tired with travel, in a cheap hotel room I'd never seen, hearing the hiss of steam outside, and the creak of the old wood of the hotel, and footsteps upstairs, and all the sad sounds, and I looked at the cracked high ceiling and really didn't know who I was for about fifteen strange seconds. I wasn't scared; I was just somebody else, some stranger, and my whole life was a haunted life, the life of a ghost. I was halfway across America, at the dividing line between the East of my youth and the West of my future, and maybe that's why it happened right there and then, that strange red afternoon.”
The open road presents us with nothingness, the vast expanse of existence. This confrontation with the utter meaninglessness of existence will happen at some point. Presented with this thought nothing else matters. When thinking about our lot, of being bound to the endless roads and passageways it is important to remember that:
He who fights with monsters should be careful lest he thereby become a monster. And if thou gaze long into an abyss, the abyss will also gaze into thee.[1]
Camus elaborates that
Camus 1957 (Albert, French writer & nobel prize winner in literature, The Myth of Sisyphus and Other Essays, pg. 12-16 [DR])
All great deeds and all great thoughts ...rapid inventory on the plane of the intelligence.

In On The Road, Kerouacs protagonist tells us an interesting bit of wisdom about the road”

What is that feeling when you're driving away from people and they recede on the plain till you see their specks dispersing? — it's the too-huge world vaulting us, and it's good-by. But we lean forward to the next crazy venture beneath the skies.


Sitting in the drivers seat of a 72’ Impala gunning it across California—its hard not to know that we are all travelers, each at our own intersection of different life highways. This thought—pondering the open road, the vast expanse of life and our journeys reveals an interesting question. Why life? Why this place? Why even exist? Why not just fade into the void and snuff it all out with a quick jog down the railroad tracks.

Camus elucidates that
Camus 1957 (Albert, French writer & nobel prize winner in literature, The Myth of Sisyphus and Other Essays, pg. 2-5 [AL])
There is but one truly serious philosophical problem,... daily agitation, and the uselessness of suffering.  

Drowning, gas leaks, immolation, broken hearts, bludgeoning, cancer, the flu… In trash cans, rivers, lakes, urns, and below ground the world is littered with the dead—but we keep this thought away—banished within a hope—we live as though we will never die… and this is our greatest lie—at any second we may leave this place, where we go isn’t important, just that we leave, and we leave behind everything that we ever thought was important. Camus explains that
Camus 1957 (Albert, French writer & nobel prize winner in literature, The Myth of Sisyphus and Other Essays, pg. 58-60 [AL])
The absurd enlightens me on this point... evidence of a life without consolation.

PART THREE IS THE ABSURD HERO
From 1972 to 1973 seven hitchhikers were kidnapped and brutally mamed and murdered near Santa Rosa, California. The American highway holds stories of terror and death, from Matthew Shepard to the Sunshine Skyway bridge collapse, traversing the open road is the constant reminder of the danger of the road, of the finitude, ambiguity and chaos of life. Far from being a call to hedonism and safety, this is a challenge, an opportunity. A devilish Fiend explains in the prologue to Thus spoke Zarathustra:
Nietzsche 1885 (Friedrich, Sponge Brain and supposed Nazi, Thus Spoke Zarathustra :A book for All and None [DR])
When Zarathustra had spoken these words, ... they laugh they hate me too. There is ice in their laughter."

And so, when confronted with this year’s resolution John and I choose to challenge this tragic paradigm. John and I affirm:
Resolved: The United States federal government should substantially increase its transportation infrastructure investment in the United States because the road is vast. It is through exploring and travel of the road that we understand our existential plight—that we are insignificant beings in the world. This turn to the road, to open ourselves to the vastness of the world. is an encounter the absurdity and capriciousness of everyday life. When one stares into the abyss the abyss stares back into them and one realizes the absurdity of existence. It is in this moment of lucid consciousness that we realize all we have is our lives, our revolt, and our passion
“There is no sun without shadow, and it is essential to know the night. The absurd man says yes and his efforts will henceforth be unceasing. This universe henceforth without a master seems to him neither sterile nor futile. Each atom of that stone, each mineral flake of that night filled mountain, in itself forms a world. The struggle itself toward the heights is enough to fill a man's heart.”[2]

As Sal tells us :
And for just a moment I had reached the point of ecstasy that I always wanted to reach, which was the complete step across chronological time into timeless shadows, and wonderment in the bleakness of the mortal realm, and the sensation of death kicking at my heels to move on, with a phantom dogging its own heels, and myself hurrying to a plank where all the angels dove off and flew into the holy void of uncreated emptiness, the potent and inconceivable radiancies shining in bright Mind Essence, innumerable lotus-lands falling open in the magic mothswarm of heaven. I could hear an indescribable seething roar which wasn't in my ear but everywhere and had nothing to do with sounds. I realized that I had died and been reborn numberless times but just didn't remember because the transitions from life to death and back are so ghostly easy, a magical action for naught, like falling asleep and waking up again a million times, the utter casualness and deep ignorance of it

It’s an absurd question why? Why the road? Why the open highway, why go out there, why adventure outside at all? Why did someone ever choose to eat a raw oyster? I’ll tell you why: bravery. To do things not because they are easy—but to dare to accomplish something because it is difficult—is to turn our tragedy into triumph. Bob Lane describes our existential plight
Lane 96 (Bob. Classics, Philosophy, and Religious Studies/ Institute of Practical Philosophy Malaspina University-College “The absurd Hero,” [DR])
Sisyphus is the absurd hero.... (p. 87) One could do worse than to consider the myths-retold in the works of Camus.
