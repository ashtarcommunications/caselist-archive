CASE 4


Trigger warning: Discourse is my gun and I’m about to pull the trigger.


First is the narrative


I have been a victim of sexual harassment during my time in debate. I’m not going to tell you that story; I’m not going to make you sit through my pain. However, I will use the debate space to try to create some positive change. Our LD community isn’t perfect – it’s not even close – and it’s in trouble. Inspired by the resolution, I seek to rehabilitate the debate community.

Starting at Columbia, I have been reading a micropolitical position regarding sexism and sexual harassment in the debate community. In the original case, I said “I must occupy. I am but one face of the anonymous movement, and our struggle is against an anonymous oppression.” I am a woman. I am a lone wolf. I am a minority in debate. Therefore I am anonymous. I am invisible. We are all invisible until we make noise, until we disturb the peace. I disturbed the peace at Columbia and started a community-wide discussion, until I was relegated back into the shroud of anonymity.


Aside from constant heckling and derision from judges, coaches, and my fellow debaters, there have also been several attempts to silence me. Tournament staff have detained me and told me to channel my angst elsewhere. One coach took the rather extreme measures of contacting tournament staff, my coach, and even my school administration in an effort to have me punished or excluded. I’ve had several judges refuse to flow my case and give me L24’s because they thought my non-topical discourse was inappropriate. And in every single debate round I’ve had on my micropolitical postion, my opponents have never failed to tell me that my arguments don’t belong in the round.


I know that micropolitical positions are not the norm in debate. I know that my non-topical discourse goes against the imaginary rules that the privileged, white males of this game created. But I never agreed to comply with the debate community’s rules or wishes for me to stop. Like the martyrs of the Occupy movement, I am rebelling. This is my “movement’s performative resistance to a system that refuses to reward action.”


And, narratives are important for critically examining our activity. No ivory tower philosopher or debate theorist can tell us what’s best for us.

Matsuda 87

“Looking to the Bottom” 1987. [Mari Matsuda is a prof at William S. Richardson Law School, former prof at Georgetown Law Center. Activist and lawyer specializing in feminist theory and CRT.]

[T]hose who have experienced discrimination speak with a special voice to which we should listen …The imagination of the academic philosopher cannot recreate the experience of life on the bottom.Instead we must look to ...‘organic intellectuals,’ grass roots philosophers who are uniquely able to relate theory to the concrete experience of oppression.The technique of imagining oneself black and poor in some hypothetical world is less effective than studying the actual experience of black poverty and listening to those who have done so. When notions of right and wrong, justice and injustice are examined not from an abstract position but from the position of groups who have suffered through history… identifiable normative priorities emerge[,]. … Looking to the bottom for ideas about law will tap a valuable source previously overlooked by…philosophers.”


Second is the R.O.B. – HOW TO PULL THE TRIGGER


Debaters are constantly fighting for recognition. The ballot is a tool of reward, punishment, and ultimately power. The object of this game is to win ballots. Ballots and bids are the currency of visibility in the contemporary debate culture. In order to “speak” I have to become visible as an individual. The name on the ballot is a voice, visibility, endorsement by authority and backing by normative force.


The judge is in a position of privilege by virtue of being the judge but also because most judges are white males. They decide who’s experience deserves to be reproduced. When the judge signs the ballot for the debater, they are giving them power, a voice, a home. They’re the director who chooses callback, giving the oppressed a weapon to fight back, and a megaphone to yell Truth at a raucous crowd.


To clarify, the role of the ballot is not about arbitrarily punishing people. The ballot with my name on it is uniquely important - the experience of marginalized voices cannot be replicated by others since you will not find better evidence about discrimination in debate than you will from a lone wolf and woman. Also, if I’m winning that topical debate masks the real issues in the debate community, then it’s not arbitrary to vote against the person in favor of topical debate and silence.


Here are my justifications:


First, critiques of the context of debate - of debate norms, topical debate, and of debate practices comes logically prior to any argument substantive or theoretical. These are functionally incomparable arguments; mine is about real effects of speech acts and conduct.

How,for example,could a judge weigh a sexist-language critique againsttheclaimed risk ofnuclear warsolvedbyaffirmative'shypothetical policy? For others, it is to share and spread also those words that are meaningful to us. But primarily for us all, it is necessary to teach by living and speaking those truths which we believe and know beyond understanding. Because in this way alone can we survive,by taking part in a process of life that is creative and continuing, that is growth. And it is never without fear — of visibility, of the harsh light of scrutiny and perhaps judgment, of pain, of death. But we have lived through all of those already, in silence, except death. And I remind myself all the time now that if I were to have been born mute, or had maintained an oath of silence my whole life long for safety, I would still have suffered, and I would still die. It is very good for establishing perspective. We can learn to work and speak when we are afraid in the same way we have learned to work and speak when we are tired. And there are so many silences to be broken.

Second is that opening up the debate space for critique allows for more kinds of oppositional voices to be heard, Butler 06**[1]**:

If the humanities has a future as cultural criticism, and cultural criticism has a task at the present moment,it is no doubt to return us to the human where we do not expect to find it, in its frailty and at the limits of its capacity to make sense. The WIN on the ballot signifies a willingness to critique the community when necessary to solve all kinds of impacts in the future.


It’s empirically proven that debate tactics adapt to what wins. At last year’s TOC, at least half of the contestants from quarters on were well-known for running util and policy arguments. This year, there’s been a much bigger influence on that style. A vote for me might not directly cause more debaters to run micropolitical arguments, but it will enable debaters in the future who want to and need to.


Third is the IMPACTS, TRIGGERED

Third is alt solvency: I’ve come a long way from Columbia. It’s not as if my efforts have gone to waste.

1. I’m calling out people for the wrongs they’ve committed in debate, which creates a deterrent effect. More silence means more harassment, and my alt is the antithesis of silence.

2. Since I started running this position at Columbia, I’ve jump-started a culture against sexual violence in debate, including a pledge, several threads on debate websites, Facebook groups, etc.

3. I’m still raising awareness to members of the debate community that don’t know that these problems even exist.

4. I’ve created real-world change since I know for a FACT that debate camps and teams are revising their policies in regards to issues of sexism and sexual harassment.

5. Speaking out gives power to the oppressed. I’m breaking the silence which oppresses the voiceless and the invisible. and I’m opening the door for more discourse.

CASE 3


Trigger warning - I'm about to pull the trigger.

Starting at Columbia, I have been reading a micropolitical position regarding sexism and sexual harassment in the debate community. Aside from constant heckling and derision from judges, coaches, and my fellow debaters, there have also been several attempts to silence me. Tournament staff have detained me and told me to channel my angst elsewhere. And in every single debate round I’ve had on my micropolitical position, my opponents have never failed to tell me that my arguments don’t belong in the round.


Since I started to use my speech times to advocate a real-life policy change, I have become a better debater. I have finally begun to understand the blood, sweat, and tears that true advocacy skills require. This is infinitely better than reading evidence about hypothetical extinction scenarios and advocating for policy changes that aren’t even remotely relevant to real life and expire after the round ends. Ultimately, I’ve become a better advocate for myself and my community and a force for actual change by advocating for the addressing of real-life issues in debate rounds. The only way to make anyone a better debater is to make debate confront its own real issues before it tries to confront hypothetical ones.


First, THE ROLE OF THE BALLOT – HOW TO PULL THE TRIGGER

I argue that the role of the ballot is to vote for the better debater. However, I defend the following interpretation of what it means to do the better debating, which is an absolute constraint on all sufficient access to the ballot.


1. The ballot is an educational tool - rounds are THE defining educational event, and ballots are artifacts from rounds/are a product of rounds. Education is the ultimate aim of debate. A founder of modern forensics,



Walter Alan Ulrich 86:

Walter Alan Ulrich. The Ethical Obligations of the Forensic Educator. Contemporary Argumentation & Debate Vol 7. 1986. p. 81

Widespread transfer for purely competitive reasons also fosters an unhealthy view of competition. While some amount of competition is inevitable in academic forensics, the over-emphasis on competition undermines the educational benefits of the activity if it is not kept in perspective. Competition is a means toward an end – increased skills as a public speaker and/or a public advocate.


And, the ballot necessarily educates - it transmits/carries information. Fairness matters, but is instrumental to larger social and educational goals. Public advocacy and actual change are the most important. The impact to better debating is that the better debating will be done by the person who is a better advocate. Competitive skill is outweighed by advocacy skills.


2. Critiques of the context of debate - of debate norms, topical debate, and of debate practices comes logically prior to any argument substantive or theoretical that is premised on the topic. These are functionally incomparable arguments; my argument is about real effects of speech acts and conduct. The best sequencing evidence you’ve heard all year.

3. The ballot is a tool of reward, punishment, and ultimately power. Ballots and bids are the currency of visibility in the contemporary debate culture. In order to “speak” I have to become visible as an individual. The name on the ballot is a voice, visibility, endorsement by authority and backing by normative force. This next argument is a pre-empt to arbitrary punishment arguments: the ballot is a zero sum game, losses are inevitable. If I’m winning that topical debate masks the real issues in the debate community, then it’s not arbitrary to vote against the person in favor of topical debate and masking. More importantly, the ballot with my name on it is uniquely important - the experience of marginalized voices cannot be replicated by others.

Guerrilla communication is political, militant action (politische Militanz). It differs from the conventional (autonomous) militant action in as much asit does not simply aim to destroy the codes of power and sovereignty, but aims to effectively disfigure them. Guerrilla communication is a particular form of political communication which does not just transport statements in plain text(like flyers, demo slogans, posters).The goal is neither the interruption nor the permanent appropriation of a communicational channel. The goal [of guerrilla communication] is the theft and distortion of messages. We start from the premise that the significance of communication is not just what is said but also the form of the communication itself. It is the aim of guerrilla communication to de-legitimate the present state of power and sovereignty which by now seems so normal and unalterable. In order to do that, one has to understand its multifaceted structures and manifestations. Guerrilla communication tries to deal with the normative power structures of the established forms of communication as well as the internalised power structures at the level of subject. This requires that situations be manufactured in which it becomes perceptible - for at least a brief moment - that everything could actually be quite different.
