Nietzsche K

We read a slightly different 1NC pretty much every round depending on the aff, but here's an example of a 1NC:

The first link is pity:
The aff acts out of a lust for power. The other is just another possession to acquire. This
1. forces the creation of more suffering others, turning case, and
2. makes us hate everything else in the world, which is the root cause of violence.
Nietzsche 1882 (The Gay Science, Kaufmann translation)
"The things people call love...name is friendship"

The second link is ressentiment:
The aff attempts to bring Apollonian order to a Dionysian world by eliminating all that is foreign and unknown so that it conforms to the rational. Their mindset is the cause of all wars and security discourse.
Huysmans 98
(Huysmans, Jef, Lecturer in Government and Politics at the University of Kent, “Security! What do You Mean?”, The European Journal of International Relations, vol. 4 no. 2, 1998, p. 235-6)
"The threat construction...relation to death"

The third link is the morality of the herd:
Morality defines value to life in terms of the individual's utility to the herd, which justifies the violent extermination of those who subscribe to other moralities. This makes violence inevitable
Nietzsche 1882 (The Gay Science, Kaufmann translation)
"Herd Instinct...in the individual"

The alternative is to reject the aff's call to action and embrace the world as-is. This allows us to find meaning within and escape the cycle of violence
Nietzsche 1882 (The Gay Science, Kaufmann translation)
"One thing is needful...bad and gloomy"

Finally, the alt comes first.
Their rush to act before considering ontological questions ensures the errors that led to their impact scenarios will be repeated, making them inevitable
Nietzsche 1882 (The Gay Science, Kaufmann translation)
"Consciousness. - Consciousness is...relates to errors!"

Nuclearism K

1. No rational actor would ever enter into a nuclear war because of mutually assured destruction.
G John Ikenberry Albert G. Milbank Professor of Politics and International Affairs at Princeton University “The Rise of China and the Future of the West” Foreign Affairs January/February 2008 http://www.foreignaffairs.org/20080101faessay87102/g-john-ikenberry/the-rise-of-china-and-the-future-of-the-west.html
"The most important benefit...as a historical process."

2. So why are we still worried? Because we're not dealing with rational actors. If war ever happened, their planning scenarios would fall apart. We don't know what would happen, and there's not much we could do about it anyway.
Chaloupka, 1992 (William, associate professor of political science at the University of Montana, Knowing Nukes, p. 14-16)
"Klein and Warner...most obvious points."

3. The nuke is the end point of a process of dehumanizing war. We have transferred control from humans to things humans created, and we depend on these things remaining in our control.
Chaloupka, 1992 (William, associate professor of political science at the University of Montana, Knowing Nukes, p. 27-28)
"However thoroughly Virilio...with functioning parts."

4.This projection of power onto the “thinking missle” makes it incredibly potent symbolically. Their prohibition against nuclear war sets it up as an object of desire. Because we are told we cannot have nuclear war, we infer that this means we would have wanted it if there were no prohibition. This is a self-replicating cycle.
Chaloupka, 1992 (William, associate professor of political science at the University of Montana, Knowing Nukes, p. 60-62)
"This is my claim...years: 'Nuke 'em!'"

5. But we don't have control anymore. Liberalism reacts to this disappearance of predictability by trying to recover it, and this leads to an obsession with controlling the bomb. Detonating the bomb is the ultimate exercise of such control.
Chaloupka, 1992 (William, associate professor of political science at the University of Montana, Knowing Nukes, p. 64-66)
"The robot - this symbolic...arrange for ourselves."

6. The alternative is to reject the rhetorical constructs of the 1AC in order to deprive the bomb of all meaning and even create our own meaning for it. Voting negative challenges the way the bomb defines the status quo culture of fear, using the power of the bomb against itself.
Chaloupka, 1992 (William, associate professor of political science at the University of Montana, Knowing Nukes, p. 137-138)
"In Stanley Kubrick's...cyborg - at last."

7. Their framework is too late: Students view appeals to democracy, role-playing, and preparing us to be policymakers as a sucker's game, and they've already withdrawn. Only the alt's playful approach can respond to this mass inertness, which will lead to political change.
Chaloupka, 1992 (William, associate professor of political science at the University of Montana, Knowing Nukes, p. 90-92)
"As Nietzsche well knew...serious substitute lifestyles"
