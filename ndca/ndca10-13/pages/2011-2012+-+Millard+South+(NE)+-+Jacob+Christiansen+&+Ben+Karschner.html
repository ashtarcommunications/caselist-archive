The Desert 1AC

We begin with a place seemingly unrelated to the vacuum of space, yet oddly similar in its vast emptiness. The distinctly barren terrain of the desert places a unique ontological thought in the discussion of space policy.
Beck,John, 1963“Without Form and Void: The American Desert as Trope and Terrain” Nepantla: Views from South - Volume 2, Issue 1, 2001, pp. 63-83

We stand in the interregnum, or more specifically the desert, of the resolution and peer over the edge of the topical abyss. William Spanos writes that this abyss is “a realm of in-between—the No more of the gods that have fled and the Not-yet of the god that is coming.” It is in the status-quo development of this abyss that we find metaphysics. Current modes of thought view space, and the desert, in one of two roles: either productive and bountiful or unproductive and useless. Spanos defines Metaphysics as:

Beck, John, 1963 “Without Form and Void: The American Desert as Trope and Terrain” Nepantla: Views from South - Volume 2, Issue 1, 2001, pp. 63-83
Something about... ... is this ‘nothing’ ” (Heidegger 1949, 335–36).

Space, so similar to the desert, has become the nothing of metaphysics. It is viewed through the trope of the empty wasteland, the vast unproductive nothingness. This nothing is difference, the in-between, the inhabited space of the bar that is excluded from policy discourse. The response that status-quo space policy has to the nothing is informed by one of two metaphysical paths: either accommodate the nothing or banish it. Metaphysical thought assumes that this nothing must be converted to something; the wild, wasteful desert must be cultivated into a productive field, and the vast nothingness of space must be transformed into a site of productive existence.
Spanos 2K(William V., English and Comparative Literature Prof @ Binghamton, Heideggerian scholar, America’s Shadow: An Anatomy of Empire, pg. 11-12 |MB|)

We return to John Beck as he notes how the attempt to accommodate the desert alters its identity to that of a site for instrumentality:
Beck, John, 1963 “Without Form and Void: The American Desert as Trope and Terrain” Nepantla: Views from South - Volume 2, Issue 1, 2001, pp. 63-83
Under the sign of vacancy... ...precariously over the abyss of noplace” (186).

Instead of attempting to develop and define that which exists beyond definition, we embrace the unknowability of both space and its terrestrial counterpart—the desert—the same way we affirm the resolution. We affirm the topical “nothing” which is not opposed to, but part of
Resolved: The United States Federal Government should substantially increase its exploration and/or development of space beyond the Earth's mesosphere.
The Marzec evidence also identifies the problem of approaching the world through certain frameworks. The desire to enclose discussion and make it manageable continues imperialism. It limits the questions we ask and the answers we can arrive at. These frameworks must accommodate discussion and inquiry or banish them, much the same way American policy seeks to accommodate or banish cultures outside of itself. More specifically, Beck notes that the Desert, the site of the nothing, is threatening to the “civilized world” because of its nothingness. The response we offer to the imperialist framework of the debate community is to infuse ontology into a concept of the problematic. We affirm the nothing which is not external to but is produced by, inside of, and belongs to the topic. It is the nonvision of vision. We refuse to respond to our anxiety with an attempt to presence the resolution by breaking it down, staking out its boundaries in a way that treats beings as manageable and exploitable. We also refuse to abide by debate mandates that require us to trade our unique agency as youth and students now for some imagined future world as policy-makers. As Henry Giroux argues, we have unique power as students:
(Henry A. Giroux, "Channel surfing: racism, the media and the destruction of today's youth." 1998)

However, instead of attempting to find differences between certain branches of thought, we need to find similarities that allow for projects to resist disciplinary compartmentalization which ensures failure. Our advocation of ontology inserts a liberating question to the problematic, allowing for true reformation.
