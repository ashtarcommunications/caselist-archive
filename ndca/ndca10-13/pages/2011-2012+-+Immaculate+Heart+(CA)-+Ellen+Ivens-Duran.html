Affirmative

Round 1:
AC

I affirm.
The resolution uses the word “individuals” without a modifier, so we presume that is refers to some, not necessarily all, individuals. Prefer this interpretation if it is reasonable to combat time skew.

Observation 1. An individual is defined as “a single human being distinct from a group, class, or family”.
Observation 2. Morally obligatory and morally supererogatory acts are separated by the harm done to the actor.

John Rawls [James Bryant Conant University Professorship at Harvard University], A Theory of Justice (Revised Edition), Cambridge: The Belknap Press of the Harvard University Press (1971),
p. 100-101

I shall say .... open to us.



Thus, the affirmative defends an interpretation of the resolution that sees moral obligations as good acts that do not cause undue or excessive harm to the actor.

The value is morality. This is the most textual value as the resolution demands an answer to moral questions. Morality refers to a code of conduct that would be put forward by all rational persons.

The value criterion, then, is consistency with universalizable principles. This is a preferable criterion for 5 reasons:
1. Universal maxims are the only reasonable expectations for moral behavior as they apply across the board.
2. Morality is founded on the premise of inherent equality. Universal expectations are the only moral rules that aren’t nonsensical in this context.
3. Creating exceptions to moral rules can quickly lead to the erosion of morality or its corollaries (rights, respect, etc.)
4. Only universal maxims can be prescriptive as only they give a specific requirement for moral actions to be deemed acceptable.
5. Inconsistency with morally sound universal principles is automatically immoral and provides clarity in an otherwise muddy debate.
If a principle is not morally universalizable, then it is not a moral rule. Principles and customs such as voting are political social, or democratic principles, no moral ones.

I contend, then, that assisting people in need is the only possible maxim to will and the only universalizable principle in this debate.
Affirming is not only the intuitive answer, but is syllogistically proven to be logical.

Peter Singer (Prof of Bioethics, Princeton) The Life You Can Safe: Acting Now to End World Hunger2009

Another example devised ......(even a high cost) to ourselves.



It is an individual’s moral obligation to redress wrongs when the cost of doing so is less than the benefit gained. Because people in need die and suffer from preventable conditions and circumstances, individuals have an obligation motivated by sympathy and confirmed by morality to assist them.

The categorical, as conceived by Kant, necessitates the concept of obligation to those in need. The alternatives are both irrational and dangerous.

Pablo Gilabert [Concordia University] “Kant and the Claims of the Poor” Philosophy and Phenomenological Research Vol. LXXXI No. 2, September2010
The first formulation .....toward each other.

Society as a whole benefits from an agreed upon principle of mutual obligation to the needy.

Even individualists benefit from a society which values helping those in need. Since ration people are the only ones who can will the maxim, they are the only ones the resolution is concerning itself with.

Morality, which operates under the assumption that all rational people able to will a maxim, are equal requires affirming because it is the only way to attempt to restore moral and actual equality.

Round 2:
The affirmative must defend the entirety of the resolution. Artificially limiting of parametricizing the resolution destroys debate.
Value: Morality, defined as a particular system of values and principles of conduct.

Value Criterion: Imposing Reasonable Obligations

1. When morality becomes arbitrary it becomes meaningless. The affirmative mistakenly mislabels a morally permissible and supererogatory action as morally obligatory. Conflating these two categories makes morality meaningless.
2. Morality is meant to guide action. Without well-reasoned moral system, morality would become so relative as to be unbinding and useless.

Contention 1.

Affirming is unreasonable because it is a blanket statement. Hold the affirmative to the resolution, as the resolution is the only thing both debaters have before the round. Necessitating topicality and textuality enhances predictability and depth of arguments as it dissuades theory. This encourages fairness, a necessity in a competitive activity, because it forces debaters to be substantive.

Even if helping those in need is a moral good, it ought not be an obligation.
