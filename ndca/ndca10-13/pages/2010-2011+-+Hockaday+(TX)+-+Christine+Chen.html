Affirmative

AFF – DEONT 1
My value is morality.
Morality is based on the notion that humans have intrinsic worth necessary to protect. Kamm, Frances, 2007 [Philosophy Professor at Harvard]. Intricate Ethics: Rights, Responsibilities, and Permissible Harm. Pg. 28. “Suppose that the…in this way.”
Using people as a means is an impersonal bad. Isak Applebaum [Philosopher] Ethics for Adversaries: The Morality of Roles is Public and Professional Life. Princeton, NJ: Princeton University Press (2000) pg 139. “If one wishes…is done to us.”

Further, morality can be assessed in terms of intention. Gerald Dworkin [Professor of moral, political and legal philosophy. He is currently Distinguished Professor of Philosophy at the University of California, Davis. He has also taught at Harvard, MIT, and the University of Illinois, Chicago.] Nuclear Intentions. Ethics Vol. 95, No. 3, Special Issue: Symposium on Ethics and Nuclear Deterrence (Apr., 1985), pp. 445-460 Published by: The University of Chicago Press, http://www.jstor.org/stable/2381031, pg. 447. “Future intentions are…of those intentions.”

If the achievement of that goal is contingent on a violation of rights, it is especially immoral. Gerald Dworkin [Professor of moral, political and legal philosophy. He is currently Distinguished Professor of Philosophy at the University of California, Davis. He has also taught at Harvard, MIT, and the University of Illinois, Chicago.] Nuclear Intentions. Ethics Vol. 95, No. 3, Special Issue: Symposium on Ethics and Nuclear Deterrence (Apr., 1985), pp. 445-460 Published by: The University of Chicago Press, http://www.jstor.org/stable/2381031, pg. 460. “Finally, and this…what we do.”
Morality restrains actions even for supposed good ends. Gerald Dworkin [Professor of moral, political and legal philosophy. He is currently Distinguished Professor of Philosophy at the University of California, Davis. He has also taught at Harvard, MIT, and the University of Illinois, Chicago.] Nuclear Intentions. Ethics Vol. 95, No. 3, Special Issue: Symposium on Ethics and Nuclear Deterrence (Apr., 1985), pp. 445-460 Published by: The University of Chicago Press, http://www.jstor.org/stable/2381031, pg. 460. “In the case of…achieve important values.”
The standard is preventing the violation of innocents.

Self-defense cannot involve harm to innocents. Jeffrie G. Murphy [Regents' Professor of Law & Philosophy at the Arizona State University College of Law] The Monist, Vol. 57, No. 4 (1973). http://www.ditext.com/murphy/innocent.html. “What I suggest...to kill me.”

Thesis: possession of nukes intends violation of innocents.

Contention 1: Nuclear deterrence holds innocents hostage.

Nuclear deterrence is a social institution that necessitates non-consequentialist violations to be moral. Steven Lee 1985 [Teaches philosophy at Hobart and William Smith Colleges. He writes on issues in social, moral, and political philosophy, especially on matters of morality and war] Morality of Nuclear Deterrence: Hostage Holding and Consequences. Ethics Vol. 95, No. 3, Special Issue: Symposium on Ethics and Nuclear Deterrence. Published by: The University of Chicago Press. http://www.jstor.org/stable/pdfplus/2381037.pdf. pg. 550-551. “But nuclear deterrence…of social institutions.”

Nuclear deterrence holds hostages. Steven Lee 1996 [Teaches philosophy at Hobart and William Smith Colleges. He writes on issues in social, moral, and political philosophy, especially on matters of morality and war] Morality, Prudence, and Nuclear Weapons. Cambridge University Press. Pg. 47-48. “Nuclear deterrence is…to these persons.”

Deprivation of liberty is not necessary for hostage holding. Steven Lee 1996 [Teaches philosophy at Hobart and William Smith Colleges. He writes on issues in social, moral, and political philosophy, especially on matters of morality and war] Morality, Prudence, and Nuclear Weapons. Cambridge University Press. Pg. 48-49. “Second, the argument…also holds hostages.”
This imposes a substantial risk which is indiscriminate. Gerald Dworkin [Professor of moral, political and legal philosophy. He is currently Distinguished Professor of Philosophy at the University of California, Davis. He has also taught at Harvard, MIT, and the University of Illinois, Chicago.] Nuclear Intentions. Ethics Vol. 95, No. 3, Special Issue: Symposium on Ethics and Nuclear Deterrence (Apr., 1985). The University of Chicago Press, http://www.jstor.org/stable/2381031. “Under the traditional...of the distinction.”

This results in innocent massacre. Jaya Tiwari 2002 [Senior Policy Analyst for Physicians for Social Responsibility’s Security Program. Involved in research, analysis, and writing about international security, nuclear weapons proliferation, arms control and nuclear disarmament, and effects of nuclear war]. Ira Helfand [MD, Specialty in Emergency Medicine, North American Vice-President, International Physicians for the Prevention of Nuclear War], Lachlan Forrow [MD, Associate Professor of Medicine at Harvard Medical School (HMS). Faculty Associate at the Harvard University Program in Ethics and the Professions having primary responsibility providing medical ethics curricula at HMS. He is Director of the Ethics Support Service within BIDMC, and chairs the BIDMC Ethics Advisory Committee.], http://people.reed.edu/~ahm/Courses/Stan-PS-314-2009-Q1_PNP/Syllabus/EReadings/HelfandForrow2002Nuclear.pdf, pg. 2. “Using the CATS…extensive radioactive contamination.”
Contention 2: Nuclear testing results in innocent violation.
Nuclear testing is necessary.

Donald MacKenzie [Professor of Sociology at the University of Edinburgh, Scotland] and Graham Spinardi 1995. “Tacit Knowledge, Weapons Design, and the Uninvention of Nuclear Weapons.” American Journal of Science, Volume 101 Number 1. http://people.reed.edu/~ahm/Courses/Stan-PS-314-2009-Q1_PNP/Syllabus/EReadings/MacKenzieSpinardi1995Tacit.pdf, 47. “As we shall…view with trepidation.”
Medalia, Jonathan 2005 [Specialist in National Defense, Foreign Affairs, Defense, and Trade Division.] “Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty: Pro and Con”. Congressional Research Service Report for Congress. Pg 3.

Testing results in innocent massacre.

Mary Dickson [an award-winning writer who has written about the consequences of nuclear testing and worked on issues of peace and justice for more than 25 years.] http://www.kued.org/productions/skullvalley/resources/past.html “Utahns know first-hand…isn’t a downwinder.”

AC DEONT 2

That inherent worth demands we do not submit to a utilitarianism of rights. Kamm, Frances, 2007 [Philosophy Professor at Harvard]. Intricate Ethics: Rights, Responsibilities, and Permissible Harm. Pg. 28. “Suppose that the…in this way.”
Using people as a means is an impersonal bad. Isak Applebaum [Philosopher] Ethics for Adversaries: The Morality of Roles is Public and Professional Life. Princeton, NJ: Princeton University Press (2000) pg 139. “If one wishes…is done to us.”
Self defense cannot involve the violation of innocents. David Rodin [David Rodin is Director of Research at the Uehiro Centre for Practical Ethics at Oxford University. A former Rhodes Scholar from New Zealand, he holds a B.Phil. and doctorate in philosophy from Oxford University]. “War and Self Defense.” Ethics & International Affairs 18 no. 1 (Winter, 2004). http://www.carnegiecouncil.org/resources/journal/18_1/symposium/1111.html.

The standard is treating people as ends.

B. Commits to retaliation.
Intent can be morally evaluated. Gerald Dworkin [Professor of moral, political and legal philosophy. He is currently Distinguished Professor of Philosophy at the University of California, Davis. Ethics Vol. 95, No. 3, Special Issue: Symposium on Ethics and Nuclear Deterrence (Apr., 1985), pp. 445-460 Published by: The University of Chicago Press, http://www.jstor.org/stable/2381031, pg. 447. “Future intentions are…of those intentions.”

Commitment to retaliation is necessary to gain any benefit. Gerald Dworkin [Professor of moral, political and legal philosophy. He is currently Distinguished Professor of Philosophy at the University of California, Davis. He has also taught at Harvard, MIT, and the University of Illinois, Chicago.] Nuclear Intentions. Ethics Vol. 95, No. 3, Special Issue: Symposium on Ethics and Nuclear Deterrence (Apr., 1985), pp. 445-460 Published by: The University of Chicago Press, http://www.jstor.org/stable/2381031

NC GOV OBLIGATION 1
The value is governmental legitimacy.
Kennan defines the obligations of a government. George F. Kennan [an American advisor, diplomat, political scientist, and historian. He is Professor Emeritus at the Institute for Advanced Study, Princeton.] http://faculty.arts.ubc.ca/price/373/kennan.pdf. “Government is an...“good” or “bad”.”
The standard is protecting the security of a government’s citizens.
1: Nuclear weapons are necessary to uphold security.
Renouncement compromises a state’s security. Bruce D. Berkowitz 1985 [began his career at the Central Intelligence Agency and is a senior analyst at RAND and a research fellow at the Hoover Institution] Proliferation, Deterrence, and the Likelihood of Nuclear War.” The Journal of Conflict Resolution, Vol. 29, No. 1. http://www.jstor.org/stable/pdfplus/174041.pdf, pg. 125. “Furthermore, as several…difficult to pass.”
(A card that I sometimes read)
This means a state will maintain the ability to counteract that vulnerability. Thomas Schelling, 2009 [American economist and professor of foreign affairs, national security, nuclear strategy, and arms control at the School of Public Policy at University of Maryland, College Park. Was Distinguished University Professor Emeritus in the School of Public Policy at the University of Maryland. Recognized by the Nobel Prize committee as a pioneer in the development of noncooperative game theory and its application to problems of politics and international relations.] “A World Without Nuclear Weapons?” Daedalus, Vol. 138, No. 4. “A crucial question…And what then?”
A world without nukes would be extremely dangerous and unstable. Thomas Schelling, 2009 [American economist and professor of foreign affairs, national security, nuclear strategy, and arms control at the School of Public Policy at University of Maryland, College Park. Was Distinguished University Professor Emeritus in the School of Public Policy at the University of Maryland. Recognized by the Nobel Prize committee as a pioneer in the development of noncooperative game theory and its application to problems of politics and international relations.] “A World Without Nuclear Weapons?” Daedalus, Vol. 138, No. 4. “In a world…a nervous world.”
It would have devastating repercussions, including nuclear launch. Thomas Schelling, 2009 [American economist and professor of foreign affairs, national security, nuclear strategy, and arms control at the School of Public Policy at University of Maryland, College Park. Was Distinguished University Professor Emeritus in the School of Public Policy at the University of Maryland. Bruno Tetrais 2010 [Research Fellow with the Foundation for Strategic Research, located in Paris; Research Fellow with the Foundation for Strategic Research, located in Paris.] 2010 Center for Strategic and International Studies. “The Illogic of Zero.” http://www.gees.org/files/documentation/08052010072620_Documen-07830.pdf, 131.
