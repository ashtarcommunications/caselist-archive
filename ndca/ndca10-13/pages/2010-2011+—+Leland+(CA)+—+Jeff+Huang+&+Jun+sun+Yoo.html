Affirmative

1AC – Golden Desert + Stanford


Plan: The President of the United States of America should issue an executive order to withdraw United States Forces, Korea. We’ll clarify.

Contention 1: Securitization

Current military policy towards North Korea is marked by a continuation of Bush era coercive diplomacy that treats North Korea as a foreign enemy, making reconciliation impossible
Mason, 10 (Jeff Mason, Reuters reporter, 2010, “Obama tells military: prepare for North Korea aggression”, http://www.commondreams.org/headline/2010/05/24-6)

This coercive diplomacy is shrouded by militarist rhetoric towards North Korea, increasing marginalization by defining the world in terms of good versus evil
Bleiker, Professor of International Relations @ UQueensland, 03 (Roland, “A Rogue Is a Rogue Is a Rogue: US Foreign Policy and the Korean Nuclear Crisis,” International Affairs, Vol. 79, No. 4, July 2003, p 731-732)

This common perception dominates American policymaking towards the “Korea crisis” – media rhetoric characterizes North Korea only as a “rogue” state, creating an irrational fear that makes military response the only option
Cumings, Professor of History @ UChicago, 05 (Bruce-, Professor of History – University of Chicago, Korea’s Place in the Sun: A Modern History, p. 487-88)

This creates a perpetual state of warfare – violent framing of North Korea justifies permanent military presence on the Korean peninsula
Pena, Senior Defense Policy Analyst @ Cato, 02 –“Axis of Evil: Threat or Chimera?” Charles Charles Peña is senior defense policy analyst at the Cato Institute (Mediterranean Quarterly, Volume 13, Number 3, Summer 2002, 40-57)

This empirically flawed policy framework leads to an endless cycle of violence – American aggression incites North Korean nuclearization and retaliatory response, making escalation inevitable –
Bleiker, 03 (Roland, PhD in International Relations at ANU, professor of international relations at the University of Queensland, 11/18, International Affairs Volume 79 Issue 4, Pages 719 – 737 (http://www3.interscience.wiley.com/journal/118868835/abstract).

Securitization ensures that status quo talks fail – Antagonism due to previous militaristic incidents prevents reconciliation
Herman, 2/10/11 (Steve, Writer for Voices of America, a trusted news source for information since 1942, “North Korea Rules Out Further Military Talks With South”, http://www.voanews.com/english/news/asia/North-Korea-Says-No-Need-for-Further-Military-Talks-with-South-115709359.html)

Securitization guarantees endless warfighting and astonishing violence – Whole populations are mobilized in order to slaughter the people of the “Other” nations such as North Korea
Campbell,1998. David Campbell, professor of international politics at the University of Newcastle. Writing Security, 1998. (199 – 202)

Contention 2: Solvency

US troop removal key – only a US withdrawal sends a credible signal to Korea
Kim Johng Sohn, 09 (Tongail Korea Net, “US Should Terminate Military Presence in S Korea As Early As Possible,” 9-8, http://tongilkorea.net/2009/09/08/us-should-terminate-military-presence-in-s-korea-as-early-as-possible/)

Empirics are on our side – Korea opened up to us the only time we considered withdrawal
Cumings, 05 (Bruce-, Professor of History – University of Chicago, Korea’s Place in the Sun: A Modern History, p. 474)

Reconciliation trumps militaristic strategy – the status quo framework makes war inevitable
Bleiker, 05 (Roland, Ph.D. in International Relations from the Australian National University, Divided Korea : toward a culture of reconciliation, 2005)

Security is a societal constructed based on how we position ourselves in the world – The fetish for national security creates a self-fulfilling prophecy because American security necessitates the de-securitization of another nation
Prerna Lal 08, Post-Graduate Student in International Relations, Deconstructing the National Security State, No Date Given, http://prernalal.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/10/css-deconstructing-the-nat-sec-state.pdf

Plan opens up a new way of approaching policy decisions – Resolving the Korean crisis key to change our responses to future crises
Kang, professor at the Tuck school of Business at Dartmouth college 03 (David C., Sept. 03, Associate professor of government and adjunct associate professor at the Tuck school of Business at Dartmouth college, “International Relations Theory and the Second Korean War”, International Studies Quarterly, Vol. 47, No. 3, Date accessed: 5/18, JH & BH)

Policymakers are limited to a realist view of North Korea that prevents a true understanding of its motivating factors that are a result of emotional, historical, and social contexts. This closes off the space for discussion because policymakers dismiss all alternatives to realism. Only the plan opens up a new way of viewing North Korea
Bleiker, 05 (Roland, Co-director of the Rotary International Center for Foreign Studies @ the University of Queensland, “Divided Korea”, p.xxxi)

Eurocentric assumptions about realism have empirically been unable to explain Asian anomalies, a new approach is needed – our evidence is comparative
Kang, Professor of Government @ Dartmouth, 03 professor at the Tuck school of Business at Dartmouth college (David C., Spring 2003, Associate professor of government and adjunct associate professor at the Tuck school of Business at Dartmouth college, “Getting Asia Wrong: The need for new analytical frameworks”, International Security, Vol. 27, No. 4, Accessed 5/18/10)
