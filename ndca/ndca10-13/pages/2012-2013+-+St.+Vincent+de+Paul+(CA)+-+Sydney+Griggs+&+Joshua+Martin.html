Contention 1 is the Transportation Infrastructure AKA The Topic:

Along with the economic boom following the Great Depression, came a car culture where the travel patterns of men were privileged due to the role they had within their family. The 1990s presented a pivotal shift in social roles for all genders. The model in which transportation infrastructure is designed is still one that privileges masculinity. As Queirs and Marques da Costa explain in 2012, the transportation sector is dominated by male, androcentric policies. Despite the statistics proving feminine travel patterns are more dependent on public transit, the transit systems are still focused on masculine travel patterns, and infrastructure policy is decided and engineered along those lines. That makes transportation policymaking a critical locus point for discussing gender issues.

Status quo transportation infrastructure privileges masculine norms and misrepresent what new studies are calling the mobility of care which makes public transportation more challenging for persons in traditionally feminine roles. The gender bias exists in contrast with the actual data of ridership patterns. In 2012, Jaffe reports that transit systems are designed for the traditional male travel patterns of the nine-to-five commute, while ignoring the burden of care work that falls to feminine roles. Transit systems fail to accommodate the bulk of their users, who are using them primarily for child and family care. Our transportation policy should be reoriented towards a more gender-equitable form of infrastructure.

Bauhardt explains in 2004 that this bias does not only exist in the physical transit systems, but in the actual infrastructure planning process. The re-appropriation of time and space as commodities to be dominated through transportation systems is rooted in masculine ideology we must accelerate and overcome, never satisfied to work with nature, only against it. This technical thought is pervasive in our transportation planning, converting time, space, and the idea of transport into economic resources rather than social categories. Our feminist perspective dictates a shift from a quantitative analysis of transport to a qualitative perspective that focuses on social and quality of life effects.

Karen Warren and Duane Cady have written several articles on this topic which many of us have probably read but they make very good arguments about how the masculine ideology is at the root cause of things like proliferation, environmental destruction, domestic violence, and war. They say, this is because of a patriarchal preoccupation with activities, events, and experiences that reflect historically [masculine] assumptions. Patriarchal frameworks legitimate impaired thinking which is manifested in behaviors which, will make life on earth impossible. But even though we can, we dont need to win some grand scale impact to outweigh whatever disadvantage the negative might present. Its common sense and common ethics that drives our planning process.

Thus the plan: The United States federal government should substantially increase its transportation infrastructure investment by recognizing current transportation infrastructure as gendered and reconfiguring public transportation infrastructure in the United States to be gender neutral.
