1AC - Grady

Contention 1 - The Status Quo


First, the situation in Afghanistan continues to deteriorate—election fraud, failed offensives, bank collapse, and increased casualty levels demonstrate the failure of current U.S. strategy.
Walt, 9/14/10 [Stephen M. Walt, Robert and Rene Belfer Professor of International Relations at Harvard University, “Defending the Afghan Study Group Report,” Stephen Walt’s Foreign Policy Blog, September 14, 2010, __http://walt.foreignpolicy.com/posts/2010/09/14/defending_the_afghan_study_group_report__]
It is of course possible that Exum will one day be proven right, but one would have more faith in his judgment if the situation in Afghanistan had not gone from bad to worse since Obama took his advice … Given the actual situation on the ground (and the condition of the U.S. economy), the Study Group concluded that it did not make sense to spend $100 billion or more per year trying to "nation-build" in a country whose entire GNP is about $14 billion.

Second, COIN is doomed by its inherent complexity—we’ll enter the accompanying PowerPoint slide into the record of the debate—it speaks for itself.
Peña, 5/23/10 [Charles V. Peña, Senior Fellow at the Independent Institute, Senior Fellow with the Coalition for a Realistic Foreign Policy, former Senior Fellow with the George Washington University Homeland Security Policy Institute and Former Director of Defense Policy Studies at the Cato Institute, Adviser to the Straus Military Reform Project, Analyst for MSNBC television, holds an M.A. in Security Studies from George Washington University, “Why We Can’t Win in Afghanistan,” Antiwar.com, May 23, 2010, __http://original.antiwar.com/pena/2010/05/23/why-we-cant-win-in-afghanistan/print/__]
Finally, there is this... And – like a kaleidoscope – a small change to one thing changes everything else unpredictably and a completely new pattern emerges.

Contention 2 - The 9/11 Syndrome


First, the current strategy in Afghanistan bogs the U.S. down and constrains the effectiveness of its power.
Friedman, 9/08/10 [George Friedman, internationally recognized expert in security and intelligence issues relating to national security, information warfare and computer security, Founder, Chairman, and Chief Intelligence Officer of Stratfor—a private intelligence company that provides customized intelligence services for its clients, holds a Ph.D. in Government from Cornell University, “9/11 and the 9-Year War,” Republished from Stratfor by RealClearPolitics, September 8, 2010, __http://www.realclearworld.com/articles/2010/09/08/911_and_the_9-year_war_99164.html__)
The initial response was understandable and necessary … After nine years, the issue is not what to do in Afghanistan but how the global power can return to managing all of its global interests, along with the war on al Qaeda.

Specifically—an exclusive focus on Islamic terrorism has diverted America’s focus and weakened its global power.
Friedman, 9/08/10 [George Friedman, internationally recognized expert in security and intelligence issues relating to national security, information warfare and computer security, Founder, Chairman, and Chief Intelligence Officer of Stratfor—a private intelligence company that provides customized intelligence services for its clients, holds a Ph.D. in Government from Cornell University, 2010, “9/11 and the 9-Year War,” Republished from Stratfor by RealClearPolitics, September 8, __http://www.realclearworld.com/articles/2010/09/08/911_and_the_9-year_war_99164.html__]
The most significant effect of 9/11 was that it knocked the United States off its strategy…The single most important result of 9/11 was that it shifted the United States from a global stance to a regional one, allowing other powers to take advantage of this focus to create significant potential challenges to the United States.

Third – Obama’s trip to Asia proves that Afghanistan prevents the US from having diplomatic influence

He previously taught at Princeton University and the University of Chicago, where he served as master of the social science collegiate division and deputy dean of social sciences. I wouldn't call it a "shellacking," but President Barack Obama's trip to Asia wasn't a stunning triumph either…But that is harder to do when you've spent the past ten years wasting trillions (yes, trillions) in Iraq and Afghanistan while other states were building their futures, and have dug yourself into a deep economic hole.

Fourth, Afghanistan erodes U.S. diplomatic leverage—Obama’s recent trip to Asia proves.

He previously taught at Princeton University and the University of Chicago, where he served as master of the social science collegiate division and deputy dean of social sciences. I am swamped with teaching, travel and some writing deadlines the next two weeks, so my blogging output will probably be sparse … Our Asian partners still need us more than we need them, and the United States hardly needs to be begging them to cooperate with us.

Fifth, this prevents the U.S. from addressing grand strategic challenges that pose existential risks—drawing down in Afghanistan is necessary to refocus foreign policy and engage with the global commons
Bajpai, 9/13/10 [Kanti Bajpai, Professor of International Studies at Jawaharlal Nehru University (India), Professor in the Politics and International Relations of South Asia at both the School of Interdisciplinary Area Studies and the Department of Politics and International Relations at the University of Oxford (England), “Exit Is A Smarter Strategy,” The Times of India, September 13, 2010, __http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/articleshow/6541291.cms?prtpage=1__]
The problem with the present US course is that the workings of the US political and economic system, its role in East Asia and issues of the global commons are being neglected. … It must find its way back to these grand strategic challenges and not lose the woods for the trees.

Sixth effective American leadership is crucial to spur the international cooperation needed to address emerging threats—failure to do so makes global conflict inevitable.
The Quadrennial Defense Review Independent Panel—a bipartisan congressional panel charged with conducting an assessment of the assumptions, strategy, findings, and risks described in the Department of Defense’s Quadrennial Defense Review, 2010 (“The QDR in Perspective: Meeting America’s National Security Needs In the 21st Century,” The Final Report of the Quadrennial Defense Review Independent Panel, Released on July 29th, Available Online at __http://www.usip.org/files/qdr/qdrreport.pdf__, Accessed 11-12-2010, p. 28-29)
These five trends frame a range of choices for U.S. government and Department of Defense planners. … It will simply lead to an increasingly unstable and [end page 28] unfriendly global climate and, eventually, to conflicts America cannot ignore, which we must prosecute with limited choices under unfavorable circumstances—and with stakes that are higher than anyone would like.

Prefer our impact—our disjunctive scenario is exponentially more probable than the negative’s conjunctive disadvantages.
Eliezer Yudkowsky, Co-founder and Research Fellow of the Singularity Institute for Artificial Intelligence—a non–profit research institute dedicated to increasing the likelihood of, and decreasing the time to, a maximally beneficial singularity, one of the world’s foremost experts on Artificial Intelligence and rationality, 2006 (“Cognitive Biases Potentially Affecting Judgment Of Global Risks,” Draft of a chapter in Global Catastrophic Risks, edited by Nick Bostrom and Milan Cirkovic, August 31st, Available Online at __http://singinst.org/upload/cognitive-biases.pdf__, Accessed 11-11-2010)
More generally, people tend to overestimate conjunctive probabilities and underestimate disjunctive probabilities. …Yet for a futurist, disjunctions make for an awkward and unpoetic-sounding prophecy.

Attempts to pinpoint the most likely proximate cause of nuclear conflict are futile—history demonstrates the importance of avoiding overconfidence and acknowledging uncertainty.
Keith B. Payne, Head of the Graduate Department of Defense and Strategic Studies at Missouri State University, President of National Institute for Public Policy, former Deputy Assistant Secretary Of Defense, 2010 (“Precision Prediction,” National Review Online, January 18th, Available Online at __http://www.nationalreview.com/articles/print/228975__, Accessed 11-11-2010)
A noted journal, The Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists, hosts a “Doomsday Clock” that symbolically counts down the minutes to nuclear doomsday; the editors occasionally adjust it with worldwide fanfare to show that the planet is a few minutes closer to or further from midnight, which represents the point of nuclear catastrophe. … They ought not, however, continue with the pretense of credible insight into the likelihood and timing of nuclear war; that much is pure political flimflam.

Contention 3 - Civilian Control


First, Obama has caved to blackmail from military leaders—a crisis in civil-military relations is brewing in the status quo.
Cohen, 9/28/10 [Michael A. Cohen, former Senior Research Fellow at the New America Foundation, B.A. in International Relations from American University and M.A. in International Relations from Columbia University, served in the Department of State as chief speechwriter for U.S. Representative to the United Nations Bill Richardson and Undersecretary of State Stuart Eizenstat, currently serves on the board of the National Security Network and has taught at Columbia University’s School of International and Public Affairs, “Our Ongoing Civil-Military Relations Crisis,” Democracy Arsenal—an international relations blog published by the Center for American Progress and the Century Foundation's Security and Peace Initiative, September 28, 2010, http://progressiverealist.org/blogpost/our-ongoing-civil-military-relations-crisis]
Today's gobsmacking quote from the new Woodward book … And yet here you have four individuals basically acting in a way that violates the most basic tenets of civil-military relations in this country. That's a crisis folks.

Second, Obama’s deference to Petraeus devastates civilian control of the military—the President, not the generals, should determine U.S. Afghanistan strategy.
Pillar, 9/16/10 [Paul R. Pillar, Visiting Professor and Director of the Security Studies Program in the Edmund A. Walsh School of Foreign Service at Georgetown University, served in the Central Intelligence Agency for 28 years, “Civil-Military Relations and Decision-making on Afghanistan,” Paul Pillar’s Blog at The National Interest, September 16, 2010, http://nationalinterest.org/print/blog/paul-pillar/civil-military-relations-decision-making-afghanistan-4089]
A feature article [3] in the New York Times points to a significant deficiency in the process through which the Obama administration will make its next major decisions about the war in Afghanistan, although the journalists don't call it a deficiency. … It also will be contrary to the American tradition of civilian supremacy over the military.

Third, now is the key time for Obama to reassert control and reject the Petraeus Lobby’s pleas for more time to continue COIN—this is vital to reassure allies and restore America’s international credibility.
Bacevich, 10/05/10 [Andrew J. Bacevich, Professor of History and International Relations at Boston University, graduated from West Point, former Colonel in the United States Army, holds a Ph.D. in American Diplomatic History from Princeton University, “Obama Must Defy His Generals,” The New Republic, October 5, 2010 http://www.npr.org/templates/story/story.php?storyId=130346439]
"The President is an elected king," wrote Randolph Bourne nearly a century ago, "but the fact that he is elected has proved to be of far less significance ... than the fact that he is pragmatically a king." … Should he squander this last opportunity, however, the king is likely to find himself soon thereafter seeking new employment.

This is a big deal—maintaining civilian control over the military is key to separation of powers, freedom, and democracy.
Kohn, 97 [Richard H. Kohn, Professor of History and Chairman of the Curriculum in Peace, War, and Defense at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, Executive Secretary of the Triangle Institute for Security Studies, and member of the Editorial Advisory Board of American Diplomacy, “An Essay on Civilian Control of the Military,” American Diplomacy, http://www.unc.edu/depts/diplomat/AD_Issues/amdipl_3/ kohn.html]
For democracy, civilian control – that is, control of the military by civilian officials elected by the people – is fundamental. … While a country may have civilian control of the military without democracy, it cannot have democracy without civilian control.

The impact is widespread oppression and violence—history proves.
Henderson, 91 [Lynne Henderson, Professor of Law at the Indiana University School of Law at Bloomington, “Authoritarianism and the Rule of Law,” Indiana Law Journal (66 Ind. L.J. 379), Spring, 1991, Available Online to Subscribing Institutions via Lexis-Nexis]
Substantive authoritarianism means opposition to the "liberal" values of tolerance of ambiguity and difference, insistence on obedience to rules, insistence on conformity, and use of coercion and punishment to ensure that obedience. … Other examples include the government largely ignoring oppression of and violence against African-American women, n22 and a long history of governmental tolerance of private oppression of women and children through violence. n23

Finally, evaluate this impact like a nuclear war—the risks are too high to justify.
Redish and Cisar, 91 [Martin H. Redish, Professor of Law and Public Policy at Northwestern University, and Elizabeth J. Cisar, Law Clerk to Chief Judge William Bauer of the United States Court of Appeals, “Constitutional Perspectives: Article: ‘If Angels Were To Govern’: The Need for Pragmatic Formalism In Separation of Powers Theory,” Duke Law Journal, December, 1991, Available Online to Subscribing Institutions via Lexis-Nexis, p. 449]
In summary, no defender of separation of powers can prove with certitude that, but for the existence of separation of powers, tyranny would be the inevitable outcome. … For just as in the case of the threat of nuclear war, no one wants to be forced into the position of saying, "I told you so."

The Plan


Thus, the plan:
The President of the United States should direct the Department of Defense to substantially draw down the United States military presence in Afghanistan by shifting from a counter-insurgency strategy to a counter-terrorism strategy including a decrease to 68,000 troops or less by October 2011 and 30,000 or less by July 2012.
We’ll clarify.

Contention 4 - Solvency


First, the plan is a substantial reduction in military presence—this saves billions and reduces resentment.
The Afghanistan Study Group, 8/16/10 (an ad hoc group of public policy practitioners, former U.S. government officials, academics, business representatives, policy-concerned activists and association leaders concerned with the Obama administration’s policy course in Afghanistan “What’s Wrong With The Current U.S Strategy,” A New Way Forward: Rethinking U.S. Strategy in Afghanistan, August 16, http://www.afghanistanstudygroup.org/NewWayForward_report.pdf)
2. Scale back and eventually Suspend Combat operations in the South and Reduce the U.S. Military footprint. … This step would save the U.S. at least $60 billion to $80 billion per year and reduce local resentment at our large and intrusive military presence.

Second, shifting to counter-terrorism is the most effective strategy—it reduces casualties, reassures allies, and keeps the pressure on extremists.
Snodgrass, 8/05/10 [Thomas Snodgrass, Retired Colonel in the U.S. Air Force, Director of Military Affairs for The Society of Americans for National Existence, Former Adjunct Professor of History at Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University, “Conservatives: Abandoning Counterinsurgency,” The American Thinker, August 5, 2010, http://www.americanthinker.com/2010/08/conservatives_abandoning_count.html]
The lesson learned from history must be to do what we know to be doable and to avoid what we have learned is not. … A "stable" Afghan government under Hamid Karzai is a losing proposition that should be carefully abandoned as inconspicuously and quickly as possible.

Third, their disadvantages are terminally non-unique—counterterrorism operations are increasing in the status quo and even the neg hacks are losing faith in COIN.
Kaplan, 10/13/10 [Fred Kaplan, National Security Columnist for Slate, holds a Ph.D. in Political Science from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, “A New Plan for Afghanistan,” Slate, October 13, 2010, http://www.slate.com/toolbar.aspx?action=print&id=2270971]
Officials say a shift in U.S. war strategy has begun to take place in Afghanistan, away from classic counterinsurgency (protecting the population, providing basic services, promoting good government) and toward the traditional business of killing and capturing bad guys. … However, it is now calculated, even by many COIN advocates, that this process would take too long—and be too corrupted by Afghan politics—to work in any practical sense.

Fourth, the plan is key to prevent precipitous withdrawal—public support makes a long-term counter-insurgency posture politically untenable.
Simon and Stevenson, 09 [Steven Simon, Adjunct Senior Fellow for Middle Eastern Studies at the Council on Foreign Relations and former Director for Global Issues and Senior Director for Transnational Threats at the National Security Council, and Jonathan Stevenson, Professor of Strategic Studies at the U.S. Naval War College, “Afghanistan: How Much is Enough?,” Survival, Volume 51, Issue 5, 2009, Available Online to Subscribing Institutions via InformaWorld]
It follows that the most difficult challenge to sustaining a maximalist US policy, leaving aside substantive questions of strategy, is that of keeping the American people on board. … If, on the other hand, the Obama administration promises more than it can deliver in Afghanistan, a reprise of Vietnam may occur: once failure becomes clear, domestic support will evaporate, the administration will be compelled to withdraw precipitously, and the United States will lose considerable traction in the region.

Fifth, the best case scenario for the current strategy in Afghanistan is even worse than outright failure—staying the course is delusional.
Engelhardt, 7/11/10 [Tom Engelhardt, Fellow at The Nation Institute, author of TomDispatch, Co-founder of the American Empire Project, “Tomgram: Engelhardt, The Petraeus Syndrome,” TomDispatch, July 11, 2010, http://www.tomdispatch.com/dialogs/print/?id=175272]
Okay, it hasn’t happened yet -- and the odds are it never will. But for a moment, just imagine stories like that leading the news nationwide as our most political general in generations comes home to a grateful Washington. …It’s clear enough that a failed counterinsurgency war in Afghanistan will be an unaffordably expensive catastrophe. Let’s not wait a year to discover that there’s an even worse fate ahead, a “success” that leaves us mired there for years to come as our troubles at home only grow. With everything else Americans have to deal with, who needs a future Petraeus Syndrome?

Finally, the plan enables a shift to more effective counter-terrorism operations—reducing military presence is key.
The Afghanistan Study Group, 8/16/10 (an ad hoc group of public policy practitioners, former U.S. government officials, academics, business representatives, policy-concerned activists and association leaders concerned with the Obama administration’s policy course in Afghanistan “What’s Wrong With The Current U.S Strategy,” A New Way Forward: Rethinking U.S. Strategy in Afghanistan, August 16, http://www.afghanistanstudygroup.org/NewWayForward_report.pdf)
3. Keep the focus on Al Qaeda and Domestic Security. The U.S. should redirect some part of the savings from this troop reduction toward improved counter-terrorism efforts and protecting U.S. citizens from terrorist attacks…The Study Group also believes that more effort should be made to exploit potential cleavages among different radical groups in the region, a goal that would be facilitated as the U.S. military presence declines.

The December Review


First, the Obama Administration is conducting a strategic review of its Afghanistan policy in December—this has set in motion a political streetfight between the President and his military commanders.
Joshua Foust, military intelligence analyst who specializes in the socio-cultural dynamics of irregular warfare, has worked on open-source analysis programs for the Defense Intelligence Agency and as an analyst for the Afghanistan cell of the U.S. Army Human Terrain System, currently serves as the Afghanistan editor at Global Voices Online and writes for Registan.net—the leading academic blog about Central and South Asia, spent 10 weeks in 2009 embedded with the U.S. Army in Afghanistan, 11-16-2010 (“Don’t bring a knife to a gunfight… over Afghan strategy,” Need To Know—a public affairs program on PBS, November 16th, Available Online at http://www.pbs.org/wnet/need-to-know/voices/don%E2%80%99t-bring-a-knife-to-a-gunfight%E2%80%A6-over-afghan-strategy/5168/, Accessed 11-18-2010)
The war in Afghanistan is at a crossroads, as it is at all times… In short order, military intelligence officials quietly pushed back, feeding their beliefs that the very high-profile attempts to kill or negotiate with the mid-level Taliban leadership haven’t noticeably affected the insurgency to still more reporters.

Unfortunately, Obama will cave to the military brass in the status quo and recommit to Petraeus’s COIN strategy.
