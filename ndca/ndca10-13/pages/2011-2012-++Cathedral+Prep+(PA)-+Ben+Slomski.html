1AC-Vegetable Lasagna (Wake Forest-all prelims, Monticello-all prelims)

I affirm: Justice requires the recognition of animal rights.

The Free Dictionary defines animal rights as[1]:
Protection of animals from cruelty through requirements of humane treatment. Laws protecting animal rights proscribe certain forms of brutal and merciless treatment of animals in medical and scientific research and in the handling of and slaughter of animals for human consumption.

Prefer reasonable aff interps
1. Time skew
2. Strat skew
3. Empirical neg bias

V-justice-Plato-give each their due

VC-Respecting sentience
First, granting rights based off of species is arbitrary and ignores one’s inherent value. The species a creature belongs to has no bearing on whether they have rights. Species does not determine interests or welfare, both of which are involved with rights. Cavaleri states[2]:
"Nonetheless, it is just ... therefore equally unacceptable."

Second, drawing the line for consideration of others anywhere else but sentience is wrong – just as racism and sexism are. Singer writes[3]:
If a being suffers ... identical in each case. "

Third, deciding what beings deserve rights based off of intelligence or cognitive capacity should be rejected. This would justify harm to humans that are mentally disabled and lack intelligence. Under this view, they would lack rights and anything could be done to them. Being a sentient creature is the only proper standard for rights. Singer continues[4]:
"In case there ... treating nonhumans in this way."

Fourth, presume the aff framework because the moral costs of being incorrect on the question of animal rights are uncalcuable. As long as there is a non-negligible risk of sentience a creature deserves rights. Kriegel states[5]:
"Scientific knowledge, being ... committing moral horrors.
"
Thus I contend that animals are sentient.

A. Science reveals that animals and humans are neurologically similar. Animals have similar mental experiences as humans. Allen explains[6]:
"Neurological similarities between humans ... the nature of animal experience (Grandin 1995, 2004)."

B. Animals are capable of suffering just as humans are. Therefore animals have the right not to suffer. Gruen states[7]:
"Contemporary utilitarians, such ... are morally considerable."

C. Animals pursue interests and ends just as humans do and that give their lives value. Korsgaard writes[8]:
"There is a more general, ... respect them as ends-in-themselves."

D. Studies prove animals can make moral claims-rhesus monkeys suffered hunger so they could avoid harming other monkeys. Rachels states[9]:
"But some animals have ... suffered really terrible hunger."

E. Animals will send signs that they are in distress-pigs and tail-chewing prove. Fraser states[10]:
"For animals kept ... well-being in numerous ways(Luescher et al.,1989)."

1AC-LARP (Bronx-all prelims)

I affirm.
Observation one is the framework. The government is the actor in the resolution. First, Humphrey defines animal rights as[1]:
"The claim that animals ... capacities of a species."

Thus the aff must defend a political policy. Second, governments are the only ones that can actually grant animals rights and can enforce and protect these rights through laws. Animal rights can’t actually exist without government action.

V-just governance
VC-utilitarianism. First, government officials specifically must act through utilitarianism because they can only make decisions based on the good of the public. Goodin states[2]:
"My larger argument ... chose general rules or conduct."
The very risk of extinction outweighs any consideration – universal morality demands my impact comes first. Seeley writes[4]:
"In moral reasoning ... sane moral code."
Thus justice requires the recognition of animal rights if it passes a utilitarian calculus.
Observation two is inherency.
The Navy currently employs Low Frequency Active sonar but the brink is now. Current regulations on LFA end in a year and the Navy is pushing to use LFA in 75% of the world’s oceans. Smith 9/29/11[6]:
"Comments on the National Marine Fisheries Service’s ... This story is far from over…"
Thus the plan: The United States Federal Government should ban the Navy’s use of Low-Frequency Active sonar. Links must be specific to the plan. Aff reserves the right to clarify.
Solvency:
LFA disrupts whales’ and dolphins’ hearing systems which interferes with their life functions and causes mass death. This is conceded by the Navy. The National Resources Defense Council writes[7]:
"In March 2000, ... confirm the risks."
Green provides a laundry list of reasons why LFA risks marine mammal extinction[8]:
"The Marine Mammal Commission, ... eaten by other predators."

The impact is global extinction.
A. Whales are key to maintaining the amount of plankton in the sea which influences Earth’s oxygen supply. This is necessary to support human life. Barstow explains[9]:
"Whales are at the top ... by whale consumption. "

B. Whales and dolphins are key to maintaining biodiversity. They indicate ecosystem health and eliminate infected fish. Mundo Azul states[10]:
"The conservation of whales ... sustainable fisheries."

Biodiversity is key to preventing extinction-it cycles chemicals that support life. Craig 03[11]:
"Biodiversity and ecosystem function ... out of business as a result."

1NC-Cohen (Wake Forest-all prelims, Monticello-all prelims, Bronx-all prelims)

I negate Resolved: Justice requires the recognition of animal rights.

V-justice-Plato-giving each their due.

VC-respecting moral autonomy, or the ability to make moral choices, laws, or claims. Possession of moral autonomy is necessary to have rights because rights are within a community of moral agents. Cohen explains[1]:
"A right, properly ... persons, human beings."

I contend that animals lack moral autonomy. Rights involve moral laws and can only be defended by humans. Animals are unable to make moral choices, make moral laws or make moral claims. Moral autonomy is a distinctly human obligation toward others. Cohen continues[2]:
"The attributes of human beings ... have none to violate."

However, this does not justify doing anything we want to animals. Humans can respect animals and protect them from harm without giving them any rights or entitlements. They should be treated humanely but do not have the right to be treated humanely. Cohen continues[3]:
"It does not follow from ... the holders of rights."

1AC-Morality Police (Ohio Valley-All Aff Prelims and Octas vs. Mountain Brook AC)

I affirm: Individuals have a moral obligation to assist people in need.

The value is morality because the resolution questions individuals’ moral obligations. Morality is defined by the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy as[1]:
"The term “morality” ... all rational persons."

The value criterion is respecting legal obligations. If individuals have a legal obligation to assist people in need then they have a moral obligation. First, morality is shaped by law. Laws help determine what actions are moral. Christie writes[2]:
"There is, however,... common modern phenomenon."

Second, judges use morality to determine the law. Spaak explains[4]:
"The primary task ... to legislative efficiency."

Fourth is the social contract. Citizens of a government enter into a social contract with their government. The government protects the citizens’ rights and the citizens follow the government’s laws in exchange. Per the social contract if the government’s laws obligate citizens to do something, the citizens are morally obliged to follow that law unless the law and government is unjust, in which case the citizens would overthrow the government. Also, the reciprocal obligation of the social contract calls for citizens to help each other and protect each other in order to advance society.

Contention one: Individuals are legally obligated to assist people in need because of duty to rescue laws.

Shelto defines duty to rescue laws[5]:
"Continental legal systems, ... cause or nature."

Duty to rescue laws exist in the US as well-Vermont, Minnesota, automobile accident, and police example.

Societal norms require adherence to duty to rescue laws. D’Amato writes[7]:
"In any case,.. distinctly societal interest.30"

Individuals are legally, and therefore morally, obligated to assist people in need.

Contention two: Duty to rescue laws are effective.

Duty to rescue laws can increase the number of lives saved for four reasons. Silver explains[8]:
"The strongest argument ... would go undetected. "

Contention three: Individuals would rationally agree to a duty to rescue law.

Guarantees rescue for them if ever in need.

Thus, since individuals have a legal obligation to follow duty to rescue laws and duty to rescue laws improve society and save lives, I affirm.

1NC-Autonomy (Ohio Valley All Neg Prelims)

I negate: Individuals have a moral obligation to assist people in need.

The value is morality because the resolution questions individuals’ moral obligations.
The value criterion is respecting autonomy, or one’s ability to exercise their freedom and make decisions for themselves. One has to have autonomy in order to act morally. First, the sole purpose of morality is to determine whether one’s choices are good or bad. All actions result from choices which are judged by morality. Second, being a moral agent involves the ability to make moral choices. If one is forced against their will and does not make moral choices on their own, they cannot morally be held responsible for their choices since they were forced against their will. Third, all moral systems require that individuals have the ability to make their own choices and all view violations of autonomy as immoral. Fourth, restricting autonomy disrespects one’s inherent value because it takes away their right to choose for themselves.
Regis Jr. defines how individuals can morally exercise their autonomy[2]:
"On the view .. the interests of others."

Thus individuals should be able to act to increase their happiness and to choose their own moral obligations.
Contention one: Forcing individuals to assist people in need violates individuals’ autonomy. Individuals should be able to choose their moral obligations but affirming forces one upon them. A moral obligation to assist people in need views people as tools to be sacrificed. Rand writes[3]:
"These three schools ... are achieving now."
Forcing the moral obligation violates authentic benevolence. Rand continues[4]:
"By elevating the ... his loved ones."
Contention two: A moral obligation to assist people in need harms society.
Helping others is dangerous because we could cause more harm since we are not informed of other’s needs. It violates privacy and encourages those in need to become dependent and unproductive. Rachels explains Rand's view[6]:
"The first argument ... social be the same.”

Assisting people in need causes those in need to become dependent on others and bringdown society with them. Societal advancement protects autonomy because the stronger a society is, the better equipped it is to make sure everyone’s autonomy is protected, but if it is weak it cannot do this. It also gives everyone a better chance to improve themselves and thus increase their autonomy.

1AC-Heidegger (Emory-all prelims and Doubles vs. Scarsdale GK)

I affirm.

The neg must prove the textual converse of the resolution that it is not morally permissible for victims to use deadly force. Textuality is key to fairness because it determines what the topic lit and ground is. If the neg doesn’t abide by the text of the resolution they can be completely unpredictable giving the aff little chance of winning.
Deadly force is defined by the Legal-Dictionary hosted by the Free Dictionary as[1]:
An amount of force that is likely to cause either serious bodily injury or death to another person.
Deliberate is defined by Webster’s New World Dictionary as[2]:
"Deliberate: “1) carefully thought out ... unhurried and methodical”
Meyersfeld defines domestic violence as[3]:

"Generallydomestic violence is ... exigency of violence escalates."

If you want another interp or definition, ask in cross-ex and I’ll grant it.

The value is morality because the resolution questions the moral permissibility of an action. Ontological systems of morality come first because morality needs to make assumptions about ontology in order to function and ontology is necessary to shape action. Dillon writes[4]:
"As Heidegger-himself an especially ... technocrats of decision making."

Existence and being in the world is a state known as “Dasein.” [dah-sine] Critchley summarizes Heidegger[5]:
"Heidegger's name for ... not distinguish ourselves."

There are two forms of Dasein-authentic and inauthentic. Inauthentic Dasein is also known as the “they”. Hornsby explains Heidegger[6]:
"Not everything is possible ... Dasein bars its own way.”

The standard is rejecting the “they.” Any system of morality requires a rejection of the “they” and inauthentic Dasein because otherwise morality can’t exist. Everything can be blamed on the “they” destroying moral responsibility. Hornsby 2[7]:
"For Heidegger, the ... inauthentic human existence (Steiner 1978)."

Therefore any action that rejects the “they” is morally permissible and the aff burden is to prove that a victim’s use of deadly force against domestic violence rejects the “they.”

Contention one: Confronting mortality. Confronting the limits of mortality allows one to understand the finitude of life, rejecting the “they” and creating authentic life. Critchley 2[8]:
"That said, the basic idea ... calls "ecstatic temporality"."

Unlike some theories of “being-towards-death” experiencing the death of others can allow us to become authentic. Critchley 3[9]:
"Despite its baroque linguistic ... grief and mourning."

A victim’s use of deadly force against domestic violence confronts mortality for 4 reasons:
1. By using force that can cause death the victim is forced violate the mortality of the abuser, making them realize the finitude of human life.
2. The victim’s use of deadly force is intended to protect their own mortality.
3. The link is supercharged since the deadly force is deliberate and premeditated. The victim has to think over the intentional taking of another’s life and must consider issues of mortality. This causes a confrontation of mortality regardless of whether the force worked.
4. By having force that could kill them used against them, the abuser is forced to confront their own mortality and the possibility of dying as a result of following the “they.”

Contention two: Angst. Angst rejects the “they” because it leads us to question our role in the world. Hornsby 3[10]:
"Inauthentic Dasein does not live ... repudiation of the ‘theyness’.”

A victim’s use of deadly force causes angst because the victim is likely to face much stress over the possibility of harming another and it is a stressful decision to make. The victim will face much worrying and distress while going through the decision process.

Underview one: Skep flows because if there are no moral rules then everything is morally permissible. If there are no moral rules then nothing can be morally prohibited and therefore everything would be morally permissible.

Underview two: Theory pre-empts. First, don’t vote off of aff violations of fairness as long as the neg has stock ground. The neg won around 64% of elims at Octas bid tournaments from doubles on before vbt where the best debaters are evenly matched. Even if the aff violates fairness they are only evening out the neg advantage and making it closer to 50-50 in the round. This leads to more fairness because it gives each an equal advantage.

There are two additional neg biases. A. Time skew-the aff only has 4 and 3 minutes to respond to 7 and 6 minute neg speeches allowing the neg to completely spread the aff out and B. Strat skew- the neg can adapt their strat to the AC, an advantage the aff doesn’t enjoy. These biases have 4 implications for the round:
1. Prefer reasonable aff interps.
2. Any “I meet” or offense on a neg theory interp is sufficient to affirm.
3. Only the aff gets RVIs because if the neg gets RVIs any aff that ran theory is practically forced to go all in on theory in the 2AR even if it isn’t the best strategy.
4. Absent offense presume aff because the aff had to overcome structural barriers.
