Affirmative


O1 - The resolution questions whether humans ought recognize the rights of other animals.
O2 - humans and animals do not necessarily have to have equally valued rights, and some rights can be overridden.

V: Justice

Science dictates that, because systems operate different than the whole of their parts, we cannot view individuals as distinct parts. Joanna Macy,
Joanna Macy [teacher, scholar, activist in civil rights and peace movements], “Working Through Environmental Despair,” Ecosychology. 1995.

People will never be happy so long as we view the world as ours. Hinchman,

Lewis Hinchman and Sandra Hinchman, “’Deep Ecology’ and the Revival of Natural Right,” The Western Political Quarterly, Vol. 42, No. 3. University of Utah. Jstor. Sept, 1989.


VC: recognizing interrelated ecology, as the organizing principle of the natural world. In order to uphold a systems view of the world, as opposed to an egotistical mindset, we must respect nature’s intrinsic value as a whole system.

1. Our insensitivity toward nature is a direct result of our failure to view the world as one interrelated system. Such failure will result in human and ecological disaster from global warming. A Commission on Global Health, headed by Anthony Castello in 2009, summarizes the catastrophic implications,
Lancet and University College London Institute for Global Health Commission, headed by Anthony Castello, “Managing the health effects of climate change.” Lancet, Vol 373, May 16, 2009.

2. Human life takes meaning from our place in the larger ecology. Carolyn Merchant,

Carolyn Merchant, “The Death of Nature,” in Michael Zimmerman’s Environmental Philosophy. Prentice-Hall, Inc. 2001.



Contention one, because humans are an equal part of the natural system, we ought not always and unconditionally value ourselves above other animals. Hinchman 3 explains how we can only respect our place in the natural system by identifying nature as an extension of us.

Furthermore, value exists in animals place in the natural system and are not dependent on human interests. Hinchman 4,



Contention two, value hierarchies that place certain beings above others are non-sensical in interconnected ecosystems. Human sentience is irrelevant to moral claims, due to the natural intrinsic worth of life. David Keller,

David Keller, “Gleaning Lessons from Deep Ecology,” Ethics and the Environment, Indiana University Press. Autumn 1997, Jstor.
Furthermore, animals do not have lower sentience than us but rather possess different types of equally valuable knowledge.


Rather than obsessing with issues of intellect, holism justifies a leveling of hierarchies in the natural world. Merchant 2:


Negative

RATIONALITY NEG

V: Justice

We should base ethics on the principles of constructivism – morality is not contingent on physical facts, but on our construction of reality. Glasersfeld,

Ernst von Glasersfeld [philosopher, Emeritus Professor of Psychology at Univ Georgia], “An Introduction to Radical Constructivism,” Originally Published in P. Watzlawick (Ed.).

Morality is created from the core concept of rationality. Watkins and Fitzpatrick explain how rationality is fundamental to our own value:
Eric Watkins and William Fitzpatrick [in respective order: Department of Philosophy, University of California, San Diego; Department of Philosophy, Virginia Tech]. O’Neill and Korsgaard on the Construction of Normativity . The Journal of Value Inquiry 36: 349–367, 2002. 2002 Kluwer Academic Publishers. Printed in the Netherlands. http://www.springerlink.com/content/4b9e64tdfr1nh9x7/fulltext.pdf

Watkins and Fitzpatrick 2 explains how this generates obligations to other rational agents.

VC: respecting rational agency


I contend that animals lack the rational agency necessary for moral recognition. Christine Korsgaard compares the mental states of non-humans as opposed to humans,
Christine Korsgaard [Harvard PhD, philosopher and professor], “The Activity of Reason,” Harvard University.


The ability to of humans to formulate language validates this point. Nordin,
Ingemar Nordin, 2001 ANIMALS DON’T HAVE RIGHTS: A PHILOSOPHICAL STUDY, Libertarian Alliance, Philosophical Notes No. 62,

Only by being capable of formulating moral maxims can we understand, follow, and thus be a part of moral codes. Carl Cohen explains how this is a uniquely human capability,
Carl Cohen, The Animal Rights Debate. Rowman & Littlefield Publisher, Inc. 2001.





DISEASE DA

A. Link:
By defending animal rights, the affirmative prevents us from using animals for medical testing.

1. The resolution specifies that the affirmative defends the inclusion of animal rights. This particular advocacy demands that we no longer use animals for our own benefits. Doris Lin,
Doris Lin [animal rights attorney, vice president of legal affairs for the bear education and resource group], “Animal Rights v. Animal Welfare,” About.com.

2. Animal rights would trump human interests. Carl Cohen explains,
Carl Cohen, The Animal Rights Debate. Rowman & Littlefield Publisher, Inc. 2001.

B. Impacts:
Animals are critical in the development of vaccines and drugs that save countless lives across the globe. Cohen 2,

More specifically, a suitable vaccine must be tested and developed for malaria. Cohen 3,


We need to use animals because it would be impossible to recruit human test subjects.
