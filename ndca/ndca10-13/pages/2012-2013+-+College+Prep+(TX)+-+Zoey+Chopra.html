Sept/Oct Topic
Neg.

A citizen is defined by the OED as "one possessing civic rights and privileges"

My Value is Morality
My standard is Upholding the Social Contract

1. We all joined a covenant and elected a sovereign so that we may be protected fully. Thomas Hobbes [Philosopher, Secretary to Francis Bacon, Worked for Cavendish Family]. The Leviathan, pp. 150-151. 1651."The only way...and common defence"

2. The Sovereign has an obligation to protect its citizens first and foremost, over anything else, or be dissolved. Thomas Hobbes [Philosopher, Secretary to Francis Bacon, Worked for Cavendish Family]. The Leviathan, pp. 195. 1651."The obligation of...it, be immortal"

Nothing is morally obligated before the covenant.Rick Parrish, Violence Inevitable: The Play of Force and Respect in Derrida, Nietzche, Hobbes, and Berlin , pp. 67, 2006."Nothing is Owed...Can be Unjust"


Contention 1: By giving non-citizens rights, we are treating non-citizens better than citizens, thereby violating the Social Contract and being Unjust.

In the Status Quo, Citizens accused of terrorism do not receive any due process rights. In fact, U.S. legalities that apply to non-citizens accused of terrorism also apply to citizens.
Kent Roach [Professor of Law and Prichard and Wilson Chair in Law and Public Policy, University of Toronto]. NATIONAL SECURITY: PART II: ARTICLE: “UNEASY NEIGHBORS: COMPARATIVE AMERICAN AND CANADIAN COUNTER-TERRORISM,” in William Mitchell Law Review. 2012. Lexis- Nexis. pp. 1727-28
"What may appear...admitted extra-legal conduct"
Due Process Rights provide a huge advantage.
“Annual Review of Criminal Procedure,” Georgetown Law Journal, 2010.
"The Due Process Clause...the same offense"

Aff.
In Most Cases, when discussing “ought” as moral, it implies an obligation. Michael Zimmerman [Professor of Philosophy and Director of the Center for Humanities and the Arts at CU, Boulder]. The Concept of Moral Obligation, Cambridge University Press, pp. 3-4, 1996.
“’ought’ cannot be…the term ‘moral’”

Value—Morality
Standard—Maintaining Consistency with Contracts
We enter contracts because they are mutually beneficial to both sides, and stop moral wrongness. Thomas Michael Scanlon [Alford Professor of Natural Religion, Moral Philosophy, and Civil Polity in Harvard University's Department of Philosophy. He has been awarded a MacArthur Foundation "Genius" Grant.]. What We Owe to Each Other, Harvard University Press, pp. 5, 1998.
“It holds that…back to Rousseau”

Because we consented to contracts, we are morally obligated to them. Thomas Michael Scanlon [Alford Professor of Natural Religion, Moral Philosophy, and Civil Polity in Harvard University's Department of Philosophy. He has been awarded a MacArthur Foundation "Genius" Grant.]. What We Owe to Each Other, Harvard University Press, pp. 260, 1998.
“From the fact…not consented to”

Promises cannot morally be broken, even with extreme consequences. Contracts are promises. John Rawls [James Bryant Conant University Professor at Harvard University]. “Two Concepts of Rules,” The Philosophical Review, Vol. 64, No. 1, pp. 17, January, 1955.
“But to do…been extremely severe”

Value- Morality
