Our Plan: The United States federal government should transition to an effective counterterrorism strategy by reducing the amount of its military personnel present in Afghanistan to 13000 by 2012.

Legitimizing Karzai’s rule opens up the door to national-local cooperation and self-determination – the biggest obstacle is COIN.
Ungaro 6/14 (Carlos, former Italian diplomat to Afghanistan, http://www.opendemocracy.net/carlo-ungaro/is-afghan-jirgah-way-forward) GAT
The military intervention in Afghanistan has become one of the longest-lasting war efforts in US history. It does not look like a coincidence that particularly deadly attacks are being carried out by the Taleban, while the repeated threats of an “all out attack” on Kandahar underlines the Sisyphean nature of NATO’s efforts. These circumstances do not bode well for the success of the recently convened “Peace Jirgah”, and yet the idea deserves close scrutiny and should not be dismissed, out of hand, as yet another failed attempt by The Afghan Government - with the support of its allies – to embark upon a political path instead of relying mainly on a military venture which shows no signs of imminent success. My first memory of an Afghan Jirgah dates back to the years of World War II, when, as a child, I was living in Kabul with my parents: the British had issued strong demands that Afghanistan deport all Axis citizens, and close their Diplomatic Missions. A “Loya Jirgah” was convened and, after many days of debate, declared – to our great relief – that this would contravene Afghanistan’s laws of hospitality. A fundamentally important decision had been taken which would guarantee Afghanistan’s neutrality until the Soviet invasion of 1979. The Jirgah has traditionally been an institute of primary importance in the Pathan tribal areas of Pakistan and the Pashtun areas of Afghanistan. Although, traditionally, it gathered only Pashtun tribal elders, in modern times it has been extended also to include representatives of all the other areas. It can be argued, as, indeed it has been argued, that this form of representation is much more suited to the Afghan political reality than a western-style Parliament, no matter how correct the electoral process. Afghanistan, as Pakistan’s North-West, is basically a tribal society, and peaceful coexistence among the tribes cannot be guaranteed by political processes which do not take this reality into account. With the passage of time, the term “democracy” has acquired a rather dogmatic aspect, and is associated with certain particular rituals which have evolved, in the course of centuries, in some western societies, and which have then been applied, with success, also outside Europe and North America. An analysis, even a superficial one, of local interpretations of democracy would be completely out of context in an attempt to examine the Afghan situation, which is of grave and understandable concern, but serious thought ought to be given on how it would be possible to reconcile the need for stability in such a volatile and strategically important area with social and political realities which long predate the current insurgency, and all this without further eroding basic liberties. It is, of course, probable that the Jirgah called by president Karzai will not have immediate, appreciable repercussions in the desired direction, mainly because, rightly or wrongly, the President no longer has the massive backing of the Afghan people, not even in his own tribal area. At the same time, his support among the leading international actors in Afghanistan has also sensibly declined. These circumstances, unfortunately, lend credibility to the accusations that this Jirgah is rigged and that it has been packed with Karzai supporters, thereby depriving it of the dignity essential to its success. A future scenario could, however, be envisaged in which enhanced local autonomy would allow the application of “our” democratic rules in Towns and Provinces ready freely to choose such a solution (Herat comes to mind), while others could choose more traditional methods of local rule. The central Government, instead, with a role more of guidance and coordination rather than dominance or rule, would be more in tune with the traditions of the land. This arrangement has worked in the past, and could be an indication – with the necessary variations – of a way forward. The calling of Jirgahs, and their composition and competencies should not be left to the arbitrary will of the Head of State, but rather be regulated in a new, bold and imaginative attempt to reconcile respected and valid Afghan traditions to the country’s aspirations to be part of the modern family of nations. Of course, the principal obstacle to any durable, credible negotiated settlement remains the massive and bellicose foreign military presence, whose support of the government is distasteful to many strata of Afghan opinion. The possibility of solutions along the lines suggested by the Jirgah would be greatly enhanced if agreements in that sense would coincide with a publicized, credible and accepted timetable for the withdrawal of the bulk of foreign troops. As things stand, there are few alternative suitable solutions, and recourse to the Jirgah as a convincing instrument of negotiation should not be discarded. Undoubtedly the most severely affected victims of failure in Afghanistan, however, would be the Afghans themselves. Whether or not the Taliban ultimately emerged victorious, what is almost certain is that the country would be plunged into a renewed state of civil war, with numerous competing factions, each with outside countries supporting them, vying for power and with Afghan civilians caught in the middle. It has been said of the conflict in Afghanistan that it is a futile and even neo-imperialistic endeavour to impose foreign values and ideas upon a people who neither need nor want them. This culturally relativist prognosis has at its heart the essentially racist assumption that the same basic human rights that Westerners take for granted, such as freedom of expression, freedom from exploitation, the right to education, sexual equality and the right to choose the form of government under which one wishes to live, are somehow not appropriate for Afghans. This assumption is not only a grave insult to Afghans, it is also false. As has been elaborated upon in Chapter II of this report, an overwhelming majority of Afghans, fully 90 per cent, believe that for all its failings, the current government is the best hope for the country’s future, whereas just six per cent wish to see a return to Taliban rule.114 Consistent with the Constitution of Afghanistan, the current government is committed to a society in which “Freedom of expression is inviolable”; citizens “have the right to form political parties”; “Torture of human beings is prohibited”; and “The citizens of Afghanistan - whether man or woman - have equal rights before the law”.115 This is to be starkly contrasted with the Taliban who, to take a recent example, sliced off the nose and ears of a 19-year old girl and left her to die for “shaming” her husband by running away on account of the torture and abuse she suffered at his hands and the hands of his family. It was the husband himself who carried the punishment out.116 It is certainly the case that many in the West, whilst not denying that Afghans are entitled to the same rights and freedoms as other peoples, nonetheless oppose foreign intervention in Afghanistan on the grounds that it is not facilitating the advancement of these rights and is in fact making matters worse. Those who adhere to this point of view should ask themselves what the likely consequences of withdrawal from Afghanistan before such time as the government can defend the rights of Afghans on its own would be. In addition to the 140,000 NATO and US soldiers on the ground, foreign assistance is developing the Afghan National Army and other security services to levels far beyond what the government, with an annual GDP of just $23 billion, could possibly sustain on its own.117 Just as happened following the Soviet withdrawal in 1989, the likely fate of the Afghan government if left to fight the Taliban and the warlords unassisted would be collapse. The just war tradition has been developing for centuries and is generally traced back to Saint Augustine and Saint Thomas Aquinas. From the time of its development, just war theory has been used to assess whether or not military force ought to be used. In the Global Studies Encyclopedia, J. C. Kunkel argues that just war theory operates under the principle of double effect: “Under double effect an action that has two contrasting effects, one good and the other evil, may be permissible under certain conditions provided that only the good end is intended” (2003, 313). Thus, the context for just war is one in which the evil effects of war are acknowledged, but if a specific set of conditions is met and the evil is not intended, then war becomes permissible. For Kunkel, proponents of just war theory operate under larger ethical systems, such as deontology, which states that humans have moral duties to obey. One discovers these moral duties by asking whether a particular action can be universalized. For instance, killing human beings cannot be universalized without falling into absurdity. Also, neglecting to preserve one’s life cannot be universalized without falling into absurdity. These two examples lead to the conclusions that a person both has a duty not to kill human beings and a duty to preserve her life. These two duties, however, may come into conflict when in order to defend herself and to preserve her own life, a person uses lethal force against another human being. When a person uses lethal force to defend her life, the conflict is an exemplary case of the principle of double effect because the action produces a good effect, preserving one’s life, and an evil effect, killing a human being. The action via which one both defends to preserve one’s life and kills another is, however, morally permissible, under the principle of double effect if the good effect is intended and is not directly produced by the bad effect (313). Just war theory takes the principle of double effect and applies it to largescale conflicts. In this case, a nation or group can act in a way that is normally excluded from ethical action. Under most conditions, it is not acceptable to kill human beings. According to just war theory, there are times in which a group or nation is morally free to act in such a way that the evil effects of war are the unfortunate by-product of seeking a good effect. To insure that the good effect, and not the evil effect, prevails, just war theory imposes five conditions that must be met for a war to be morally permissible: just cause, right intention, discrimination, proportionality, and legitimate authority (314). Just cause tests the end or purpose for which war is fought. Within the just war tradition, this is translated as defending human beings from death or serious human rights violations. Right intention adds a further condition to just cause by seeking “a caring common ground for resolving conflicts before proceeding to the belligerent military option” (314). If one can prevent serious harm and death without resorting to war in which serious harm and death happens intentionally to combatants and unintentionally to noncombatants, then one can obviate the principle of double effect and have multiple good effects instead of an evil effect and a good effect. Should negotiations fail, then just war theory adds another condition on those who would pursue war as the means to a good effect: discrimination between combatants and noncombatants. Military action cannot seek to harm noncombatants as part of its strategy to resolve a conflict; however, just war theory does allow that foreseeable noncombatant deaths are permissible so long as they are not intended. The fourth criterion that must be met is proportionality, which is an overall assessment of the probability and cost of success. Those who are seeking a good effect in spite of foreseeable, but unintended evil effects, must weigh whether the number of lives that will be saved is likely to be more than the number of lives lost and that the human rights that will be preserved are greater than the human rights that will be sacrificed. If a nation or group can satisfy all of the above conditions, they must also have legitimate authority. A suitable governing body must approve military action (315–17). Although all five conditions of just war theory must be met in order for war and military action to be morally permissible under the principle of double effect, the picture of war that just war theory presents is too narrow especially when it comes to war’s costs, justified or not. If just war theory were followed in its entirety, wars would presumably decrease. In 1915, while World War I was spreading, Jane Addams, who went on to win the Nobel Peace Prize in 1931, participated in the founding conference of the Women’s Peace Party in Washington, D.C., where she gave a speech that detailed the many hidden and added costs of war that simple calculations do not measure (1915, 61–64). Addams overall concern was that war destroys “sensitiveness to human life.” War destroys this sensitiveness by diminishing the protection, the nurture, the fulfillment, the conservation, and the ascent of human life. Addams elaborated each of the ways in which war undermines sensitiveness to human life. First, was destroys the protection of human life by instead redirecting toward the war effort resources and medical progress that could be channeled into, for example, curbing infant mortality and childhood diseases. Second, war compromises the nurture of human life by removing soldiers from society to fight—soldiers that would otherwise have been productive, cooperative members of society. The nurturing work is forever lost when soldiers die, and it is forever compromised and undermined even when those soldiers return. War circumscribes the fulfillment of human life when children who might otherwise have entered professions that serve humanity are instead sacrificed to professions that obliterate rather than uplift other human beings. War destroys the conservation of human life by diverting time, talent, and money away from social programs for children, the elderly, and the disabled. Finally, war cuts short the ascent of human life by decreasing sensitivity to human life instead of advancing our sensitiveness to human life between generations. Instead of building on the achievements of the last generation, those who have gone to war and suffered through war must begin again because war produces a hardening against human suffering (62–63). The costs of war that Addams enumerated serve as an important reminder that war takes its toll on some of the most fundamental relations of cooperation among people. Just war theory calculates the evil effects of war but does not define the evil effects of war broadly enough to account for the ways in which not only is justice compromised but sensitiveness to human life is compromised as well. What Addams pointed out was that wars, even justified wars, do far more destruction than what is apparent on the surface. Beyond tremendous loss of life and physical dismemberment, war undermines human trust and human life. Adams comments were against war because she favored its elimination, not its justification. Addams was saying that even with just wars the toll is too great. Stop wars and get on with life-giving practices. Wars destroy the very human trust they hope to protect and defend.
