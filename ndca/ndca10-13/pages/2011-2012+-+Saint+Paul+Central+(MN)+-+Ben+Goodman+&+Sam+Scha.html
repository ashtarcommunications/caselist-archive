Star Trek 1AC

Warp Speed. Engage.

The most immediate cultural reference for space exploration comes from the last three decades of the TV show Star Trek. Star Trek isn’t just a TV show. It’s how Ben and I know “space exploration,” and it’s a powerful mega-text that both represents and simultaneously critiques the mainstream narrative of the colonizing mission of the United States in space. Just as space exploration programs attempt to make sense of the unknown, the Federation approaches the alien Borg in what seems like a hegemonic narrative. In both cases however, when we confront the alien, Star Trek shows that the alien…is…us.
Lynette Russell and Nathan Wolski, archaeologist before shifting her research interests to focus on history and the cultural construction of knowledge, Senior Research Fellow at the Centre for Australian Indigenous Studies at Monash University; PhD. in contact archaeology at the University of Melbourne, his research includes postcolonial theory and the social construction of knowledge; 2001, Intensities, “Beyond the Final Frontier: Star Trek, the Borg and the Post-colonial,” (f)

Lady Gaga 1AC

We play the Born This Way Music Video (2 minutes and 30 seconds)



Thus, we artistically and metaphorically affirm the exploration of space beyond the Earth’s mesosphere.

The role of the ballot is to judge the most persuasive performance of identity surrounding the topic. The stage of debate is where we perform – our identity is a construct of this space that is the cloth from which we are cut; the womb from which we are infinitely born. Ben and I were born this way, as debaters, when we made the decision to join the activity. To ignore our choice to affirm our identities, in which the resolution is ingrained, is to end our continuity within this space. Absence of self in our own womb existentially jeopardizes both of us – that is to say that our very birth, life, and identity are literally annihilated. Without the explicit inclusion of celebratory otherness omnipresent in the manifesto of Mother Monster, we become two very real people that are subsumed and destroyed by the dissolving of identity.
Kate Durbin and Meghan Vicks (Kate Durbin is a Los Angeles-based writer, performance artist, and founding editor of Gaga Stigmata. She is the author of The Ravenous Audience, The Fashion Issue, and, with Amaranth Borsuk, Excess Exhibit. Meghan Vicks is a doctoral student of Comparative Literature, currently working on her dissertation, "Narratives of Zero: Aesthetics of Absence in Modern and Postmodern Literature." She teaches Humanities courses at the University of Colorado at Boulder.) “From The Fame to Born This Way: Lady Gaga and the Monstrous Evolution of Identity,” Gaga Stigmata, 3/1/2011 (gagajournal.blogspot.com/2011/03/from-fame-to-born-this-way-lady-gaga.html)
When Lady Gaga hit the pop culture-scape ... creation of her identity becomes how she was born.

The manifesto of Mother Monster begins its exploration of the resolution upon the Government-Owned Alien Territory in space – the imagery through which Gaga translates her message is that of the cultural idiom of the superstar. In giving birth to her own race of freaks, she simultaneously explores and develops space beyond Earth’s mesosphere to familiarize us with the notion of the alien.
Victor P. Corona Ph.D. (Corona is a Lecturer in the Department of Sociology at Columbia University), “Born Superstars,” Gaga Stigmata, 3/2/11 (http://gagajournal.blogspot.com/2011/03/born-superstars.html)

She teaches Humanities courses at the University of Colorado at Boulder.) “The Icon and the Monster: Lady Gaga is a Trickster of American Pop Culture,” Gaga Stigmata, 1/19/2010

Art is the sole way to redefine our own cognition and perception of the world – art teaches us that there is no one solution to a problem, no singular “correct” interpretation of a resolution, but instead a language in and through which we can bring images and utopias into reality. Space exploration is such an image in and of itself, but there is a clear distinction between our performance and that of a traditional policy debate – our integration of images is both a literal and metaphorical rupture in the divide inherent to debate which defends the use of static, external jargon binaries to give meaning to performance. With the use of images we can become the race of freaks whose biological identity is performance; whose very existence transcends the dividing nature of status quo debate that is apart artistic transgression.

In a very literal sense, competing and generalizing frameworks lead to the extermination of difference-ness within our own womb, turning the body from which we are birthed against its own child. Alignment against the hybridity of performativity and essentialism is alignment with the dichotomic oppressor.
