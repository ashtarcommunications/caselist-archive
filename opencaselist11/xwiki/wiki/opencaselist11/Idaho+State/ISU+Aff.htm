Section 1 is Our Community

 

We begin with the question “what is debate?” Is it a place where we pretend to be politicians? There is a persisting narrative on what this space should be, any time an aff chooses to endorse something other then a utilitarian policy frame they’re reigned down upon with arguments about destruction of ground, predictability, the death of debate, and one of my favorites, “cedeing the political.” We think its time to call into question that larger narrative, that the only time we can be political is when we pretend to be politicans, when we make “ethical” choices that “save the most lives.” This means that systematic violence, atrocities, and authoritarianism not only become acceptable in the name of securing security, but increasingly justified to satiate our desire to feel safe in the world

 

The focus on extinction, nuclear apocalypse and global war has distorted our perception and understanding of how we relate to the world around us. Instead of looking to atrocities in the world we look to the potential death of all species and say that any other impact has to take the back seat so we can avoid all out discussion – this renders the debate as a negative knowledge production center in the world

Danny Povinelli, a former NDT debater wrote in ‘85 (Danny Povinelli, 1985 University of Massachusetts, Clarifying Water Policy http://groups.wfu.edu/debate/MiscSites/DRGArticles/Povinelli1985Water.htm)

 “Debate is catastrophe....potentially valuable forum.

And this crisis focus blocks any attempt, or ability to use the debate space as a productive sphere to shape our relations to the world and formulate our ethical considerations into action – it’s the same ideology and discourse that was perpetuated during the cold war that only leads to social inaction and the worst forms of conservativism.

Ira Chernus 2k2 Professor at UColorado-Boulder “Cold War Redux” Shared Governance: Pleas and Provocations October, 2002 http://www.colorado.edu/FacultyGovernance/JOURNAL/index.html

 

The most important intellectual....and nothing need ever change

This discourse makes violence an inevitability – when we leave this space we will interact with the world – whether we become policy makers or not we will interact with the world and be we will be responsible for the changes we do form onto the world

Campbell 98

(David- PHD, Prof of cultural  & poli geog @ U of Durham, Writing Security, p.54-55)

 

It requires an emphasis on the.... Christendom's evangelism of fear. 

Section 2 is The Topic

 

Just like in debate, where we can prioritize the smallest risk of nuclear extinction over the ongoing killing of civilians, the United States embraces a view of the world that says we must always act in a utilitarian realist frame that pits us against the world with everything on the line. This ideaology is manifested in the US response to the protests in the middle east, to those countries that wont threaten the US’s conception of security in the region we offer our full “support” and cheer on the protestors, but when it threatens a US freidnly regime we send in the tanks and up our funding of the government and talk about “reforms” that need to occur – Bahrain is the exception to the rule of “Democracy for all”

Winegard 2011

[Ben Winegard Political Activist and Journalist, “Understanding Bahrain: How Bahrain Shines a Light on Imperial Policies,” Z Net, PDF, March 11, 2011, Accessed: July 30, 2011, MJ]

 

Given this milieu...of democracy, reform, and change.

 

There is an ongoing atrocity that is occurring in our name – civilians are rolled over by tanks and killed by bullets and suffocation in the name of protecting us from a demonized Iran. People are reduced to Objects, tools, that can be calculated, dispersed, and ordered to fit the US strategic interests, to fit into our game of trying to escape death. This is manifested not only in the policies we have in place like giving the regime bullets, ammo, tanks and money, but in the way that we Talk about them – in the Status quo “Bahrain” is a dirty word that gets ignored, we’d much rather talk about all the great help we’ve given to Egypt, or to libya, but the refusual to talk about Bahrain makes it an irrelevant issue and allows for the continuation of the worst atrocities

Fisher 2011

[Max Fisher, Associate editor of the Atlantic, “Obama’s UN Address and the Bahrain Exception,” September 21, 2011, http://www.theatlantic.com/international/archive/2011/09/obamas-un-address-and-the-bahrain-exception/245467/, AD: 9.22.11 MJ]

 

President Barack Obama, in.... it to Bahrain anytime soon.

Discourses of security are deployed to try and fragment society, and to build up walls of difference that prevent commonality or community – this form of ordering in social relations makes violence an inevitability as a means to secure ourselves from a threat

Czajka 2011

[Agnes Czajkla, Assistant Professor of Sociology at the American University in Cairo, “Securing the People (or State?): Efforts in Governing through Fear,” http://www.jadaliyya.com/pages/index/1090/securing-the-people-%28or-state%29_efforts-in-governin, Accessed: 8/17/11, MJ]

 

The concept of securitisation can...gain legitimacy through fear.


Section 3 is what it means to vote affirmative

 

Our Affirmation of democracy assistance to Bahrain is a demand that US hypocricsy should end when it comes to the region. We do not think that we should pretend to be USFG policy makers here, rather our advantage comes from us affirming the resolution here as a criticism of US Bahrain policy. We criticize the predominance of US security values and read this 1 ac as a protest to the on going atrocities being committed in our name in Bahrain

 

The United States supports autocratic violence out of the assumption that security is the highest value.. Security, Peace, Prosperity, in our name. This makes us complicit with those actions when we don’t choose to stand up and protest against those sorts of policies. People are quick to say that “its not my fault, im not obama, im not congress, im not a policy maker,” but the belief that our personal spheres aren’t political, and shouldn’t be used to resist these violent policies only reinforces the same policy paradigm that leads to wars for security – instead we have an obligation to act out against these policies from our own spheres 

Kappeler 95

[Susanne KAPPELER, Lecturer in English at the University of East Anglia (UK) and freelance writer, 1995 ["Violence and the will to violence," The Will To Violence: The Politics of Personal Behaviour, Published by Polity Press (Cambridge, UK) MJ]

 

Yet our insight that indeed... values of war and violence.

And we have an obligation to speak out from our individual locations against hegemonic discourses of violence and security – every day we have oppurtunities to make decisions that either feed or detract from the hegemonic discourse. Its these every day decisions like the ballot that provide us a chance to reject dangerous paradigms and open ourselves to new understandings of relating to the world

Bleiker 2000

[Roland Bleiker, Philosopher and Writer, “Discourse and Human Agency” Accessed: January 12, 2011, MJ]

 

Confronting the difficulties...positive contribution to the political.

The Traditional notion of what it means to “Affirm” calls for us to talk about hypothetical enactments, and doing the most good for everyone, but we have already shown why this sort of framing not only makes us complicit with larger systems of violence and denies us the ability to create agency in the debate space. Instead of asking what we would do if we were policy makers, we should be asking what we can do as debaters, once we start from the frame the changes may be small but they are better then epistemologically flawed knowledge that traditional debate provides us.

 

Debate is a sphere where we call into question the assumptions that we all have, the assumptions that we as individuals have, and then have a discussion on the way that we will relate to the world outside this space. Our 1 AC is democracy assistance – we call for the end of the status quo framing of Bahrain that says we have to protect the regime, that we have to protect the fifth fleet, that we have to protect ourselves from these giant threats. Instead we call for the support of the Democracit resistance in Bahrain, as an acknowledgement that right now we are complicit in the US policy toward Bahrain, that we have made this domination possible through our refusual to protest against those sorts of politics. Voting aff is an endorsement of our protest against the dominant security paradigm, both in Policy debate and in Foreign policy.

 

positive contribution to the political.
