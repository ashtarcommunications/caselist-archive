1AC - AFF: PRODUCTIONIST METAPHYSICS

Contention One: Descarte’s Bag of Tricks

Kaiser Sosé claimed that the greatest trick the devil ever pulled was
making the world believe he did not exist.  Our modern era has
increasingly been characterized by captivity to a civilizational system
that compels us to go on destroying the world in order to live. There
are many among us who would be glad to release the world from its
captivity. But they are prevented from doing this because they can not
see the bars of the cage.

The discipline of modern metaphysics was inaugurated by Rene Descarte’s
meditations by a fireplace. He thought to himself: how do I know that
my senses correspond to an external reality? How do I know that my
senses are not being manipulated by an evil genius?

A. Descartes thought he had found a solution: even if I doubt
everything else, I am sure that my thought is there. I think, therefore
I am. This description of consciousness depends on the depiction of a
subject as a psychosomatic apparatus bringing objects into certainty
through the workings of its mental apparatus. It is this objectifying
method that reduces the world into calculable quantities that comprise
a large machine.

hanspeter Padrutt, member of daseinanalytisches institut, heidegger and
the earth, ed. ladelle mcwhorter, 1992, pg 19-21

This understanding of subjectivity is neither natural nor inevitable.
But we are subtly conditioned to buy the Cartesian line. Think back to
Sesame Street: we see Elmo and Big Bird – we see thought bubbles above
their heads – and we see scenes playing out in the thought bubbles.
This is a depiction of mental content as representations about the
external world in the mind of a subject. What is the reason we have
been conditioned to understand ourselves in this way?

B. Humanity has found itself mired in an understanding of being that is
synonymous with production, a move that has resulted in the yoking of
the very forces of nature in the service of technological expansion.
Let us be clear: technology is not a set of devices – rather, it is a
particular set of ontological conditions that makes possible the
worldview of industrial production.

michael e. Zimmerman, professor of philosophy at tulane university,
heidegger’s confrontation with modernity, 1990, pg. xii-xiv

C. How does technology relate to our self-understanding? Technological
humanity is compelled to turn nature into a stockpile of raw materials
whose only value lies in their usefulness for our purposes. Central to
this project is the appropriation of natural entities for the purpose
of energy consumption. The continual consumption of fossil fuels
represents the triumph over nature as a potent object. The world is
revealed as standing reserve and humans as nothing more than the
orderer of the standing reserve.

Tad Beckman, Professor Emeritus of Philosophy at Harvey Mudd
College, “Martin Heidegger and Environmental Ethics,” 2000, [Online:
http://www2.hmc.edu/~tbeckman/personal/Heidart.html]

D. At the very pinnacle of this technological culture, however, a
fundamental reversal of the traditional subject/object dichotomy sets
in. Machine technology now rebels against us. We become slaves to a
technological mode of being that uproots us from the earth in an
infinite quest of magnification of power. Social heirarchies and
disciplines are produced in order to master humanity and make us
amenable to the demand of technology’s call to master the earth.

michael e. Zimmerman, professor of philosophy at tulane university,
heidegger’s confrontation with modernity, 1990, pg. 199-202

E. The violence of the Holocaust and hydrogen bombs were made possible
because of humanity’s conception of everything as resources to be
produced or destroyed at will. It is the logic of industrial
technology’s mode of being that made possible the most horrific
violence of the twentieth century.

michael e. Zimmerman, professor of philosophy at tulane university,
heidegger’s confrontation with modernity, 1990, pg. 43

F. Ultimately, our one-dimensional disclosure of entities denies them
any being at all. Nature is reduced to energy and we lose the capacity
to be-in-the-world. We can only exist instrumentally in the service of
technology’s final victory over nature. This eclipse of being has fated
us to exist in a state of ontological damnation, the eradication of any
possibility of a clearing through which life can manifest itself.

Michael Zimmerman, professor of philosophy at Tulane University,
Contesting the Earth’s Future: Radical Ecology and Postmodernity, 1997,
p. 119-20

Contention Two: The Open Expanse of the World

A. Language is the house of being. It discloses things about the way
our being has been constituted. The resolution allows us to see the
bars of the civilizational system that is keeping us captive to
technology’s imperative to continually consume fossil fuels. The call
to “let these entities be” represents a step back from the constant
purposiveness demanded by modern technology. The statement reveals that
technology is merely a purposeless historical world that is driven by
the infinite self-expansion of production. This disclosure of
groundlessness makes possible the openness necessary for the arrival of
a new era.

michael e. Zimmerman, professor of philosophy at tulane university,
heidegger’s confrontation with modernity, 1990, pg. 234-6

B. What effect does this opposition to absoloutist resource extraction
have on our own self-understanding? The resolution is couched in the
form of a grammatical imperative to an agent, the federal government.
This statement calls on that agent to let-being-be, to stop being as it
is, a call that threatens the typical configurations of subjectivity in
the modern age.

ladelle Mcwhorter, assistant prof of phil at northeast missouri state
university, heidegger and the earth, ed. ladelle mcwhorter, 1992, pg 4-7

C. The effect of this paradox is to inaugurate a shift away from the
objectifying subject to the open expanse of everyday being in
connection with what encounters it, what it cares about. Our project is
not a search for a true or authentic identity – rather, it is a call to
shift from the forced derivative disclosure of a calculating subject to
the open interconnected mystery of the world itself.

hanspeter Padrutt, member of daseinanalytisches institut, heidegger and
the earth, ed. ladelle mcwhorter, 1992, pg 19-21

D. Let us be specific about the changes that are being called for in
our relationship with the world - our encounter with care enables us to
dwell on the earth without engaging in the violence of forced
disclosure of materials as standing reserve. The plan does not
represent detachment or drawing back, rather it enacts our belonging to
earth in a manner of heedful attentiveness.

gail Stenstad, professor at east tennessee state university, heidegger
and the earth, ed. ladelle mcwhorter, 1992, pg. 71-2

E. To shrink back from the earth, to alienate ourselves from the
capacity to care – this path is conducive to a violent way of being on
the Earth. This refusal of belonging tends toward contraction into
nothing. If we are to flee from our connectedness, we will necessarily
bring death to everything earthly and alive.

gail stenstad, professor at east tennessee state university, heidegger
and the earth, ed. ladelle mcwhorter, 1992, pg 72-3

F. What effect does this talk of subjectivity have on our ability to
act ethically? The shift to an understanding of the openness of being
means that being is a radically interdependent condition, made possible
only because of my responsibility to the Other. Thus, one has a
responsibility for the Other that is incalculable and without limits.
One’s being must be affirmed in terms of a right to be in relation to
the Other.

David Campbell, International Politics, Newcastle, and Michael Dillon,
International Relations, Lancaster, The Political Subject of Violence,
1993, p. 169-171
