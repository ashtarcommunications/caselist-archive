Contention One – Attack of the Clones

 

Modern Science relies on a fundamental split in the world between itself and the realm of society and politics. Just like in Plato’s Allegory of the Cave, the Scientist-Philosopher is the privileged expert capable of interpreting the Natural world and conferring absolute knowledge of it to the ignorant masses. The fundamental operation of Modernist Science is to craft the world into a single unified totality that must be defended from differing viewpoints at any cost.

Bruno Latour, Professor in the Center for the Study of Innovation at the School of Mines in Paris, 2004, Politics of Nature, p. 10-12

 

The goal of Modern Science is to subvert debate over the constitution of the common world and enforce its own totalizing vision. The myth of the Cave holds that there is one absolutely universal and pre-given common world that only Science has the capacity to access and translate to the ignorant masses. This makes all democracy impossible.

Bruno Latour, Professor in the Center for the Study of Innovation at the School of Mines in Paris, 2004, Politics of Nature, p. 12-14

 

Modernity’s totalizing vision proceeded uncontested, always marching boldly on to a radiant future. This vision that unified all humanity is devoid of meaning and its boundaries must be violently policed. This is the metaphysical foundation of all war in modernity.

Bruno Latour, Professor in the Center for the Study of Innovation at the School of Mines in Paris, 2002, War of the Worlds: What About Peace? p. 10-12

 

The ultimate result of this system of Modernist Science is the quite literal elimination of everything, the ultimate destruction of the entire world.

Bruno Latour, Professor in the Center for the Study of Innovation at the School of Mines in Paris, 2004, Politics of Nature, p. 188-90

 

Thus, the United States Federal Government should ratify the Kyoto Protocol and implement by mandating a reduction in total non-governmental consumption of fossil fuels to 7% below 1990 greenhouse gas emissions levels.

 

Contention Two: A New Hope

 

The Kyoto Protocol embodies a new vision of political ecology – in the Kyoto negotiations, it is impossible for any of the parties to refer to a transcendent, unified nature that structures all reality. The negotiations are an instance of parties with disparate interests coming together to democratically define the constitution of our common world. We affirm Kyoto as this instance, one that unifies (dissolves) the long-divided spheres of politics and nature into one collective, capable of assembling a democratic common world. A new politics is just beginning.

Bruno Latour, Professor in the Center for the Study of Innovation at the School of Mines in Paris, 2004, Politics of Nature, p. 55-57

 

Political ecology must address the problematic of the ability to speak in its deliberations over the common world, and it does so through the figure of the spokesperson, which refuses to differentiate whether the party speaking is human, nonhuman, or represents any particular interest. The Kyoto negotiations embody political ecology practiced by diplomatic spokespersons – each of the parties must recognize all the others as spokespeople, allowing for a profound modification in our normal conception of political discussion. In political ecology, deliberative speech is no longer a solely human property – the collective of humans and non-humans is truly democratized.

Bruno Latour, Professor in the Center for the Study of Innovation at the School of Mines in Paris, 2004, Politics of Nature, p. 64-66

 

Our vision for a world apart from the Modernist Constitution is known as the collective – a society that makes impossible Modernity’s division of the world into the realms of the Natural/Scientific and the Political/Societal. This literally disinvents modernism and makes its perpetual conflicts impossible. Now is the key time – failing to engage political ecology in the current moment risks replicating the whole of modern ecology’s mistakes.

Bruno Latour, Professor in the Center for the Study of Innovation at the School of Mines in Paris, 2004, Politics of Nature, p. 193-94

 

Ecological crises force and necessitate a new political ecology – contemporary crises of the natural world escape rationalization by modernist Science that divides human politics from the administration of nature. Climate change questions our ability to control and hierarchalize the actors and values associated with nature. This is our chance for a truly democratic conception of the natural world.

Bruno Latour, Professor in the Center for the Study of Innovation at the School of Mines in Paris, 2004, Politics of Nature, p. 23-25

 

To end the vast and perpetual conflicts of modernity, one change is needed in our conception of the common world – instead of battles to enforce one common world defined in advance, the battle must be about the makeup of that very common world and the outcome must be uncertain. That’s enough to change literally everything. A focus on climate change is the perfect place to start this process because it replaces the certainty of modern ecology with precaution and vigilance over the construction of the common world.

Bruno Latour, Professor in the Center for the Study of Innovation at the School of Mines in Paris, 2002, War of the Worlds: What About Peace? p. 32-34
