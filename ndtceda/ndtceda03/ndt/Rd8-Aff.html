Our framework for the debate is that the affirmative’s advocacy should be evaluated as if it was a doctoral thesis while the judges function as the advisory review board.  That means that if we, as the negative, can demonstrate a flaw in the affirmative’s method, it’s a reason for the affirmative to be rejected and sent back for revision.  We think the negative should have one unconditional advocacy throughout the debate to preserve reciprocity and prevent regression into insignificant metaphorical tests.  Any additional advocacy adopted by the affirmative after the 1ac would not be part of their submission to the board and are not demonstrations of the resolution as it applies to the negative advocacy.  Framing debate this way emphasizes its value as an academic activity.  We think that is the best approach for several reasons.

 

First, debate is a training ground for the rest of the world.  Since debaters enter into many disparate fields after debate, they need to be trained for the broadest set of arguments.  The colleges that send us to these debates advocate general education classes to ensure a broad curriculum, and we should try to replicate that standard for academic thought. 

 

Also, it is the best way to develop advocacy skills.  Doctoral theses are evaluated in front of a panel and put up against many types of criticism because it has been proven to be the most effective way to test the validity and soundness of the thesis.  Again, we should try to copy the already proven model.

 

Furthermore, it guarantees argumentative consistency from each team.  When both sides have only one consistent advocacy, the affirmative is forced to defend their selected section of the resolution as a good idea, ensuring the most germane education and topic specific understanding.

 

Also, the framework is predictable, because it allows the affirmative lots of strategic latitude on how to affirm the resolution.  We don’t enforce fiat or non-fiat, or any particular interpretation of the Federal Government.  Similarly, they should be ready to defend the entirety of their advocacy and what went into it.

 

Additionally, it allows for diverse debates, as each negative can test the thesis in a different way.  They are sure to have policy, fiat type debates, rhetoric debates, method debates, and others.  That framework provides for the most comprehensive education by providing depth within the debate and breadth throughout the year.

 

Finally, the academic emphasis is particularly important in a debate like this.  The circumstances for this debate subsume other normative interpretations of what debate should be because we are in a non-break round at the end of the year.  The notion of debate as a game is preserved within our framework.  More importantly, the idea that debate is fundamentally based on competition isn’t true in this instance where the competitive carrot to win the tournament is no longer within reach.  At the end of the year, we are no longer building toward some other end, and it’s most important for the judge to vote for the most academically rewarding framework. 

 

A  Introduction

            In Anti-Oedipus, Gilles Deleuze and Felix Guattari provide what Foucault calls “an Introduction to a Non-Fascist Life,”[i] outlining how society, currently modeled on Oedipus and repression, can be rejected in favor of the “schizoid revolutionary pole.”[ii]  We will provide a sketch of this system, demonstrate how the affirmative advocacy furthers the totalizing effect of repression of desire, demonstrate how the affirmative’s participation in destructive system of capitalism furthers the system, and advocate the revolutionary potential contained in schizophrenic breaks with the destructive system.  Ultimately, we will relate our arguments to the role of the judge, conclude that a revolutionary break with capitalism is needed in order to prevent the destruction of all freedom, and argue that such a break is inconsistent with the affirmative advocacy.

B  Summary of Framework

            Deleuze and Guattari argue that what we commonly think of as individual people are “desiring machines”: we are selves that want certain things and seek to fulfill our desires with our actions.  Desiring-machines are molecular in that desire is individual.  Society organizes desiring-machines into a social aggregate: desiring machines exist as part of society and what is desired is determined by how individuals are organized as part of society.  The social aggregate is molar, rather than molecular; society is constituted by many individual desiring machines.

            The actions that molecular and molar machines take are constituted by flows: every change in the position of machines is a flow: buying a book in a capitalist bookstore so is blowing up a police car in resistance to capitalism.  Capitalism organizes flows and desiring-machines into controllable aggregates.  This is done by appealing to the fascist in all of us: our desire to be led.  Capitalism forces an unconscious investment of desire into the paranoic, fascist pole of social libidinal investment, determining what desire is and organizes it as part of a social order that explains everything and adopts a materialist metaphysics, excluding free choice.  Additionally, the system of capitalist representation intertwines lack and desire, defining desire in terms of lack, and determining desire to be a want for something that one does not have rather than a process that is occurring in the present.  Desire is also, however invested in a schizoid revolutionary pole: desiring machines do not only have an internal desire for fascism, they also seek freedom; desire is invested in seeking choice.

            Capitalism continually seeks to expand its hold on the world.  As a result of its very desire to explain all of reality and to include all nations in a market economy, it seeks to expand until the periphery of the capitalist system (Iraq) and the center of the system (the USA, Germany, etc.) are bound to each other.  Capitalism also continually gains greater control over desiring-machines that are already part of its system (treating mental illness).  Thus capitalism gains control over new territories not presently included in the system and simultaneously gains greater control over territories that are already in the system.

            Capitalism expands by engaging in a continual process of deterritorialization and reterritorialization.  “The modern immanent machine…consists in decoding the flows on the full-body of capital-money.”[iii]  Capitalism continually engages in production for the sake of production: production with out limit.  Thus, capitalism decodes the flows of society and removes restrictions on what people are permitted to do.  Simultaneously, capitalism “can only do so in the framework of its own limited purpose, as a determinate mode of production.”[iv]  The mantra of modern capitalism becomes: “yes, anyone may play our game, but they must agree to play by our rules.”

            This control of the social order by capitalism is itself violent and leads to the replication of oppression on a broader scale: the desire to be told what to do let Hitler come to power.  As capitalism increases its explanatory power, “final solutions of the ‘Jewish problem’ variety are handed down.”[v]  Deleuze and Guattari argue that “the factories…do not resemble prisons, they are prisons.”[vi]  Capitalism really is enslaving in that it prevents free choice to escape the molar aggregate which it controls.

            Fortunately, the possibility remains for a radical break with the capitalist system: schizoanalysis is the practice or replacing the neurotic as model of the psychoanalytic system with the schizophrenic.  Breaking with the system and refusing to participate in capitalism means that desiring-machines do not locate the objects of their desire within the molar aggregate as determined by capitalism.  “Those exploited become conscious of their preconscious interest, and that interest is truly revolutionary—a major break from the standpoint of the preconscious.”[vii]  The nomadic war machine is the creative, multifaceted opposition to capital—this opposition is not fixed, limited, or dependent on lack.

C  Thesis

            Affirmative’s advocacy contributes to the system of control that emerges from capitalism, statism, and psychoanalysis.  By appealing to the United Nations and Europe to fix the problems in Iraq, connecting lack and desire in their attempt to fix the problems of the status quo, and participating in despotic explanations of territory provided by capitalism, the affirmative permits capitalism to expand its hold over the world and forecloses the possibility of revolutionary investments of desire.  This participation in the capitalist system not only furthers the destructive fascist logic of control but also destroys the possibility of individual choice and eliminates the possibility of value.  Schizoanalysis is needed to allow revolutionary investments of desire and the nomadic war machine is needed to make use of this revolutionary investment and break free from fascism.  The ballot endorses our imaginative, creative act of resistance through the nomadic war machine and schizoanalysis.
