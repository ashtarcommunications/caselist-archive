Tragedy K

This year’s resolution has brought to light the ongoing tragedy of agriculture in America. We have considered the economics and the ecology of farming. We have seen the international and the domestic politics of this ancient institution, and, as the 1AC made clear, it is time for our national investment in the practice to end.


Yet, we cannot help but feel that something important is missing from this discussion. The farmers themselves are deeply embroiled in these issues and yet they themselves are absent. Thus, we begin our side of the debate with one farmer’s desire to leave his mark:

Hanson is a pessimist. Rather than give up the fields and chase his dreams, he chose fields without dreams. In those fields he found no comfort, progress, salvation, or happiness, but unadulterated joy against a society who has forgotten it.

Joyful pessimism is what the affirmative misses, and it is the central figure of our telling of the tragedy of American agriculture. We affirm the doomed farmer against optimism, against progress, against the bland re-selling of free markets and liberal politics. And pessimism is precisely what makes stories like Hanson’s matter:

Hanson’s story reminds us that western civilization began in the fields, where the first farmers affirmed their difference from nature and created themselves as political animals. Out of the fields also came the dionysia, the mad celebration of the struggle with nature. The dionysia came into the cities in the form of pessimistic tragedy where the horror of this struggle with nature found artistic expression.


This was a time of heroes and gods who became great through struggle and through the representation of that struggle.

When Greek civilization gave up on the agrarian struggle with nature and instead valued the philosophy and the reason of the cities, it came under the delusion that civilization had won and that the world could be molded into ever-more perfect forms. This delusion is reflected in tragedy, where the mad Dionysian chorus gave way to descriptive and moralistic commentary.


This is a debate about tragedy, and the affirmative’s optimism is a bland script. Optimism reeks of the old Socratic lie equating Truth with virtue. Rather, we contend that Truth gives us hollow virtues and worse art. This is how Socrates poisoned Greek Tragedy and continues to poison the agricultural tragedy of the 1AC.

Life is essentially chaos, and chaos entails suffering. It is a repulsive folly to blame suffering on anything in particular, for this reactivity can only end in blaming life, collapsing into a nihilistic will to nothingness.
