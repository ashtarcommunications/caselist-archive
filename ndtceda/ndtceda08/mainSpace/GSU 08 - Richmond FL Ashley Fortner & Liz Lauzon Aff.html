Contention 2: Dis-membering

CAFOs are the embodiment of Agamben’s “camp.” The camp—based on the concentration camp—is literal and metaphorical. This camp allows exclusions and exterminations to proceed as normal operations. This is the ultimate expression of the sovereign’s power to create exceptions of “necessary animal suffering.” These camps are thinly disguised by the need “to make food,” when really they are unique places where animal life is held within a zone of sovereign exception. The camp, then, spills over to manipulate all politics---all living beings are now seen as objects to be ruthlessly surveyed.
Wadiwel, 2 (Dinesh Joseph, doctorate at the University of Western Sydney, “Cows and Sovereignty: Biopower and Animal Life,” Borderlands E-Journal, volume 1, number 2, www.borderlandsejournal.adelaide.edu.au/vol1no2_2002/wadiwel_cows.html)

“8. It is in this sense… the powers of the sovereign.”


Rejection of any complicity with the concentration of bare life in the form of the “animal death camp” is paramount. The link to the state of exception and bare life is inextricable. Non-human animals are held at the edge of life—prior to, but worse than, death—in order to maximize efficiency and profit. The calf is but one example.
Wadiwel, 2 (Dinesh Joseph, doctorate at the University of Western Sydney, “Cows and Sovereignty: Biopower and Animal Life,” Borderlands E-Journal, volume 1, number 2, www.borderlandsejournal.adelaide.edu.au/vol1no2_2002/wadiwel_cows.html)

“For it may be… borders upon death itself.”


Re-configuring the relationship between humans and non-humans is a pre-requisite to any challenge to violence or sovereignty itself.
The human-divide does not need to be closed, but its existence needs to be opened and exposed for any politics outside total extermination.
Extinction is nothing as an impact or a concept in light of the way the meaning of human and non-human animal solidifies in the practices of factory farming. The very destiny of the planet—all humans and all animals—is caught up in the bio-politics of this intersection: there can be no consideration of greater magnitude.
Wadiwel, 2 (Dinesh Joseph, doctorate at the University of Western Sydney, “Cows and Sovereignty: Biopower and Animal Life,” Borderlands E-Journal, volume 1, number 2, www.borderlandsejournal.adelaide.edu.au/vol1no2_2002/wadiwel_cows.html)

“14. Digging deeper into… the gap in its entirety.”


This anthropological machine lies at the root of atrocities such as the holocaust, in which the dehumanization and violence toward others becomes possible through the distinction between human and sub-human.
Pick, 6 (Anat, University of East London, Review of “Giorgio Agamben, The Open: Man and Animal,” Bryn Mawr Review of Comparative Literature, volume 5, number 2, winter, http://www.brynmawr.edu/bmrcl/Winter2006/Agamben.htm)

“Levi's unflinching account… identity of the human.”


Thus the plan: The United States Federal Government should substantially reduce its agricultural support for Concentrated Animal Feeding Operations, at least eliminating nearly all of the domestic subsidies for Concentrated Animal Feeding Operations.


Contention 3: Re-membering

Our prioritizing of the animal body as a commodity that should not be subsidized by the state is an important political gesture. Our intervention can spark a active dialectic between humans and animals. The way out of Agamben’s inadvertent erasure of the animal-other as well as the status quo’s annihilation of the animal-other is an active dialectic based on the responsibilities of both language and silence. The entire notion of “impact” is swallowed by the magnitude of the anthropocentric machine.
Young, 6 (Katherine, UC Berkeley, Ph.D. Candidate Political Science Department Colorado State University, “Animal Politics: Sovereignty and the Animal Body,” Annual Meeting of the Western Political Science Association Albuquerque, New Mexico March 16-18, 2006, http://www.allacademic.com//meta/p_mla_apa_research_citation/0/9/7/2/6/pages97264/p97264-1.php)

“An alternative reading of… make the unknown knowable.”


The slaughtering of animals continues to be ignored because people place it out of sight, and out of mind. Current political discussions focus on the state as the starting place for change and ask questions like “what is the appropriate legislative response?” However, we must reach beyond this boundary and understand the intertwining of the animal and human to allow for possible solutions to present themselves.
Wadiwel, 2 (Dinesh Joseph, doctorate at the University of Western Sydney, “Cows and Sovereignty: Biopower and Animal Life,” Borderlands E-Journal, volume 1, number 2, www.borderlandsejournal.adelaide.edu.au/vol1no2_2002/wadiwel_cows.html)

“But such a political… shared by both entities.”


Bringing these issues to the minds of the people in the room is necessary to understand that what we eat constitutes a political decision that can confront sovereign domination.
Pollan, 6 [Michael, Professor of Journalism at UC Berkeley’s Graduate School of Journalism, "Our National Eating Disorder," in The Omnivore's Dilemma: A Natural History of Four Meals, pg. 9-11.]

“Yet as different as… only deepened by knowing.”


Challenging Agamben’s theory of the animal by enacting advocacy through politics can also rupture the political. Thus, the framework of our discourse in terms of the human-animal dialectic comes before predictive assessments of the consequences of withdrawing state support.
Young, 6 (UC Berkeley, ‘6 Katherine, Ph.D. Candidate Political Science Department Colorado State University, “Animal Politics: Sovereignty and the Animal Body,” Annual Meeting of the Western Political Science Association Albuquerque, New Mexico March 16-18, 2006, http://www.allacademic.com//meta/p_mla_apa_research_citation/0/9/7/2/6/pages97264/p97264-1.php)
