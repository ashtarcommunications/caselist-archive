I begin with our first main point: A Justification to Consider an Ethical System of Policy Debate.
Former political scientist and University President, W. Robert Parks wrote in a 1955 article entitled the “Goals of Democracy” that there is only ONE ultimate objective for a democracy. He notes that,
Democracy also has certain high-level principles which may be changeless. The American political process involves many mechanical devices for implementing these principles which, through traditional association, we may come to consider as democracy itself. But such mechanical apparatuses as a federal system, a two-house legislature, a separation of powers scheme are all simply means chosen from among several alternatives for achieving democratic ends. As means, they have no inherent sanctity and must always be subjected to the pragmatic test of utility.
If alternative mechanical apparatuses can be shown to offer more promise for achieving democratic goals and subgoals, it is not only permissible but mandatory, under the democratic theory, that the alternative devices be adopted in order that the end of democracy-the well-being of the individual-be better served.

The first affirmative speech is the beginning of a policy debate and that is what we must subject to the pragmatic test of utility. The goal of a policy debate in a democratic society should be to create, implement, and evaluate effective decision making for a multicultural democracy. We have concerns about how a democratic society like America and the debate community protect the well-being of minority interests against broader majority concerns.

_ and I believe we must examine three areas that directly impact the policy making process: how we create policies, how we implement policies, and how we evaluate policies. Only by examining how the policy debate addresses these areas can America understand how to make better policies for all of its constituents.

I now advocate our second main point: An Ethical Policy Creation Process.

Louisville’s advocacy in today’s debate begins with an acknowledgement of our decision not to create a policy argument strategy regarding the elimination of _ subsidies.

The creation of effective policy requires the establishment of an ethical relationship between the constituent who claims they have been harmed, the advocate who speaks on behalf of said constituent, and the policy maker who evaluates evidence and testimony for the purpose of creating policy. The constituent’s sole responsibility is to speak out about the injustices that have been done to them with supporting evidence for their position. Our responsibility as advocates begins with an acknowledgement that we, not they, have the opportunity to speak to you today, and we must use our agency by advocating policy interests that they have created for themselves. The policy maker should interrogate the privilege of testimony and evidence to create the best policy option for those with lesser privileged status.

I now turn to our third main point: An Ethical Policy Implementation Process.

Louisville also acknowledges our decision to not advocate a policy implementation strategy for the policy we chose not to create regarding the elimination of _ subsidies.
The policy implementation process should require the establishment of an ethical relationship between all impacted constituents, their advocates, and those with the power to implement policy. The constituent must speak to the nature of their fundamental concerns. We, as advocates must advocate an unconditional policy based on what we are persuaded to be true from our constituents. Those in power must judge any competing claims to create the best policy option that best serves the needs of the greatest number of people. In making that determination, the decision maker should never impose a compromise that ignores the needs of a lesser privileged constituency group. Moral and ethical leadership requires that the policy maker interrogate the possible consequences to those with more privilege to ensure the implementation of an ethical policy regarding those saddled with less privilege.

The affirmative decision to exclude the NBFA as part of their constituency group is unethical with regards to policy implementation. Just because members of the NBFA won’t directly benefit from the affirmative advantages, doesn’t mean they should be politically ignored, nor does it mean the affirmative has to ignore “their” best interest. Given the outstanding debt owed regarding subsidies by the USDA to these farmers, that should still merit your consideration as a policy maker interested in the well-being of all Americans. The Environmental Working group issued a report in 2004 explaining the legacy of discrimination:


In this historic civil rights case, known as Pigford v. Veneman, USDA promised to pay billions to African American farmers who claimed that the USDA had systematically discriminated against them for decades, denying them access to essential crop loans that were made readily available to "similarly situated" white farmers in their communities. The settlement was largely based on USDA's own admission of discrimination in its 1997 civil rights study, and the Reagan Administration elimination of the USDA's Office of Civil Rights in 1983, effectively denying African American farmers any recourse for claims of discrimination from 1983 through 1996, when the Office was reestablished. In part due to lack of equal access to USDA loans, the number of farms operated by African Americans has declined dramatically over the past 20 years, plummeting from 54,367 in 1982 to just 29,090 in 2002.
