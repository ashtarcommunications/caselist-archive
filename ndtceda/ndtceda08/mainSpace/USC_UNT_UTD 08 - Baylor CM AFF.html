If the sacred is that which is removed from common use, our political response must be that of profanation. To profane is to return that which is sacred to the free and common use of humanity.
If we are to approach the topic in a meaningful, life affirming way, we must wrest the resolution away from the apparatuses of power that negate our experience by orienting us around a false notion of progress. Capitalist alienation operates by putting things to work, by preserving the sacred economic order. We choose to look for a new use for the resolution, a profane experience of what it means to affirm the topic.

We affirm the resolution through the act of play. Play happens when any sacred object is used towards profane ends, as an object of fancy and negligence. Like a cat who replaces a ball of yarn for the mouse, or a child playing with an old religious relic, we engage in the form of debate’s political ritual without any of its sacred intent. We refuse to cast a decision on the resolution’s relationship to spectacular political events. We see in the debate game’s call for affirmative role playing a chance for playful symbolic inversion. When confronted with the metaphor of the state as community decision making, we respond with an equally metaphorical investigation of how our community experiences agriculture. We, who are supposed to role play as policymakers, choose to confront the way that we personally subsidize agriculture.
