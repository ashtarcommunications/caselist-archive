Hanson’s story reminds us that western civilization began in the fields, where the first farmers affirmed their difference from nature and created themselves as political animals. Out of the fields also came the dionysia, the mad celebration of the struggle with nature. The dionysia came into the cities in the form of pessimistic tragedy where the horror of this struggle with nature found artistic expression. This was a time of heroes and gods who became great through struggle and through the representation of that struggle. When Greek civilization gave up on the agrarian struggle with nature and instead valued the philosophy and the reason of the cities, it came under the delusion that civilization had won and that the world could be molded into ever-more perfect forms. This delusion is reflected in tragedy, where the mad Dionysian chorus gave way to descriptive and moralistic commentary. This is a debate about tragedy, and the affirmative’s optimism is a bland script. Optimism reeks of the old Socratic lie equating Truth with virtue. Rather, we contend that Truth gives us hollow virtues and worse art. This is how Socrates poisoned Greek Tragedy and continues to poison the agricultural tragedy of the 1AC. Life is essentially chaos, and chaos entails suffering. It is a repulsive folly to blame suffering on anything in particular, for this reactivity can only end in blaming life, collapsing into a nihilistic will to nothingness.
