Contention One – Legal Exceptionalism

In 1942, the U.S. government executed six German citizens who President Roosevelt had designated as ‘enemy combatants’. Only after the executions did the Supreme Court release its decision in Ex Parte Quirin, as the result of executive coercion. Sixty years later, Quirin’s act of judicial fiat has been resurrected from the history books and employed as the justification for the detention of so-called ‘enemy combatants’
Jesselyn A. Radack, J.D., Yale Law School, Founder and Executive Director of the Coalition for Civil Rights and Democratic Liberties, 2005, Review of Law and Social Change, 29 N.Y.U. Rev. L. & Soc. Change 525, p. 531-536




The Supreme Court has played an active role in allowing the executive to declare anyone an enemy combatant, and deny them legal standing. This establishes a two-tiered legal system, where people can be detained indefinitely and tortured under the auspices of law.
Amy Kaplan - professor of English & in the Humanities @ U. of Pennsylvania, former president of the American Studies Association - 2005 American Quarterly 57.3 831-858 “Where Is Guantánamo?” [nfb]



The ‘enemy combatants’ designation lies at the heart of the security state’s power over life and death. To wage global war on behalf of everyone, those declared to be ‘illegal’ combatants are removed not only from the field of the political, but from the very field of the human.
Bulent Diken, Senior Lecturer in Sociology at Lancaster University, 2006, Irish Journal of Sociology, Vol. 15, No. 1, p. 84-86
Treating suspected terrorists as a threat beyond the law creates never-ending war though an us-them mindset that divides the world into enemies we must destroy at all costs, this war will not end before the total annihilation of the globe.
Gil Anidjar – Ass't Professor, Middle East and Asian Languages & Cultures @ Columbia - 2004
CR: The New Centennial Review 4.3 35-69 “Terror Right” Muse [nfb]



Furthermore, the authority to designate people as ‘enemy combatants’ has pernicious effects on all of us, not just those unfortunate enough to be detained at Guantanamo Bay. The Bush administration’s prosecution of the ‘war on terror’ has held all of us as a captive audience, and the American people’s acquiescence has allowed all politics to be held hostage by the imperatives of “homeland security”.
Donald Pease, Avalon Foundation Chair of the Humanities at Dartmouth College, 2003, boundary 2, Vol. 30, No. 3, p. 14-17


This apolitical state of affairs, with its attendant foundation of enemy-creation and catastrophe management, turns life in the ‘war on terror’ into nothing more than waiting for the next catastrophe to happen. Unfortunately, the ultimate catastrophe is this way of living, in permanent fear and in permanent need of the security state’s salvation.
Bulent Diken, Senior Lecturer in Sociology at Lancaster University, 2006, Irish Journal of Sociology, Vol. 15, No. 1, p. 83-84


This endless focus on survival above all else removes all value from life
Slavoj Zizek, Professor of Sociology at the Institute for Sociology, Ljubljana University, 2003, The Puppet and the Dwarf: The Perverse Core of Christianity, p. 94-95

Instead, we must refuse to murder the other for our own security – this imperative is the basis for non-violent solutions that impel connection, not destruction.
Spoma Jovanovic (Department of Communication, University of North Carolina) and Roy V. Wood (Human Communication Studies, University of Denver) 2004 Philosophy and Rhetoric 37.4 317-334 “Speaking from the Bedrock of Ethics” [nfb]
