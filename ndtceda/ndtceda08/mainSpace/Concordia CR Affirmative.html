We begin with the historical development of the resolution.
We started out thinking constructive engagement meant X some means of spreading neoliberalism, based on conditions. Tit for tat. But then we found that this seemed incompatible with the term financial assistance, which is unconditioned aid. Clearly, it was difficult to create an affirmative that would meet the burdens of such a contradictory resolution. Then we discovered that these contradictory, static terms of art are actually fluid, as admitted in a New York Times ’88 (“U.S. Official Nears Elusive Triumph: An Intricate Peace in Southern Africa” p. online) article. Crocker’s catch phrase can mean anything:
“But for Chester Arthur Crocker, […]he couldn't take it back.''


Both for semantic reasons and the words themselves, the resolution doesn’t have any meaning until we give it meaning ourselves. Example: [conditions but not necessarily conditions]. We don’t care if we have to be conditional or unconditional, we’re saying you are trying to solidify what foreign assistance is makes it universal [T is neg being Crocker]. Language is fluid. We’re not saying let the rez wash over us. We’re saying it already did and we have half a year left.
