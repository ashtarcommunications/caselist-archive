Framework:
1) Our Affirmative is a criticism of discourse exercised in the Milliken decision that re-entrenches state power, upholds the idea of the law and the constitution as supreme standards of justice, all in order to maintain the paradigm of the dominant socio-economic, and political class.

2) Milliken decision reaffirms the idea of the law as a unique standard of justice refusing to analyze the source of its authority. Law’s authority is nowhere outside its linguistic boundaries; its power is derived from our blind, non-critical acceptance of it.

3) The affirmative advocacy is deconstruction of the dominant legal, political, and socio-economic paradigm that must precede any form of action.

4) We must acknowledge the hierarchical social system before we can achieve equality and justice

Contention 1: Constitution Methodology

The construction of the Constitution as a supreme standard of justice was one of the assumptions in the Milliken decision. This belief in justice as contained in some eternal principles that can be discovered rationally and then applied for centuries without an end is simply a myth of the Enlightenment period. The problem with this approach is that those in power can wash their hands of the injustices that are not prohibited explicitly by the Constitution. The Constitution references many important values, but viewing it as an exhaustive set of principles is flawed and can lead to absurd distinctions such as the one in Milliken between constitutional and “unconstitutional radically discriminatory acts.” They should just be seen as wrong, period. To assume that there is no justice outside that principle is to make injustice permissible in a new situation. We should change the fundamental laws to fight racism, not allow racism in order to be able to keep society’s fundamental laws.

Faith in this constitutional mythology makes us ignore the realities of our social issues.
Reynolds 94 (“Deconstructing State Action: The Politics of State Action,” Ohio Northern University Law Review, 20 Ohio N.U.L. Rev. 847)
“Faith in our constitutional…historical pursuits.”

Contention 2: The state

1) Seeing “The State” as an entity is a trap and an excuse for discrimination.
Reynolds 94 (“Deconstructing State Action: The Politics of State Action,” Ohio Northern University Law Review, 20 Ohio N.U.L. Rev. 847)
“The line between… federal powers.”

2) The District Court that reviewed Milliken before the case reached the Supreme Court noticed precisely this point and I quote:
“While it would be unfair… Ibid.”

This is due to the flawed conception of the state as an entity. In reality, the bureaucratic apparatus is a collection of actors between which the responsibility is divided, subdivided and sub-sub-divided until no one is responsible for the holistic outcome anymore. The different decision-makers can therefore hide under the umbrella of the notion of “the state” and escape individual responsibility.

Also,
Subpoint 2(b) in the Milliken Holding highlights the state’s bias towards maintaining the status quo and avoiding administrative inconveniences and I quote:
“(2) thus the remedy… and administration.”

The idea that changes to the system would cause serious disruptions in the activity of the state begs the question of whether changes would be beneficial for society. A smooth-functioning and well-oiled state machine is meaningless if the society at large struggles with huge problems. The state should not exist for the sake and well-functioning of the state, but for the collective good. Justice White criticizes this state bias in his dissent opinion:
“The core of my disagreement… to the State.”
