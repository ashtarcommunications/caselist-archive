We believe debate should open up the possibility for liberation strategies from oppression

Currently our community privileges a system of alienated politics through a certain type of knowledge and a very specific limited set of perspectives. The people who need debate the most, who are marginalized and discriminated the most in their everyday lives are further systematically excluded from participating in debate. Our community fails to create a libratory education that can facilitate a process of freedom as explained in Simone DeBeauvior’s Ethics of ambiguity

In debate, education has become highly specialized and restrictive through the rules, technicalities, speed and the elevation of one form of politics. Therefore, education has become existentially alienated, as described by Paul Freire
Freire 1970 – Pedagogy of the oppressed chapter 2

Alienated Politics is not true liberation, but rather becomes a way to impose ideologies, like alienated politics of the Supreme Court or the specialized form of debate. This is devoid of people’s capacity to act and think. External solutions force persons into the passivity, stifling social transformation
Dale 2003 – “Freire, Aristotle, Marx, and Sarte: A Critique of the Human Condition” Project Muse p. 1

The system of alienated politics creates an existential crisis that disregards ambiguity. This type of education takes away from true revolutionary communication and produces a politics of Sub-Humanity, where we are blind of the dangers of our ideology and pedagogical misunderstandings. Simply, the affirmative is a reject of this “other’s” personal ethics/freedom, which is the first step to annihilation
DeBeauvoir 1947 – “Personal Freedom and Others” the Ethics of Ambiguity p. 35-73

Consequently, we believe that we need to end our dependence on the framework advanced by the affirmative. This takes form in a deeper understanding of concrete existential reality. Therefore, we call for a re-configuration of pedagogy characterized by a radical change in the education methods within our debate community and society. This takes form in dialectical mutual responsibility with ourselves and oppressing groups, and a way of coming to grips with unconditional freedom within our social locations. The starting point of the affirmative cannot merely capture our approach; otherwise, it becomes a prescribed vision into the oppressed
Slattery and Moms 1999 – “Simone De Beauvoir’s ethics and postmodern ambiguity: the assertion of freedom in the face of the absurd” Educated theory Winter, vol 49 num 1
