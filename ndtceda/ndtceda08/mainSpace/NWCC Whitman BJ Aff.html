Intro and Thesis
Much of the literature and most of this year’s debates concerning agricultural policy depoliticize and dehistoricize the emergence of its specific form of geopolitical discourse. A history of the sugar industry in the United States in particular shows that, far from being an objective fact in the world, sugar and the discourses that surround its production write and in turn are written upon by specific political ideologies and operations. In the brief history we present, we ask you to note how the sugar industry has continually justified its existence through the creation of a national security ideology which has created its own dangers and impacts, in the process requiring massive government support. We ask you to see the markets that have bubbled up, evolved and morphed over time to be constructed and fluid. We will argue that the United States Federal government, far from being an entity that we interact with passively, is infinitely available to us at our localities and that we can access its symbolism to perform a deconstructive genealogy of sugar subsidies. We should engage in this process as a way to challenge discourses of security and rationalism.


The US sugar kingdom began getting a foothold in the nineteenth century by generating a discursive contrast to productive sugar markets in Cuba that continued to use slave labor. The U.S. response to secure sugar was imperialist colonization of Hawaii, Puerto Rico and the Philippines as well as other military conquests throughout the Pacific and Caribbean. Plan
Plan: The United States federal government should eliminate at least nearly all agricultural support including at least all subsidies for sugar.



The sugar industry in the United States exists as it does today because of neoliberal discourses of national security. By deconstructing geopolitics in this way it becomes possible to see how similar practices have shaped other commodities and imagine new possibilities for politics. An aff ballot would reject securitization by recognizing it as a discursive construct that enables infinite violence.
