Contention One: The Affect of Fear

First, December 3rd, 2007: “If there was ever a possibility that President George W. Bush would drop bombs on Iran, the chances have now shrunk to nearly zero.”1

Now, March 3rd, 2008: “Suddenly, the confrontation with Iran had reignited.”2

In less than three months, media sentiment on Iran had swung from war on, to war off, to war on again. Between December’s U.S. National Intelligence Estimate and March’s IAEA report, the facts may have changed but the climate of information had not. Information has evacuated its relationship to fact and persuasion in favor of an affective function, shaping the sentimental structures that discipline our bodies, causing them to react to information before it even reaches the level of thought.

This is why George W. Bush could last week proclaim about Iran that “[t]hey’ve declared they want to have a nuclear weapon to destroy people,” even though a leading expert claims that “That’s as uninformed as McCain's statement that Iran is training al-Qaeda.”3 In a society permeated by the affect of fear, complex webs of meaning are inexorably reduced to a single, emotional signified: THREAT. It is this paranoia that sows the seeds for a new wave of fascism.

Massumi, professor of Communication at Université de Montréal, 2k5 [Brian, “Fear (The Spectrum Said),” Positions, 13.1, muse]

Contention Two: The Uncertainty of Otherness

In a climate of regime change, the question of Iran has always-already been answered, and that answer is WAR. In the current political field, establishing a basic symbolic relationship with others is a prior question to any possible policy. Before we can ask the question how should we engage Iran? we must determine whether or not the image we are engaging is anything more than a projection condensing all that we hate and fear under the signifier Iran: Iran the Evil, Iran the Despot and the Nazi, Iran the Terrorist, the Muslim, the racial and sexual other, Iran the Monster.

Against this affective climate of fear, we must reassert the symbolic link of communication and become resolved: that the United States Federal Government should offer to normalize relations with the government of Iran. This is not a call for diplomatic politics as usual or grand bargains, this is not the normal of normalization. Rather, it is to reassert this monstrous other in the place of the normal and thus call into question the one-sided nature of the normal itself.

Only thorugh an affirmation of symbolic connection against the affect of fear can we step out of the radical violence of the status quo and introduce a poltics of hope that deploys the possibility of dialogue against the suicidal enclosure of the world in a mirror-image of the self
