In 1941, President Franklin Delano Roosevelt delivered the following words to Congress:
“In the future days, which we seek to make secure, we look forward to a world founded upon four essential human freedoms.
(PRESENT FREEDOM OF SPEECH)
The first is freedom of speech and expression -- everywhere in the world.
(PRESENT FREEDOM OF WORSHIP)
The second is freedom of every person to worship God in his own way -- everywhere in the world.
(PRESENT FREEDOM FROM WANT)
The third is freedom from want -- which, translated into world terms, means economic understandings which will secure to every nation a healthy peacetime life for its inhabitants -- everywhere in the world.
(PRESENT FREEDOM FROM FEAR)
The fourth is freedom from fear -- which, translated into world terms, means a world-wide reduction of armaments to such a point and in such a thorough fashion that no nation will be in a position to commit an act of physical aggression against any neighbor-- anywhere in the world. 1

The speech inspired artist Norman Rockwell to produce a corresponding series of illustrations. By valorizing the universal expansion of U.S. Values “everywhere in the world”, these images helped unite the U.S. public behind the war effort.

The images of the four freedoms depicted the beauty and simplicity of idealized life in America. These artifacts helped manufacture a sense of American innocence. Rockwell’s world was a commodified utopia – with free exchange of ideas, pious neighbors, safe streets, full plates, and happy children.

Nowhere is the staging of commodified innocence as noticeable as in Rockwell’s Freedom From Want. The illustration makes clear that freedom from want for America meant the freedom for ravenous consumption. There is no want, no poverty, and no hunger in Rockwell’s picture. Rockwell’s proud American family is preparing for a huge turkey dinner. One cannot help but notice that the family members are locked in each others gaze’s, grinning in a manner that almost appears sinister. It is impossible to tell if, like the grin of a hyena, they are merely drunk off the smell of the carcass they are about to consume, or rather if they are reveling in their shared knowledge of the violent rituals hidden behind the consumerist mask. One man, however, has turned his wild eyes away from the family, and towards the viewer. His stare seems to challenge the viewer to investigate further, to find out what dark history is lurking behind the spectacle of the modern consumer lifestyle.

The Four Freedoms pictures are unique reminders of the ways in which American culture mirrored the development of the Third Reich. The resemblance between the Norman Rockwell images and Nazi agrarian propaganda is both shocking and illuminating.
(SHOW PROTECT THE HARVEST/ FREEDOM FROM FEAR)
In one image, a young well dressed Aryan saves a peasant farmer’s barn of hay from being destroyed in an accidental fire. The caption reads, “"Protect the harvest. It ensures victory!" In this image, the third reich’s freedom from fear requires the Nazi bureaucrat to protect the sacred agricultural harvest. The Rockwell painting could just as easily depict a German family sound at rest while the Fuhrer stands guard over home and harvest. 8
(SHOW BREAD IS FREEDOM/ FREEDOM FROM WANT)
Freedom from want stands in juxtaposition simplicity of the Nazi poster whose bold red text announces over a bountiful field of golden wheat that Freedom Is Bread. By asserting control over food, the party was able to secure its control over the very elements of life itself.
It is now much clearer that the values asserted in Roosevelt’s four freedoms speech were not democracy’s answer to nazism, but rather the method by which democracy mirrored the operational strategy of fascism. The United States did not replace the world order of the national socialists, it merely replicated political spectacle of the third reich in order to beat it at its own game – more effective manipulation of public opinion, deadlier weapons, better technology, a culture with universally superior values founded on agrarian idealism.

Instead of letting the Four Freedom images sit in the cultural dustbins of the U.S. waiting to be resurrected by bureaucrats and business leaders, we seize control of these commodities. Our abstract assemblage of technologically reproducible art tells us more about the power at work in farm subsidies than any rational political program ever could. We do not desire the power of either the farmer or the politician, nor does an affirmative ballot represent the actualization of any new political reality. Instead, you should think of your ballot as an artistic challenge to the fascist logic of industrialized agriculture and the cultural images that accompany it.
