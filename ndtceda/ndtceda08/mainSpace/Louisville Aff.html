Chapter One: Ethos

Who I am grants me the permission to speak against societal injustices that my black community faces. By being a black male in these days and time I am not only seen, but am also treated as a problem. A problem that our governmental officials feel they need to properly contain and control. I come from a reality where being caught dark, after dark meant that you were hustling, mugging, or thugging. Carrying a book-bag after school hours is enough of reason to get stopped walking home. I come from a reality where the police don’t protect or service the people, but only service their monthly quotas for that next promotion. Continuous oppression, marginalization, and disenfranchisement of my Black community attempted to hinder my progress of becoming a successful Blackman. But I made it. I made it through the drug game, through a broken community overcoming the crack plague, through the bullets, through the police and their fucked up policies. And since I made it through what some may call the “trap” I have the innate responsibility to protect my people from any further depredations caused and maintained by our governmental officials.

Chapter 4: Stylicality

It is our argument that debaters do not fully embrace Aristotle’s concept of persuasion – the use of ethos, pathos, and logos. We will defend that ethos, pathos, and logos is necessary for effective persuasion.
Gary ORREN, Professor of Public Policy @ the Kennedy School @ Harvard University wrote in 2000

“We live in a world of competing for our attention with a daily…blends the persuasive elements of logos, ethos, and pathos.”

Given that debate attempts to educate the participants as well as the audience we believe that a persuasive model of debate would not only be more educational but would allow for broader participation and societal advantages. It is therefore, our contention that the current style used by most debaters to articulate their arguments actually undercuts the persuasiveness of those arguments. We hold that debaters should use Pathos, Ethos, and Logos to fully articulate a persuasive argument in contrast to the current logos-centered arguments delivered by most debaters.

Evaluate the Debate, based on who wins 2 of the 3.
