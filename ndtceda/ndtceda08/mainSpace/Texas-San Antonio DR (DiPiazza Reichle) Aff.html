Camus AFF:

Why are we here? All things aside, this is the only real question, everything else is an answer that helps a little bit in the grand mystery, a way of describing and mapping the world that we live in. Abandoned, and having no way of knowing what happens when we die—we collect tid-bits of information and pretend that the factoids that we file into our minds will help us unravel the answer to our great dilemma. So, why do we continue to live in this world, this absurd and ridiculous reality, where we are destined to fail, where at our best, our life’s works are subject to being snuffed out by an unexpected death? The fact is that people leave this world, we don’t know why but they leave. Some decompose in the open air, some lay at the bottom of lakes, in trash cans, some are cremated—but the reality is that we will all die. This is nothing new. We live with a feeling in our hearts that things are not the way that we want them to be, we are all trained to believe that some day we will be movie gods or rock stars, first round debaters, CEDA champs, but we won’t—and that is the problem with reality, it seems to have a plan contrary to the way that we think life should be. Faced with the fact that our lives can end at any moment, and compounded by the fact that we are without instructions from our creator we are perpetually lost in this wacky world. And this is the Absurdity of reality. Faced with the absurd there are two options, suicide or continued existence without the need for meaningless goals, magical type thinking about other worlds, what if situations, and a refusal to hope.

Albert Camus Explains in 1957 [Albert. Nobel Prize winner for literature. The Myth of Sisyphus and other Essays. Page 12-15]

So then it is a question of the topic hail. Philip and I stand resolved that the United States Supreme Court should overrule ex parte quirin. This doesn’t mean that the Supreme Court will actually change its mind because we say so. After the events of September 11th 2001, President Bush authorized the detainment of enemy combatants at Guantanamo bay, citing the Quirin decision as precedent. Our affirmation of the resolution doesn’t come with an endorsement of every past action of the legal system, but rather this case and its discussion of the absurd cause. We aren’t members of the Supreme Court and don’t see the merit in pretending that we are. We have no problem saying that it would be nice if quirin were overruled, but we aren’t going to make believe that we can magically suspend reality for the sake of a game. We do believe that this year’s resolution, namely the quirin decision provides for an intersection with the Absurdity of life that is worth discussing. It is important to note that the information about the going on’s at Guantanamo are kept hush, and the recent “suicides” of three inmates remains a mystery, all we are told about their deaths are interpreted through military speak, as though their deaths were nothing but political… and Denbeaux and Denbeaux explain in 2006.
