Contention 1: Suffering

Concentrated Animal Feeding Operations are a living hell for billions of farm animals across America, which are viewed as nothing more than Cartesian machines incapable of feeling pain.
Pollan, 2002 (Michael; Journalism Professor at Berkeley, Director of Knight Program in Science and Environmental Journalism, and author of several books on agribusiness; November 10; The New York Times Magazine, “An Animal’s Place”)
It gets worse than just cannibalism, surgery without anesthesia, and severe confinement
Farm Sanctuary, 2008 (“Factory Farming”, http://www.farmsanctuary.org/issues/factoryfarming/, the card is the combined articles on eggs, pork, beef, dairy, and poultry)
And these animals are all capable of feeling physical and emotional pain
Sneddon, 2005 (Lynne, “Can animals feel pain?”, Lynne is in the School of Biological Sciences at the University of Liverpool, http://www.wellcome.ac.uk/en/pain/microsite/culture2.html)

Because nonhumans feel pain, preventing their suffering is an ethical imperative even if they will die regardless
McMahan, 2002 (Jeff “The Ethics of Killing: Problems at the Margin of Life”, Prof. of Phil. at Rutgers, pp. 199-202)

The suffering of sentient nonhumans is morally the same - the species barrier does not justify discrimination. Just as racism and sexism are based on irrelevant, ostensibly physical differences, so too is speciesism.
Ryder, 2000 (Richard, British experimental psychologist turned animal activist, “Animal Revolution: Changing Attitudes Towards Speciesism”, pp. 5-8)

Plan: The United States federal government should eliminate nearly all of the United States federal government’s agricultural support for concentrated animal feeding operations.

CAFOs will cease to exist if they are not subsidized by the US
Gurian-Sherman, 2008 (Doug, April, senior scientist in the USC food and enviro program, PhD from Berkeley, EPA health assessor for biotechnology and FDA advisor for food biotechnology, “CAFOs Uncovered: The Untold costs of Confined Animal Feeding Operations”, the Union of Concerned Scientists for Environment Solutions, www.ucsusa.org)

The subsidies are tax dollars fund makes us complicit in the suffering caused by factory farming
Porter, 2006 (Ana, graduate of the Master in Divinity program at UChicago, Jan. 8 “Getting Real About Food”, http://www.uua.org/spirituallife/worshipweb/sermons/submissions/8783.shtml)

We must reject our complicity in factory farming or we are no better than the Nazi doctors who turned a blind eye to the camps. Voting aff to acknowledge that we are personally responsible for the pain of hundreds of animals is the only ethical choice you can make to solve.
Jenni, 2003 (Kathie, Vol. 20 No. 3, Journal of Applied Philosophy, “Vices of Inattention”)

Ignoring our complicity in the system is the ethical separation that enables us to inflict suffering without regret, thus enabling factory farms
Emel and Wolch, 1998 (Jody and Jennifer, Geography Profs, “Witnessing the Animal Momment”, Animal Geographies, 22)
