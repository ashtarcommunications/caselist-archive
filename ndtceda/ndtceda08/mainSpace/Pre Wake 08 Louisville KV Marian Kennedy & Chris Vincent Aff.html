I begin with our first main point: A Justification to Consider an Ethical Model of Policy Making.
Former political scientist and University President, W. Robert Parks wrote in a 1955 article entitled the “Goals of Democracy” that there is only ONE ultimate objective for a democracy. He notes that,
Democracy also has certain high-level principles which may be changeless. The American political process involves many mechanical devices for implementing these principles which, through traditional association, we may come to consider as democracy itself. But such mechanical apparatuses as a federal system, a two-house legislature, a separation of powers scheme are all simply means chosen from among several alternatives for achieving democratic ends. As means, they have no inherent sanctity and must always be subjected to the pragmatic test of utility.
If alternative mechanical apparatuses can be shown to offer more promise for achieving democratic goals and subgoals, it is not only permissible but mandatory, under the democratic theory, that the alternative devices be adopted in order that the end of democracy-the well-being of the individual-be better served.

One could easily read Parks as defending the American political process to only ethically serve American constituents. However, in an age of globalization and world interdependence, where United States decision making impacts others in the world, we have to broaden our conception of where our ethical responsibility begins and ends, as well as to who. In today’s world, the United States is equally affected by the life circumstance of others. Martin Luther King’s prolific quote is truer today than ever that “injustice anywhere is injustice everywhere.” We therefore present an ethical system of policy making to assess the responsibilities in a democracy as they relate to the scope of power and privilege held by the United States on the world, as well as by the world on the United States.

_ and I believe we must examine two areas for consideration that directly impact the policy making process: how we create policies as well as how we implement them. Only by examining how the democratic process addresses both of these areas can America understand how to make better policies for all of its constituents.

I now advocate our second main point: An Ethical United States Cotton Policy Creation Process.

The creation of an effective policy requires the establishment of an ethical relationship between the constituent who claims they have been harmed, the advocate who speaks on behalf of said constituent, and the policy maker who evaluates evidence and testimony for the purpose of creating policy. The constituent’s sole responsibility is to speak out about the injustices that have been done to them with supporting evidence for their position. Our responsibility as advocates begins with an acknowledgement that we, not they, have the opportunity to speak to you today, and we must use our agency by advocating policy interests that they have created for themselves because they, not us, have actually experienced the harms which creates the policy being advocated. Finally, the ethical policy maker recognizes the importance of interrogating the privilege both of the constituent and the advocate as it relates to their testimony to create the most effective policy option for any and all affected constituents.

We begin our policy creation process by listening then deferring our advocacy to the words of the presidents of two West African countries, Mali and Burkina Faso, Presidents Touré and Compaoré, in 2003:

By AMADOU TOUMANI TOURé (Mali) AND BLAISE COMPAORé (Burkina Faso)
Published: July 11, 2003; “Your Farm Subsidies Are Strangling Us” http://query.nytimes.com/gst/fullpage.html?res=9E04EFD8103DF932A25754C0A9659C8B63&n=Top/Reference/Times%20Topics/People/B/Bush,%20George%20W.
This vital economic sector in our countries is seriously threatened by agricultural subsidies granted by rich countries to their cotton producers. According to the International Cotton Advisory Committee, cotton subsidies amounted to about $5.8 billion in the production year of 2001 to 2002, nearly equal the amount of cotton trade for this same period. Such subsidies lead to worldwide overproduction and distort cotton prices, depriving poor African countries of their only comparative advantage in international trade.
Not only is cotton crucial to our economies, it is the sole agricultural product for our countries to trade. Although African cotton is of the highest quality, our production costs are about 50 percent lower than in developed countries even though we rely on manual labor. In wealthier countries, by contrast, lower-quality cotton is produced on large mechanized farms, generating little employment and having a questionable impact on the environment. Cotton there could be replaced by other, more valuable crops.
In the period from 2001 to 2002, America's 25,000 cotton farmers received more in subsidies -- some $3 billion -- than the entire economic output of Burkina Faso, where two million people depend on cotton. Further, United States subsidies are concentrated on just 10 percent of its cotton farmers. Thus, the payments to about 2,500 relatively well-off farmers has the unintended but nevertheless real effect of impoverishing some 10 million rural poor people in West and Central Africa.
The Presidents go on to offer their solution:
By AMADOU TOUMANI TOURé AND BLAISE COMPAORé
Published: July 11, 2003; “Your Farm Subsidies Are Strangling Us”
Something has to be done. Along with the countries of Benin and Chad, we have submitted a proposal to the World Trade Organization -- which is meeting in Cancún, Mexico, in September to discuss agricultural issues -- that calls for an end to unfair subsidies granted by developed countries to their cotton producers. As an interim measure, we have also proposed that least-developed countries be granted financial compensation for lost export revenues that are due to those subsidies.
Our demand is simple: apply free trade rules not only to those products that are of interest to the rich and powerful, but also to those products where poor countries have a proven comparative advantage. We know that the world will not ignore our plea for a fair playing field. The World Trade Organization has said it is committed to addressing the problems of developing countries. The United States has convinced us that a free market economy provides the best opportunities for all members of the world community. Let us translate these principles into deeds at Cancún.

I now turn to our third main point: An Ethical United States Cotton Policy Implementation Process.

Before this subcommittee agrees to transform our testimony into a bill and move forward persuading relevant government actors to eliminate United States agricultural cotton subsidies, persuasion of several groups in this room must occur. While we recognize our advocacy will directly benefit our West African cotton producing countries, it will also have consequences for other constituency groups. In the end it will result in long term systemic benefits for everyone. The Congressional hearing begins an ongoing coalition building exercise to build political compromises that minimize negative consequences on those other constituents, so we can all enjoy the long term benefits.
This process requires the establishment of an ethical relationship between constituents, their advocates, and those with power to implement policy. The initial aggrieved constituent must convincingly demonstrate their fundamental concerns are true which should be treated as the unconditional part of any negotiating process. When we choose to represent them, we must advocate an unconditional policy of subsidy elimination on behalf of those two countries because we believe their claims to be true. While you recognize that other constituents will be negatively affected, your role as an ethical decision maker should address those consequences without compromising the fundamental needs the most aggrieved constituency group. We as ethical advocates should work with you as an ethical policy maker to find acceptable compromises for everyone involved.
This requires that those in power must recognize which constituency groups have the power and privilege to impose a compromise that ignores the needs of a lesser privileged constituency group. Moral and ethical leadership requires that the policy maker interrogate the possible consequences to those with more privilege to ensure the implementation of an ethical policy regarding those saddled with less privilege. Columbia University Nobel Prize Professor Joseph Stiglitz writes in 2006
[Joseph, professor of economics at Columbia University and recipient of the Nobel Prize in economics, “Social Justice and Global Trade”, Far East Economic Review, March, p. http://www.globalpolicy.org/socecon/trade/2006/0306stiglitzjustice.pdf]
In the most recent trade talks, for example, enormous attention has been focused on developed countries’ protection of the agricultural sectors—protections that exist because of the power of vested agricultural interests there. Such protectionism has become emblematic of the hypocrisy of the West in preaching free trade yet practicing something quite different. Some 25,000 rich American cotton farmers, reliant on government subsidies for cotton, divide among themselves some $3 billion to $4 billion a year, leading to higher production and lower prices. The damage that these subsidies wreak on some 10 million cotton farmers eking out a subsistence living in sub-Saharan Africa is enormous. Yet the United States seems willing to put the interests of 25,000 American cotton farmers above that of the global trading system and the well-being of millions in the developing world. It is understandable if those in the developing world respond with anger.
The anger is increased by America’s almost cynical attitude in “marketing” its offers. For instance, at the Hong Kong meeting, U.S. trade officials reportedly offered to eliminate import restrictions on cotton but refused to do anything about subsidies. The cotton subsidies actually allow the U.S. to export cotton. When a country can export a particular commodity, it does little good to allow imports of that commodity. America, to great fanfare, has made an offer worth essentially zero to the developing countries and berated them for not taking it up on its “generous” offer.

An ethical evaluation of policy making for the United States cotton industry requires you as the committee chair accept the ethical responsibility of this hearing to:
1) Affirm policy making valuing all types of persuasive appeals when using debate as a means to influence the democratic process.
2) Affirm policy making that avoids theoretical diversions from persuasion when using debate as a means to influence the democratic process.
3) Affirm policy making that avoids contradictory, inconsistent, or conditional argument strategies when using debate as a means to influence the democratic process.
4) Affirm policy making that values switch-side policy debate as a means of influencing the democratic process.
5) Affirm the policy that best applies the policy creation process as outlined in main point two and the advocates who better engage in the policy implementation process as outlined in main point three.
