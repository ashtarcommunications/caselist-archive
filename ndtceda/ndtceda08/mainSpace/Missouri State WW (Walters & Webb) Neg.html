Our perception of the world does not come to us innocently from the mere facts of events. Instead, something we call the real mediates this perception. The real is not the reality lurking beneath representation of the world, but instead an excess or lack introduced in the very act of representation, and indeed the very act of forming the social order. Our society lurches forward from crisis to crisis: communities in decline, poverty, nuclear weapons, environmental destruction, etc., each time implicitly believing that each crisis is a historically specific situation that requires some minor corrective course of action. This perspective misidentifies the real as a historically specific situation rather than a feature inherent in the social edifice itself. Lurking beneath every fantasy of peace, stability, and resolution is a threat of disorder and chaos. Catastrophe always hovers; such is the nature of our being.
Zizek 04, conversations with Zizek, p. 78-79
“Here I would agree … inherent, self-blockade.”

In order to protect ourselves psychologically from the traumatic effects of this ever-present Real, we have all acquiesced to the spell of ideology. Ideology works by constructing a fantasy of reality in which it is possible to overcome the inevitable crises generated by the Real. As a result, we have entered into a cycle of distance and proximity to the Real in which we wait anxiously until catastrophe confronts us as “on the brink,” and then we take some small corrective measure thereby deferring our reconciliation with catastrophe until the next time the threat emerges. This process of constant deferral prevents us from ever fundamentally reordering the world, and instead moves us toward a new world order which selectively constitutes groups of people to eliminate as barriers and risks rather than recognizing the intrinisically risky nature of the entire subjective venture.
Zizek 04 Conversations with Zizek, p. 12-3
