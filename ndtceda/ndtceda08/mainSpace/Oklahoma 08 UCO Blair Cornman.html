We read from Rosset – WTO Out of Agriculture 2006
Then read this statement, then more from the book…………

Our position in this debate is that we need to re-orient the debate about agriculture.

Now is the time to say enough is enough, too many peasant farmers are suffering while a minority group of people reap the benefits of the current agricultural policies.

We stand in solidarity with La Via Campesina, with the farmers across the globe, that we need to change the framework by which current agricultural policies.

A criticism of subsidies in the northern countries, and specifically the US, is needed because these subsidies help exporting of crops occur below the global market prices. When this happens, millions and millions of farmers across the globe go hungry or commit suicide in the face of the impossibility of competition within that global marketplace.

Many of these farmers did not choose to lose their basic pillars of life, or becoming dependent on the marketplace. It is part of the larger global agribusinesses attempt to enclose our global commons, working side by side with the World Bank and the IMF to open up communal lands to private property ownership, locking many peasant farmers into the basic contours of capitalism. The goal of these conglomerates is not to feed the world, but control the food for the world.

Policies that benefit a minority amount of the population on the planet are being used to oppress the majority. Many more are suffering from US subsidies than those that are benefiting.

There are two reasons subsidies in the north need to be re-oriented towards an ideology of food sovereignty rather than one of trade.

The first reason is that some of these subsidies intensify and encourage dumping.

This dumping is the key problem for the global peasant farmers, destroying lively-hoods and cultures.

The second reason is that this is stigmatizing subsidies that will encourage the elimination of subsidies in multilateral agreements. This would be devastating to the millions of peasant farmers across the globe, and open our world up to having all food controlled by multinational conglomerates such as Cargill and Monsanto.

These problems are not just local, they are global, and that is why need to build coalitions that can cross borders and cultural barriers. The alternative is only found in such an effort.


Rossett, in 2006
