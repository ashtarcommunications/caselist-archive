The University of Louisville Malcolm X Debate Program is please to announce its intentions to solicit partnerships to join the policy implementation multiculturalism proposal (P.I.M.P.). The Purpose of PIMP is to create a set of norms and procedures in NDT/CEDA debate that focuses not solely on finding the Best Policy Option, but to also train debaters in effective policy implementation to diverse populations using effective communication. Two arguments demonstrate the need for the debate community to consider joining our movement.

First, we identify the harms of the inability of contemporary NDT/CEDA debate to make policy debate effective in a pluralistic society. Professor Cynthia Valdivia-Sutherland of Butte Community College presented this argument at the National Communication Association Convention in 1998:
Valdivia-Sutherland, Butte Community College, 1998
Cynthia-; “Celebrating Differences: Sucessfully Diversifying Forensic Programs”; National Communication Association’s 84th Annual Meeting, November 22, http://www.phirhopi.org/prp/spkrpts5.2/sutherland.htm

So you want to increase diversity within your forensics program? And you are seeking what you hope will be relatively simple solutions to a seemingly difficult problem: How to bring about increased diversity within an academic program that traditionally has been ethnically and culturally homogenized (Loge 1990).
The good news is you aren't alone in your quest for increased forensic diversity. The bad news is nothing worthwhile is ever easy.
Consider this: In 1991, Preston developed a forensics philosophy appropriate to urban commuter colleges, arguing that for these educational institutions an ideal forensics program "combines doing the greatest good for the greatest number with cultural diversity, and effective and ethically sound forensics pedagogy" (p.12). Although for the most part Preston's philosophy was not innovative, his call to incorporate an emphasis on cultural diversity within a forensics program was.
Preston's call arose out of a desire to more fully develop a forensics program that could meet the needs of a rapidly changing academic population, one that was becoming increasingly diverse. For many of us, this same call has sounded within our own programs as we witness the rise of our culturally and ethnically diverse faculty and student populations. And rising they are.
According to the National Center for Educational Statistics, in 1994 approximately 30 percent of all community college students were classified non-white; additionally, approximately 25 percent of all students attending four-year universities and colleges at that time were classified non-white (National Center for Educational Statistics, 1996). Additionally, not only has academia been impacted by a marked increase in ethnic diversity, there has also been an upsurge of re-entry students, a population that will only keep rising with the advent of the 1997 Welfare Reform Act sending numbers of individuals back to school.
Considering the weight of this information, adopting Preston's philosophy appears critical to the future success of forensics programs, not just within urban commuter colleges, but everywhere. Consequently, many of us are seeking the ways and means by which we can diversify our forensics programs.
However, while we may agree that adoption of a philosophy of multiculturalism within forensics is ethically sound -- thus, answering for ourselves the question "why?," -- the question still remains: How are we to accomplish this? This answer lies in an understanding for, and adoption of, a philosophy of pluralism, "a process by which both minority and majority culture members adopt some norms of the other group" (Cox, 1991, p.35).
To help us develop a better understanding of both the question and the answer, we will examine the traditional monocultural, Westernized approach to forensic competitions in comparison to multicultural speaking styles; and then, we will explore the implications of implementing intercultural communication within forensics competitions. The Westernized Perspective of Forensics
Currently, the polyethnic and polycultural student population is comprised of various groups (i.e. Blacks, Native Americans, Latin Americans, Asian Americans, reentry, etc.) with unique, subjective and characteristic ways of behaving, thinking, and communicating. This is significant in view of Albert and Triandis' (1985) argument that because cultural elements are functional "individuals who have appropriate patterns and personalities for a particular environment will fare well and will receive positive outcomes in that environment," but when "individuals from one culture are forced to adopt a very different cultural pattern...they are likely to experience high levels of stress, a reduction in positive outcomes, lower self-esteem, anomie, and general demoralization" (p. 391).
In light of Albert and Triandis' argument, a possible explanation for a lack of cultural diversity within forensics' organizations to date is that forensics competition fosters a climate of assimilation, one in which "minority culture members adopt the norms and values of the dominant group," rather than a climate of pluralism (Cox, 1991, p.35). The importance of the assimilative versus pluralistic environment is crystallized when we recognize that forensics activities uphold an "argumentative perspective in examining problems and communicating with people" (Bartanen, 1995, p. 2). An argumentative perspective is one that involves the use of reasons in support of acts, beliefs, attitudes and values.
Essentially a westernized perspective founded on the philosophies of classic rhetoricians such as Aristotle, Protagoris, and Quintilian, and modern rhetoricians such as Stephen Toulmin, Chaim Perelman, and Richard Weaver, the argumentative perspective promotes "rationality in the pursuit of knowledge," (Kim, 1985, in Samovar and Porter, 1991, p. 404). In contrast, an Eastern perspective "places an emphasis on perceiving and knowing things and events holistically and synthetically, rather than analytically" (p. 403). The significance of these differences is marked, with Eastern thought comprised of vague, sometimes imprecise statements utilizing circular reasoning, while Western thought is comprised of logical and analytical categorizations, utilizing linear reasoning.
In forensic competitions, then, where events are prescribed, speakers are expected to conform to critic-as-audience expectations asserting the Western argumentative perspective; such expectations and assertions equate to utilization of standardized events and conformity constraints. Consequently, in prepared platform speaking events such as persuasive or informative speaking, as well as limited preparation events like impromptu and extemporaneous speaking, critics expect to and do hear speeches conforming to Western rhetorical conventions: an introduction with a thesis and preview; a body made up of three to five points, well supported by evidence; and a conclusion that reviews and sums up what has been presented. In addition to this basic format, critics will also be evaluating clarity and precision of language, careful enunciation and correct pronunciation of vocabulary, and nonverbal behaviors like direct eye contact, appropriate facial expressions, and vocalics. In events like debate, critics will evaluate speakers on the analytical expertise with which they approach the topic, the amount and credibility of their evidence, and their rhetorical strategies in attempting to defeat opponents. Finally, critics evaluating oral interpretive events not only assess the ability of speakers to develop characterizations and emotive levels, but also to create introductions functioning as arguments supported by the literature presented.
Prescribed in such a manner, forensic competitions function monoculturally, effectively offering the dominant Westernized perspective. Yet forensic programs exist in environments that are not monocultural. Consequently, the polycultural environment within which forensics thrives contains a multiplicity of communicative styles -- styles that have been for the most part ignored, marginalized, and silenced in the world of competitive speech and debate. As Barlund (1975) notes, communicative styles may be defined as: By communicative style is meant the topics people prefer to discuss, their favorite forms of interaction -- ritual, repartee, argument, selfdisclosure -- and the depth of involvement they demand of each other. It includes the extent to which communicants rely upon the same channels -- vocal, verbal, physical -- for conveying information, and the extent to which they are tuned to the same level of meaning, that is, to the factual or emotional content of messages (p. 12). To better understand the significance of Barlundís definition, a consideration of culturally different communicative style is warranted.
