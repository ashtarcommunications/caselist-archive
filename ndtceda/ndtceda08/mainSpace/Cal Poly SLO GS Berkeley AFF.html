It is not far to move from the epistemology of situated knowledge to the ontology of the cyborg. The binary oppositions and rigid categorizations of Enlightenment thought—self and other, mind and body, nature and culture, male and female, agent and resource, total and partial cannot help but be broken down in the act of worldly living. These blurred boundaries between men and women, between humans and animals, between humans and machines, between nature and culture offer a deviation from the binary oppositions and rigid categorization that to quote Haraway ‘have been systematic to the logics of domination of women, people of color, nature, workers, animals—in short, domination of all constituted as others, whose taste is to mirror the self” (Haraway 1991). As progressive people, we “should embrace and explore these transgressed boundaries, potent fusions and dangerous possibilities” (Haraway 1991).
