Negative against Morrison:
The courtroom drama reproduces myths of femininity and masculinity that are responsible for gender violence and maintain structural exclusion of the subaltern. We begin with the story of Medusa, as told by Klausman, Menzerin, and Kuhn, in 1997 Women Pirates: The Politics of the Jolly Roger
“Medusa is the Gorgon’s head threatening to emerge from the sea.
…the beautiful woman and her monstrous mother relate one and the same femininity.”

Perseus is the savior, divinely enabled to right the world’s wrongs. In order to achieve his mastery, he must dominate the mysterious and horrible Medusa. His prize at the end is the virgin princess, a beautiful image of the other, passive and without agency. Gayatri Spivak provides analysis in the context of intellectual discourse. The practice of ‘speaking out’ on behalf of the marginalized and suffering can lock them into the passive role of the feminized object. It simultaneously allows the speaker to play hero, imagining that he or she is saving the other rather than enslaving it.
Spivak, “Can the Subaltern speak?”
“In the Foucault-Deleuze conversation, the issue seems to be that there is… obliteration of the trace of that Other in its precarious Subject-ivity.”

The myth rehashes itself in the legal setting of the courtroom.
Caroline Picart, “Rhetorically reconfiguring victimhood and agency: the Violence Against Women Act’s Civil Rights Clause”, Rhetoric and Public Affairs 6.1
“Putting aside the inflated rhetoric… the vengeful woman (agent) and the terrified woman (victim).”

The victim identity is also intensely racialized, making it difficult for women of color to access the legal tools and remedies provided by the Affirmative.
Adele M. Morrison, “Changing the Domestic Violence (Dis)course: Moving from White Victim to Multi-Cultural Survivor”, UC Davis Law Review 39.3
“The first element of domestic violence legal discourse… society imagines that it is she who needs protection.”

We should win because our investigation of gender dominance is more nuanced and productive than the affirmative’s. our criticism creates understanding and has intrinsic transformative value.
Marti Kheel, “From Heroic to Holistic Ethics: The Ecofeminist Challenge”, in Ecofeminism: Women, Animals, Nature.__ Ed. By Greta Gaard, 1993

Our story of Medusa has unique value in undermining phallogocentric logic – it shows how images of the feminine can be both hideous and seductive, monstrous and passive. This ambivalence touches upon the Real and demonstrates the cracks in feminine identity.
Laurens de Vos, To See or not to See, The Uncanny Issue 5, January 2003 www.imageandnarrative.be/uncanny/laurensdevos.htm
