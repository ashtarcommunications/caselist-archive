When one thinks of texts being open to multiple meanings, poststructural theories about the infinite play of language might come to mind. But "polysemy," while related to the deconstructionist concept of indeterminate meaning, is not identical to it. Jacques Derrida has always been careful to distinguish "polysemia" from "dissemination."15 The latter is a thematic that represents an "irreducible" multiplicity of meaning, while the former "is organized within the implicit horizon of a unitary resumption of meaning."16 In other words, with polysemy, distinct meanings exist for a text, and they are identifiable by the critic, the rhetor, or the audience; with dissemination, meaning explodes, and the text can never be reduced to a determinable set of interpretations. According to Roland Barthes, the ideal "writerly" text is "a galaxy of signifiers . . . the codes it mobilizes extend as far as the eye can reach, they are indeterminable."17 The "moderately plural" or "merely polysemous" message is different; it lies somewhere between the indeterminable "writerly" text of the poststructuralist and the univocal "readerly" work of classic literature.18 In short, for the deconstructionist literary critic, polysemy represents multiple, but not limitless, meaning. Likewise, in the study of mass communication messages, critics are careful to define polysemy as a delimited phenomenon. David Morley says polysemy is "structured"; it is neither a message with "a unilateral sign," or "a disparate sign which can be read any way."19 Instead, it is a message that is structured in dominance, but that is open to a certain number of different readings by different groups and classes of viewers. According to John Fiske, "[p]olysemy works through textual devices which admit of a variety of readings," but the polysemic text "is not anarchically open so that any meaning can be derived from it."20 Polysemy indicates a bounded multiplicity, a circumscribed opening of the text in which we acknowledge diverse but finite meanings. In addition to distinguishing polysemy from the more extreme concept of indeterminate meaning, it is important to recognize the difference between a "polysemous" text and a merely "polyvalent" one. Several years ago, Celeste Condit introduced a sensible differentiation between these two terms that changed the way critics talk about multiple interpretations. According to Condit, polysemy is the condition where there is more than one denotational meaning for a text; polyvalence is the condition where there is shared understanding of the denotations of the text, but disagreement about the valuation of those denotations.21 For example, if a pro-choice activist watches a television program and understands it to be supporting a pro-choice position, and a pro-life activist sees the same television program, but understands it to be supporting a pro-life position, the two readings would be polysemous. Each audience member develops a different understanding of what the message means. On the other hand, if both the pro-choice and pro-life activists recognize the program to be a pro-choice message, but the pro-choice viewer cheers the protagonist and the pro-life viewer becomes angry at the protagonist, the two readings would be polyvalent. They both understand that the protagonist is encoded as a "hero," but one accepts the text's message, while the other does not. In this case, rather than contest the denotational meaning of the text, the two audience members contest the valuation of that denotation; what is under dispute is not the literal meaning of the text, but the "attached connotations" that are drawn upon when the text is judged from within particular value systems. Another way of putting it is that polysemy indicates a cognitive gap; polyvalence represents an attitudinal gap, a break in how two or more readers feel about the text. While polysemy is about interpretation, or how people understand messages, polyvalence is about judgment and whether or not people are persuaded by messages.
