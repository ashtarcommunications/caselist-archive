Towson’s approach to debate as a space of liberation repeats the errors of the Warren Court. By transforming debate into a place for judging the inclusion of blackness into polity they implicitly turn whiteness into a constant narrative of enlightenment from bigotry to moral visibility. The consequence of this stance not only legitimizes prior acts of hatred and violence by making them a necessary part of white enlightenment, but guarantees that each step forward for us comes at the cost of someone else getting pushed back.

Lacan DA

Towson’s project misses a crucial insight into the operation of racialized domination. Race is, fundamentally, linked to the power of visibility and the act of marking and rendering bodily images that then are solidified or encoded by other distinctly cultural signs (all of which are seen as linked to a centrally pictured identity). The problem then is one of making racialized domination also visible and contestable. However, race doesn’t work like many theorists of power believe, it isn’t exclusively a product of historical circumstance or power, but also a product of discursive functioning. Whiteness here stands as a master-signifier that in a sense sets the agenda for idealization. The attempt to place another set of signifiers to stand in for whiteness doesn’t disrupt or contest the structure of domination, but rather places a different antagonism at the core of this conflict. Fighting for inclusion becomes about organizing essentialism and reifies violence through associations of guilt, involvement and despair. Only refusing race as a method of looking resolves this. The 1817 slave revolt of Savannah proves our argument.
Kalpana Seshadri-Crooks, professor of English at Boston College, Desiring Whiteness: A Lacanian Analysis of Race, 2000, pg. 35-38
The above view of the ego and the body image raises the question of the … logic of anti-Semitism not as a “racist” aberration of difference, but as the kernel of all racial practice as a mode of looking. 14

Towson’s explanation of racial politics makes two errors: 1) It begins in the middle and 2) it presumes race to be historically bound. The concept of Whiteness as a hegemonic signifier originates internal to the European contintent as a method of differentiating people with white skin by calling them “non-white” and “non-pure,” the Black Irish are the penultimate example. Historicist attempts to challenge whiteness by situating it within practice and negotiation and to vitalize black spaces by producing new aesthetics and languages only reproduce Whiteness as the Master-signifier by introducing new splits. Their advocacy leads to a worse form of neoracism and silently solidifies anatomical discrimination. We must think outside of this paradigm to solve.
Kalpana Seshadri-Crooks, professor of English at Boston College, Desiring Whiteness: A Lacanian Analysis of Race, 2000, pg. 49-56
