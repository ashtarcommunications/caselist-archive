Observation 1: Where we stay

Like your hotel room clean when you travel to a debate tournament? Of course you do! You’re the guest! And to make it that way, it’s most often “nonskilled” migrant workers who do back-breaking work that you’re literally not supposed to see.
Vanselow et al 2010 (Achim Vanselow, Chris Warhurst, Annette Bernhardt, & Laura Dresser; “Working at the Wage Floor: Hotel Room Attendants and Labor Market Institutions in Europe and the United States,” In Low-Wage Work in the Wealthy World [edtrs Jerome Gautie & John Schmitt]; March; 269-318)

What is remarkable… …inconspicuous to the customer.

This ‘unseen’ work is part of a larger project of hospitality within the walls of the hotel… In fact, seeing how social relationships among different bodies are scripted in hotels is key to understanding how the service economy maintains old forms of discrimination in the 21st century
McDowell, Batnitzky, & Dyer 2007 (Linda McDowell, Adina Batnitzky, and Sarah Dyer [School of Geography, University of Oxford]; “Division, Segmentation, and Interpellation: The Embodied Labors of Migrant Workers in a Greater London Hotel”, Economic Geography, 83, 1-25)

Observation 2: What we do


Policy debate takes a lot of work… the kind of work that leaves people who don’t do it, or even who do other debate formats, baffled about why we continue to practice something that requires so much energy… so much repetition… so much of our time… And for most of us the answer is the same. We love it. It brings us joy!

Out of all the things we can’t control in life, this is one thing we can control. We create our own success… our own upward mobility…

Our agency in policy debate is our living labor; even as it keeps us busy we’re able to produce the value that we want to produce… as opposed to the tedious and grueling work many people face on a daily basis
Hardt & Negri 1994 (Michael Hardt & Antonio Negri, The Labor of Dionysus)
This book is aimed... ...Dionysian powers of the netherworld.

To be blunt… this debate community and our joy, our love of this activity, could not function without the labor that cleans up after us... Without the hotel, there is no debate community… It allows us to come together across the country… And for many of our programs, we need cheap hotel rooms… We are all implicated… We work hard to make our labor visible, to take positions, to be advocates… To win!... Yet this year, as we debate visas and immigration, we risk distancing ourselves from the elephant in all the rooms… From the fact that we couldn’t do what we do without room attendants doing what they do… Can the federal government solve?!?! That question abstracts the problem in a way that divorces us from our own work and our places of dwelling… It asks us to present a solution and then wash our hands of the problem… And also that the problem terminate in an apocalyptic scenario, since that’s what our labor is supposed to prevent. Therefore……..

We affirm that our labor would not be possible without the labor of hotel room attendants.

The exploitation of service labor is the engine of the global economy; women & immigrants are unable to actualize as organized labor power because their work is made invisible; we must focus on practices that work to make this labor visible and unitable… In short, we should use our labor to make theirs visible!
Sassen 2003 (Saskia Sassen [Robert S. Lynd Professor of Sociology at Columbia University and Centennial visiting Professor at the London School of Economics; coined the term global city]; “Strategic Instantiations of Gendering in the Global Economy,” In Gender and U.S. Immigration: Contemporary Trends (edtr.
