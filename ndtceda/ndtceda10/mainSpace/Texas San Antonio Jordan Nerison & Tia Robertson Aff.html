An ellipsis of the sign, an eclipse of meaning: an, illusion. The mortal distraction that a single sign can cause instantaneously. Consider the story of the soldier who meets Death at a crossing in the marketplace, and believes he saw him make a menacing gesture in his direction. He rushes to the king's palace and asks the king for his best horse in order that he might flee during the night far from Death, as far as Samarkand. Upon which the king summons Death to the palace and reproaches him for having frightened one of his best servants. ; But Death, astonished, replies: "I didn't mean to frighten him . It was just that I was surprised to see this soldier here, when we had a rendez-vous tomorrow in Samarkand. "


There is a problem with the way that we see death today; for us, death is something we try to ascribe a meaning to, that we fear, that we run from. Especially in debate; every argument impacts back to preventing death, and it goes as an unwritten rule that death is something we want to avoid at all costs, regardless of the argument we are reading. Death is the end, and nothing comes after it; death is the objective destiny that haunts each of us. However, death is more of a rendezvous encounter; it has no strategy, no aim, and no meaning. It is precisely this meaninglessness that is what educes us the most about death.

Yes, one runs towards one's fate all the more surely by seeking to escape it . Yes, everyone seeks his own death, and the failed acts are the most successful . Yes, signs follow an unconscious course. But all this concerns the truth of the rendez-vous in Samarkand; it does not account for the seduction of the story, which is in no way an apologue of truth...........................meaning or "seduced," that the story itself is seductive. It is when signs are seduced that they become seductive


Picture this; when someone does die, we immediately ship them off to god knows where so that they can be cut open, drained out, wired shut, mouth clamped into a smile, embalmed and the spray tanned; ready to go for the funeral. We put them in nice cute little coffins; we hide them underground, where we can’t smell their rotting putrid flesh. At best, we give them flowers once a year (if we can remember), but for the most part, the dead to us have nothing to offer, nothing to exchange, and no longer represent anything of interest to us. Simultaneously, we increasingly obsess over the prospect of a catastrophic death; car crashes, terrorist attacks, horrible slasher movies, assassination's, genocide, murder, accidents…we cant get enough. This obsession is part and parcel with a system that works to exclude death, and leaves us searching for a way to re-inscribe death as a social phenomena.

The next piece of Baudrillard evidence indicates that life is a crime if it survives unilaterally, if we prioritize life at the expense of death. Life and death aren’t inseparable, but symbolic counter-parts, or reversible. Esoteric societies see life and death in this manner, and they have constructed rituals in which death is socially articulated by the group in terms of a symbolic life and death are given and exchanged. Insofar as H-1B visas exist solely for the purpose of guaranteeing productivity from those who immigrate here, they are part and parcel with a system that looks to maximize productivity and life at the expense of its absence; death. Visas represent the symbolic life of the immigrant in the US.
