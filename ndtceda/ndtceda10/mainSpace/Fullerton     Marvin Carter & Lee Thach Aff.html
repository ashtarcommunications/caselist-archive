Citizenship is “the debates, interpretations, and professed needs and interests that surround legal statutes and the social practices to which these are linked[1].” It is “a marker of identity” that “signals "belonging" and "insider-status" in a privileged way[2].” Debates on citizenship tell us “what it means to be an American[3]”. According to Jonathan Weinberg, of the Michigan Law Review, in 2009, “There is no such thing as a U.S. immigrant visa that does not carry with it the promise of citizenship[4].” But the visa cannot make an American out of the foreign other. As Bill Ong Hing argues, when the US inevitably cycles through a nativist period, even the 14th Amendment cannot protect the targets of “de-Americanization[5].” “The de-Americanization process can reinvent itself generation after generation… it is a nativism cloaked in a Eurocentric sense of America that combines hate and racial profiling[6].”

Immigration history reveals the brutal violence of white supremacy actualized in citizenship. Russ Costronovo argues, US political membership is “complicit with a logic that kills off the past and leaves citizens[7].” Part of the neutrality of citizenship the way it sidesteps history[8]. Naturalization is “an amnesiac operation of ideology, in order to signal that the identity of citizen disavows conflictual and antagonistic processes that frame political being. As a natural identity, the citizen discards the scraps and heterogeneous leftovers that do not fit the pattern of legal personhood[9].” Citizenship dehistorizes its own “historical conditions of its own articulation, conditions laden with memories of alternative geographies and temporalities for political being[10].” Although it is not possible to note the countless impositions of white supremacy in US immigration history, we will attempt to note several.

America is a falsely inclusive discourse that seeks to appear neutral and open to the other,[11] where immigration policy in the post-civil rights era circumvents the 14th Amendment by separating race from ethnicity[12]. Shireen M. Roshanravan explains in 2009, “The separation of whiteness from European ethnicity exposes the empty constitution of white racial identity that, in turn, facilitates its conflation with an all-inclusive “America.” According to this logic, those who were of European origin possessed ethnicities amenable to “American” (read: white) ideals, while those with national origins racialized as non-white would forever be labeled foreign and incapable of assimilation (, 7–8). If white is a racial prerequisite for citizenship, and one’s national origins are racialized as non-white, then becoming “American” is an impossible task. Hailed as a color-blind meritocracy, this white-supremacist America nevertheless denies racial barriers to national inclusion. The popular discourse that anyone can achieve the “American Dream” so long as they work hard seduces immigrants of color to negate their racial-ethnic identity as a means of becoming “American just like everybody else.” The seduction is facilitated by the negative formulation of white racial identity[13].” The seduction of American neutrality invites immigrants of color to assimilate, but in order to complete assimilation immigrants must negate the self “becoming non-Mexican Mexicans, non-Indian Indians, non-Korean Koreans, and so on. The illusory base of the seduction is evidenced by the fact that one remains “Mexican,” “Korean,” “Indian,” “minority,” or “foreign” even after the processes of negating one’s racial-ethnic self in a mimicry of white/Anglo ways… in a racial context where whiteness is coded as the unmarked norm of society, inclusion in that society may seem less impossible[14].”

American citizenship is tied to our beginnings, Manifest Destiny. The U.S. settler population mentality, to this very day, shares “a core belief that it is somehow inherently, singularly, even mystically, entitled to dominate all it encounters, possessing or at least benefiting from that which belongs to others, regardless of the costs and consequences visited upon those thereby subjugated and dispossessed…there are few who would queue up to argue the defensibility of a way of life predicated in/sustained by invasion, conquest, genocide, ecocide, colonization, and other modes of systemic theft[15].” “The genocide allows an “acceptable cost of doing business” blend(s) “perfectly into their own autogenocide until the grim prospect of species extinction has at last been realize[16].” From 1492 to 1650, the indigenous population of North American declined from 112 million people to 6 million when the US drew its geographical and sociological lines determining access to the country[17].

The genocide of North America was the foundation of US immigration policy whereby the U.S. reserves the right to expand its territory while determining which bodies belong in that territory based on white supremacist assumptions. Part of this process was the Treaty of Guadalupe, signed post the Mexican-American War, “formally requires of Mexican Americans that they give up, relinquish, the "character" of Mexicanness in order to enjoy "all the rights of the citizens of the United States[18].” Maria Josefina Saldana-Portillo, associate professor of ethnic studies at Brown in 2004, contends, “the first step on the road to becoming a U.S. citizen is a negation, a becoming un-preserved, disposed of, lost, wasted[19].”

The economy would be a major catalyst in the need for immigrants, but the people brought to the US would not be considered American. One of the most egregious immigration programs was the Trans-Atlantic slave trade. African slaves were kidnapped in one of the most brutal crimes in US history and forcibly brought to the US; “the Trans-Atlantic slave trade was a crime against humanity.[20]” The conditions on slave ships were so inhumane that “an English surgeon in 1790 estimated that two-thirds of the deaths on the slave journey were due to a "mortal melancholy" similar to an involuntary suicide.[21]” Once in the US, slave traders dehumanized and profited from the labor of slaves by physically and psychologically terrorizing them. Families were separated/babies sold at auctions; rape was a constant threat their entire lives; slaves limbs were amputated to prevent them from fleeing; and traders sometimes burned slaves alive or hanged them from trees for reasons ranging from simple torture to punishment for attempting to secure their freedom[22].

The Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882 was one such act. Chinese laborers laid around 1700 miles of tracks for the US economy. “On average, for each two miles of track laid, three Chinese laborers were killed by accidents. More than one thousand Chinese railroad workers died [23].” And when the Trans Atlantic railroad was built, Chinese laborers had been used up and were now being prohibited from entering the country as they were competing against white citizens.

Immigration restrictions to protect the American worker became the norm. The Immigration Act of 1924 made national quotas permanent[24]. “The quota machinery legitimated ideas about assimilability, identified certain kinds of immigrants as being incapable of ‘fitting in’…and validated the belief that ‘race’ classification was the most accurate way of making sense of the world’s peoples[25].” “While the national origins category racialized some Europeans, it reaffirmed their common whiteness. In naturalization applications, European immigrants were expected to fill in their “color” as “white,” while indicating their national origin as their “race”…In the context of growing undocumented…immigration created by quota legislation, an independent Border Patrol was established[26].

This racialization process has created the racial and religious animosity toward Arabs and Muslims in the post-9/11 era[27]. Modern visa issuance and immigration enforcement must be screened through the lens of national security in an effort to prevent a highly racialized terrorist from entering the US[28]. Natsu Saito argues, “Arab Americans and Muslims have been "raced" as "terrorists": foreign, disloyal, and imminently threatening[29].” The fear of Arab and Muslim people as imminently threatening resulted in a social condition of inhumanness that the government leveraged to justify torturing enemy combatants who fit their profile[30].

In this way, the Lebanese politizen in Australia evacuates the dichotomy between "inside" and "outside" of any political meaning. Nowhere on the Australian sociopolitical landscape is the fiction of "insider/outsider" binary revealed more starkly, or ironically, than in that most famous form of homosocial bonding: mateship. Best translated as absolutely loyalty to your friends, mateship is a name for that close bond that connects, say, surfers on a particular beach to each other, which sees men engrave tattoos with their postal codes (as happened widely in the aftermath of Cronulla) on their bodies to mark [End Page 154] their loyalty to their mates and their place. However, as an "autoimmune" construction of community, mateship rests upon a loyalty to the self and those other selves that is necessarily in an antagonistic relation to the Other/s, to those who may be white but are not your mates or to those who are not white. "Not white, Not welcome at Cronulla" is about as reductive as mateship gets. But Cronulla was not without its twists in the politics of mateship. Although theirs is not by any means a secure form of citizenship, the Pacific Islanders are "immigrants" of longer standing—they are not presumed to be Muslim, they have no links to the Bali bombings—so they made common cause with the Bra Boys. Represented as out of place in the place where they live, they turn, in the event, to that form of political community that is at the core of local values, which are also transnational and even, we might say, transcultural: is there anything more Australian than being loyal to your mates, even if that means resorting to violence? 'The community wants these people locked up and I'm going to keep raising the issue every day until those couple of hundred Middle Eastern thugs are behind bars.'"16 The politizen reveals the limits, the biopolitically grave limits, of those who have rights but have no rights. The politizen reveals, if you will, the gap between the Law—which grants the rights—and the sovereign—who determines which rights can be enacted. The politizen is compelled, in this way, to act within the terms of the Schmittian nomos: under the order of the sovereign. The politizen is likely to run afoul of both the Law and the sovereign, reduced from precarious citizen—the raced citizen who owes its citizenship to the "hospitality" of the white [End Page 156] host—to the, at once and alternately, vulnerable and aggressive politizen. The politizen’s political unpredictability is “its singular advantage: the citizen never knows, can never know, what stirs the politizen into life; neither the citizen nor the state is ever sure if, or when, the politizen will erupt into the political. The politizen's lack of "political characteristics," organization, party structure, trade union membership, constitutes its signal advantage: it is the political actor that disrupts the political and then retreats into, if not its "foreignness," then certainly its circumscribed citizenship
