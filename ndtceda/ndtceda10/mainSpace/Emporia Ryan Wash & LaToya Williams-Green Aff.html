The invisibles – the people who’s bodies are here but who’s faces you never look at. They are here but they are not seen. What does citizenship mean to a family who is torn apart by arbitrary legalities that can remove Tony’s father but leave the rest of his family? What does citizenship mean to someone who works harder than most citizens but gets shorted the benefits? What does citizenship mean to someone who is here but is not supposed to be?

Tony’s words tell us a little about what life is like for those who cannot become legal citizens. But we open with Tony’s words as an opening for a broader discussion of what life is like for EVERYONE who is not supposed to be here – not in debate, not in this country, and not where they feel their home is.

This affirmative is a rhetorical investigation of the rhetoric of citizenship, community, and belonging on behalf of those who are ‘not supposed to be here’.

This discussion will include the perspectives of those who are labeled as “illegal migrants”, those who don’t fit into the traditional notions of debate, and the belonging of debate as a whole in the communities we come from.



For now, lets talk about debate. The resolution includes and excludes voices along the lines of who is and is not supposed to be here. But just like the laws that ripped Tony’s daddy away from his family, the “law” of the resolution is arbitrary. There is not stable way of saying how one should interact with it.


Yet, we tend to interact with the resolution in a very specific way according to the traditional norms of debate – the resolution is a border: inside the border are the voices that belong and outside are those who shouldn’t be allowed. There is a resolution but how we interact with it is not some rule or law.

In fact, as ethical actors in debate, we are obligated to engage in discussions that allow us to interrogate the ways the resolution functions and, in turn, the ways we interact with it. The resolution is also empty. It looks like there is advocacy in there – personal investment in argument – but instead, the resolution becomes a mechanical guide-post for how we should go through the motions. An awkwardly-worded sentence that tells us what the limits of our speeches are, the limits of who we can be when speak in debate rounds. Most importantly, it tells us what arguments are worthy of our labors and which ones are irrelevant – which 9 minute chunks are just noise.

And similar to its form, the content of the resolution also relies on the rhetoric of worthiness. Some people who want to come here are worthy – they come from a certain country, they are a family member of a type of family the USFG thinks is real, they don’t have diseases, they’ve never been criminals.

The resolution takes aim at investigating our relationship to the other outside of ourselves, but leaves unquestioned the method by which we determine who is worthy and who is unwelcome.

This affirmative is about acknowledging those voices that have been excluded by this rhetoric of worthiness; those who have been forgotten to death.

And even those who become migrant workers don’t even discover some dream-world when they get to their destination. Consider Lulu, who received a visa to work in London as a domestic worker:

“I received a very low wage which I accepted because there was no alternative. My employer did not follow the contract. There was no day off. I was maltreated, overworked and [had] few hours sleep. When I arrived in London my employer was always shouting at me for whatever little mistakes I did in the housework. Whenever I said that I was not feeling well my employer would shout back at me: ‘Why are you not feeling well? I did not pay for you in the agency to be sick. I paid for you to work’… I wasn’t feeling well because the food that I was eating was not enough to sustain me to do all of my work. In the morning I would eat a slice of bread and have a cup of tea. During lunch I would usually have a bowl of rice and some water, and in the evening I would have a slice of bread and a cup of tea again…. The lady wants me to sleep in the garage if there is a carpet but I argue with her that the garage is only for the car and it’s very cold. I told her that I cannot bear to sleep there” (Anderson 1993, 48).

Although these stories are very different, they have one thing in common. They are all the victims of what we will call “social death”. This is different than physical death, although too often closely related to it. Social death is the feeling of not being there, of disappearing. Those who experience social death watch others respond to their voice as though it were noise, a distraction, a statement that simply did not take place.

Therefore, what all these narratives indicate is that this topic is leaving something out. The rhetoric of worthiness that informs debate AND the topic creates an undercurrent of social death. Our engagement with this resolution demands that we investigate our relationship to others.

We feel that this investigation must not only begin with an analysis of the topic, but an analysis of ourselves as well. How do we relate to other people? How do we understand their death and, consequently, our own?

This debate should be about who has the best method for acknowledging the dead.

By “dead”, we don’t mean only the physiologically dead – we mean social death as well. How do we heed the perspectives of those who have been excluded and silenced? How can we affirm the topic and empty is problematic politics of the rhetoric of worthiness? What parallels can we see between the social death of those denied or awarded visas and social death in our own activity? How do we socially die and how can we better acknowledge each other’s voices? Social Death, being the result of the inability to make other peoples existence important, occurs in debate at an alarming rate. It happens every time framework limits are used to justify problematizing the way people interact with the topic. We should approach this activity with the desire to bring revelation to the topic instead of diming the light it sheds on our understanding of it. The separation in style of argumentation in debate also creates and intensifies social death. Traditional teams, K teams, performance teams, and any other classifications of debate styles are constructed currently to indicate a system of privilege placing traditional styles at the top of what should be equally accepted tiers of debating. Without a shift in the way we acknowledge the people and practices around us, we gain no appreciation for our surroundings, making them socially dead to us and we continue living like nothing happened. That’s why we believe that this debate should be about who has the best method to acknowledging the dead.
