Mental Health Aff (Narrative Version)

On January 8th a shooting occurred in Tuscon that produced a national debate on American public discourse. Arizona is a key site in the immigration debate – the discursive environment was ripe with hatred towards illegal immigrants and anti-government rhetoric. Sherrif Dupnik in response to the initial attack “The anger, the hatred, the bigotry…Arizona I think has become sort of the capital. We have become the mecca for prejudice and bigotry.” The tragedy produced a ripple in the discursive and political conscious of the country. We immediately began a critical reflection upon the discourses we produce the way violence in our speech act can create violence in our politics and induce material suffering. An opportunity was opened to re-orient criticism back onto the constant state of violence and securitization that plagues public discourse in our country.
Almost immediately a number of political discourses attempted to fill the opening – ranging from calls to end the violent political imagery to demands for increased gun control. These competing narratives shape the meaning of the event and the histories it produces by structuring the meta-narrative of the event. The event has become a site of knowledge production – with each shaping of knowledge competing for dominance to shape the history of the Giffords shooting. The Giffords tragedy offers us the opportunity to glimpse how power operates to subvert narratives that challenge authority and instead diffuses resistance into realms that obscure the operation of power. With a sleight of hand the discussion is reoriented from liberal or progressive calls to a fundamentally conservative one.
9/11 provides an example – an opportunity for critical reflection on the process of globalization and the way US hegemony and intervention in the Middle East had produced animosity and antagonism was bypassed for a retreat back into an ideology. Power shaped the knowledge and history of the event producing identities and subjects. Terrorists were crazy fundamentalists who hated our freedom. This knowledge meant only violent solutions were available and directed our policy choices in favor of a narrative that reinforced US hegemonic forces and biopolitical control. Today we are seeing a similar reshaping of a narrative to produce a similar retreat into ideology and power via the production of knowledge with the solutions limited to discipline and punishment.

The Giffords shooting has produced another set of narratives that seek to deflect criticism from violence and political justice towards calls for 60’s styles reincarceration of the mentally ill. One of the first instances of this narrative was produced by Sheriff Clarence Dupnik when he claimed, “We have very, very serious problems in this community. Back in 1960, when I was a young cop on the beat, we put the mentally ill people who were threats into a system that incarcerated them. Today, they’re out on the street, and we’re paying a price for it.” (Democracy Now, CNN).

This narrative has spread to the front-pages of the New York Times and begun to dominate media coverage of the Tuscon shooting. Instead of critical reflection the dominant narrative claims that the Tuscon shooting was an isolated incident composed in the deranged mind of an uncontrollably mentally ill individual. There is no societal narrative, no reason to contest power or discourse, in fact the only thing that is necessary is more biopolitical control of a population far on the periphery of society. Instead of challenges to state authority and rhetoric we get calls for more centralization, more involuntary commitment, more discipline, more punishment.
