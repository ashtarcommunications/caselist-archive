One way of challenging whiteness ……. the very metanarrative world-making of whiteness itself.






Another very important manifestation of whiteness is its invisibility. This is what makes it so powerful. Because it is invisible and not “explicit”, then it also becomes fluid and changing. We would like to identify the difference between racism and whiteness. Because “racism” has been defined by most as a physical objective characteristic, we full that we can do things to “solve” this problem. The civil rights acts thought that if we attacked “racism” there would finally be an end to inequalities, but it actually only gave us this false faith that racism was gone, while it then slide between the cracks, behind the laws, and in the bricks and fields of the prison system. This whole conception of being able to “solve” a problem through external means is what we will identify as transcendance… This is also how whiteness continues to manifest itself, through the bad faith thought that we have transcended the inequality or that you have a “universal” tool to address the problem, allows that which is undone to be more powerful than what you think you have “solved”…
Birt ‘04
[Robert Birt, Professor of Philosophy Morgan State University. “The Bad Faith of Whiteness.” In George Yancy, ed., What White Looks Like: African-American Philosophers on the Whiteness Question. 55-64.]

Our argument is simple. We feel that a politics of whiteness should be rejected because of the ontological manifestations that it perpetuates. Politics built upon ontology’s of whiteness function to reproduce the powerful norms of white supremacy in a different and ever changing mode. By making whiteness visible, by identifying it within the political and social methodologies, we can begin to effective combat the powers of white supremacy that make all of the problems discussed in the previous speech inevitable.
