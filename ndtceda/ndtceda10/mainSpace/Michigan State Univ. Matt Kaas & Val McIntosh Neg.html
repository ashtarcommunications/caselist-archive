First, I am not a lawyer and only do the adjudication for non-immigrant visas here in Brasilia. But this is a fun question. Technically, a visa is required in order for an alien to be admitted into the country unless the alien carries a valid passport from a visa waiver program country. In this sense then, in order to be "admissible" an alien must first be found "eligible" for a visa and also receive a visa. "Eligibility" precedes "admissibility". It seems that one cannot be "ineligible" and "admissible". We do have cases where people were eligible for non-immigrant visas and were admitted to the U.S., but because they "went out of status" (e.g. stayed beyond six months on a visitor's visa), they are now ineligible for a new visa and have lost their "admissibility". In some cases, ineligible persons can pursue a waiver. They remain "ineligible" in the system but get approval for a visa through the waiver process. Ineligibility also pertains to visa type. There are some people who are ineligible for certain types of visas but eligible for others. For example, I had a case of a diplomat family member who is not eligible for a tourist visa due to an illegal presence in the U.S., but if the diplomat gets assigned by his/her government to the U.S., the entire family becomes eligible for a different class of visa, including the one with the ineligibility for a different type of visa. Here is a case where someone is ineligible for a tourist visa but can be eligible for a diplomatic visa and then admissible. I hope this information was of use to you. By the way, did you pose this question to other consulates?
