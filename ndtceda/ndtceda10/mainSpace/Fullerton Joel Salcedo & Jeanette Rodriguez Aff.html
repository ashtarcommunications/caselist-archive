My Stories I live in the city of santa ana, one of the citys with the biggest mexican immigrant population where I experience first hand the ways that immigration happens on a day to day level. Say bye to my mom as she leaves to work at social service agency helping migrants who need financial help from the state. She tells me about the way they feel when they come to the US and have to ask for help … intimidated, scared, unaware of the way the US functions oftentimes denying federal aid at the false belief that their children would have to pay off later in life. You see there are a lot of single moms that don’t have documentation but they have kids who are US citizens who need help with school, tuition and money to survive. Feeling abandoned, they put their pride off to the side and reluctantly ask for help from the state. While society pushes the idea that all immigrants want is for the state to do things for them, they can’t do things on their own.

My dad was born in tiajuana mexico on his way toward the US. He entered the US when he was one and got a green card that expired when I was 15. He was able to work but only for a low budget painting company that paid him under the table. My dad knew his residency card was going to expire so he applied for citizenship which was a painstaking process, that took time and money, something that was hard for a man who lives check to check to survive, but he did it and put his life on hold because he knew otherwise he’d risk being deported if he was ever pulled over by a cop. Something he couldn’t let happen because he knew that our future rested in his ability to make a good living. He asked my grandmother for his birth certificate which had to be sent from mexico into the US… he had never seen it before. This piece of paper that was suppose to know his life, claimed that he was born on a different day than we had been celebrating it for the past 15 years before that ….. and what was worse was that the officials who had his life hanging by a string put him down for his past, acting like they knew my father better than he knew himself. When he applied for his papers the officials at the immigration office brought up his file. He had some minor offenses from when he was young that the cops kept bringing him back up. They treated him like a criminal… Since my brothers and I were born he changed his ways and stopped drinking stopped going out , he and my mom divorced and he was a new man with a new wife with a new life. He couldn’t prove he was a good man, he had to rely on the objective exerts to analyze him and determine if he was a good man. His sense of worth and value in the hands of professionals. 1. People like my dad feel intimdated by state officials. The real issue that immigrants face is the colonial legacy that disempowers them during interactions in face-to-face encounters with state agents at the lowest level (cops, border agents, school officials, etc.). The constant fear at the sight of a police officer; hoping the cop doesn’t pull you over and ask for papers you don’t have; hoping you don’t get put in the back of the car in handcuffs because you’re existence is a criminal act. The anxiety of applying for legal residence and the fear of being rejected and denied because of things you did when you were young poor and desperate. Constantly feeling inferior when border patrol agents and lawyers remind you that your future and well being rests in their hands; they are in power, you must obey. Culture, power, and knowledge are all always interlinked. What we are taught in school is determined by power and what power thinks if valuable. Since I was young I never learned about Mexican culture, the history of people in Mexico or the reasons why people like my family migrated to the US. People in power chose my curriculum, and what I was taught was cowboys and Indians where the domination of natives was divine and necessary. What I was taught was education that privileges a Anglo understanding of how things went down, it was powers ability to influence how knowledge is produced and allowed for the masked Eurocentric cultural understanding of history to be embedded into my elementary school textbooks as if it was neutral and objective, when it was really tied to the cultural knowledge production of the authors who wrote it. You see… [story of my dad being pulled over by a cop and the trauma involved with being seen as a criminal] I am exactly how my dad is, Its hard for me to be passionate and emotional and to feel… He has taught me to be tough. To survive harassment, rejection, being alone and feeling worthless….. its hard for me to convey the severity of my story but believe me, its shaped who I am today. 2. Our purpose is to speak to policy and theoretical issues from personal experience. The process is called auto-ethnography and it seeks to create knowledge from a different cultural perspective. One that doesn’t claim to be objective and neutral. This method can produce knowledge that is emancipatory; knowledge that claims neutrality entrenches oppression; one can not describe culture from an outside perspective as if they are disconnected from the phenomena. I’m going to read a quote now, not because I think it proves anything, but because it has an especially clear way of stating what autoethnography tries to do. Remember, we are ONLY trying to explain what we are TRYING to do, we’re not saying this proves that we do it. It is from Mariolga Reyes Cruz of Universidad de Puerto Rico and Christopher Sonn of Victoria University in Australia: “As Smith (1999) has indicated, this requires researchers and practitioners to examine the motivations and the basic assumptions that inform knowledge production within contexts of intergroup relations. The deconstruction opens the way to transformation as we recognize different ways of knowing and value the lived experiences and voices of the marginalized” (p. 207; Am J Community Psychology, vol. 47, 2011). Obviously, we want to know the topic in a different way. ANY interpretation of the topic is no more than the cultural expression of the person offering the interpretation. We believe the core issue of the topic is whether to expand opportunities for immigrants, we believe that we should, and we think the best way to do so is using the method of deconstruction.

“Home”… is a truly magical word, offering a most needed relief from the world of the weird, the unsafe, even the real. Home is where the personal sometimes unexpectedly, sometimes inextricably meets the political. The home question can thus carry us from the confines of our own skin to the open space of a world inhabited by others like and unlike me. The notion of home carries us to a politics of location.
The United States is depicted as a welcoming home, a land of opportunity, where people come by choice to live out the “American Dream” and improve their life. The reality is very different for migrants, The VISA system within the United States was supposed to provide an equal opportunity for all people of the world to immigrate to the United States if they so wish. The reality behind this system is that it is one that is highly privileged and exclusive. The VISA system and immigration law create conditions that exclude the voices of migrants who are not only workers and are greatly exploited, but Anne Shea states that immigration and labor law work together to create a legal space which excludes the voices of guestworkers. Despite a well documented record of workplace abuses by farmworker advocates, no worker has ever registered a complaint with government regulators. Through the exclusion of the farmworkers’ narratives, the law becomes a univocal script written by lawmakers and employers, and serving their interests. Because labor and immigration laws make it difficult and dangerous for farmworkers to register complaints legal recourse against employers becomes merely a myth.
Shea goes on to explain how workers’ bodies are physically coerced through systems of control regulating their movement, in the fields, in the camps, and across borders. The state produces discourses that reduce, instrumentalize, and disassemble workers bodies leaving them in an optimal position for continued exploitation.
