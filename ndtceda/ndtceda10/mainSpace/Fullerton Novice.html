Contention 1: The Status Quo is Failing

1. Even though labor laws prohibit the abuse of temporary immigrant employees, extreme abuse by employers is routine
Carr, JD from Columbia Law, 10 (Eleanor G., Columbia Journal of Law and Social Problems, “Search for a Round Peg: Seeking a Remedy for Recruitment Abuses in the U.S. Guest Worker Program”, Summer, 43 Colum. J.L. & Soc. Probs. 399, database: lexis)

Contention 2: An Invisible Labor Force

1. Immigrants are invisible labor which makes them vulnerable to exploitation
Haynes, Associate Professor of Law at New England Law, 9 (Dina Francesca, Notre Dame Journal of Law, “EXPLOITATION NATION: THE THIN AND GREY LEGAL LINES BETWEEN TRAFFICKED PERSONS AND ABUSED MIGRANT LABORERS”, 23 ND J. L. Ethics & Pub Pol'y 1, database: lexis)

2. The situation is even worse for undocumented workers who can be treated like chattel slaves under current immigration law.
Haynes, 9 (Dina Francesca, Notre Dame Journal of Law, “EXPLOITATION NATION: THE THIN AND GREY LEGAL LINES BETWEEN TRAFFICKED PERSONS AND ABUSED MIGRANT LABORERS”, 23 ND J. L. Ethics & Pub Pol'y 1, database: lexis)

3. Contemporary human trafficking is not about snatching people from their homelands. It’s about people who want to make their lives better and have that desire exploited. To combat trafficking, we must recognize its link to the economy and empower those who are vulnerable
Haynes, 9 (Dina Francesca, Notre Dame Journal of Law, “EXPLOITATION NATION: THE THIN AND GREY LEGAL LINES BETWEEN TRAFFICKED PERSONS AND ABUSED MIGRANT LABORERS”, 23 ND J. L. Ethics & Pub Pol'y 1, database: lexis)

4. Trafficked humans are reduced to commodities and we should not rationalize their exploitation.
Haynes, 9 (Dina Francesca, Notre Dame Journal of Law, “EXPLOITATION NATION: THE THIN AND GREY LEGAL LINES BETWEEN TRAFFICKED PERSONS AND ABUSED MIGRANT LABORERS”, 23 ND J. L. Ethics & Pub Pol'y 1, database: lexis)

5. The coercion of workers bodies instrumentalizes them. Workers become the dehumanized commodity
Shea, 2003 (Anne, ELUS, “Don't Let Them Make You Feel You Did a Crime”: Immigration Law, Labor Rights, and Farmworker Testimony, database: ebsco)

6. Dehumanization makes annihilation via genocide, warfare, or conquest an obsession. Dehumanized people possess no meaning and become waste. Regard all people as worthy of a moral obligation for protection.
Waller, 02 (James, Becoming Evil: How Ordinary People Commit Genocide and Mass Killing., p. 244-245)

Thus the Plan:
The USFG should increase eligibility beneficiary for human trafficking visas by defining trafficking in the TVPA as all economy activity that treats human beings as a commodity and provide temporary residency permits, not conditioned on a criminal investigation, for the victims of trafficking.

When Americans think about labor and the economy in the US, there are a series of workers who do not exist in terms of rights, but only exist in terms of their economic output. Those laborers are generally immigrants, some documented and other not document. They are subject to unbelievable situations of abuse at the hands on unscrupulous employers. This affirmative is an attempt to listen to those who suffer in the invisible labor system in the US and respond in a pragmatic manner. Failure to re-prioritize the system continues to sacrifice people in the name of the economy.

The current visa system is a mail order workers program. The system is designed to make abuse easy and employers design conditions to keep workers silent and productive

Thus We Offer the Following Plan:
The United States Federal Government should increase eligibility beneficiary for human trafficking visas by defining severe labor trafficking in the Trafficking Victims Protection Act as all economic activity that treats human beings as a commodity and provide temporary residency permits, not conditioned on a criminal investigation, for the victims of trafficking. We reserve the right to clarify intent.
