Last night, when we were reading west georgia’s case it seemed ironic to me that the way I was able to do that was on the internet. The obsession with being worried about nuclear war provided the incentive to the development infrastructure of the internet. Independent of warfighting, this is one of many ways I connect to nuclear weapons.

My personal experience with nuclear weapons encompasses more than just experiencing the technology. Growing up, I loved many things. Two of these were the Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles and Dr. Seuss. With video games, comics, and action figures, they were an integral part of my playing during my childhood. At the time, I knew that they had gained their abilities from nuclear radioactive waste . Independent of the turtles, I loved reading Dr. Seuss. One of my favorite Seuss books was the Butter Battle Book, a story about the Yooks and Zooks who are separated by a giant wall and disagree over the proper way to eat toast. The sides fought and eventually developed the “Bitsy Big-Boy Boomeroo.” I was fascinated not only by the idea of eating toast with the butter on the bottom side but also by the drive these two sides had to develop technology to defend their positions. After I had moved on from Dr. Seuss and the Turtles and was about 11, I became fascinated with space. I wanted to become an astro-physicist or an astronaut and explore the universe. I remember reading that one of the ways humans could travel throughout space is by using nuclear power and technology. This was my first introduction to the science of nuclear technology, during 7th grade chemistry. Although I no longer pursue a career of nuclear engineering or astro-physics, that has no stopped by fascination with what role nuclear weapons play in space. Over the summer, when Joe Packer and I began researching this topic, we had idea to look at what effect nuclear weapons have on asteroids. This nostalgic research reminded me of my desire to explore space. We investigated why in 2005 NASA recommended a policy to use nuclear weapons against an asteroid especially given the ineffectiveness of such a deflection technique. This technology has provided me the opportunity to speak about my experience on a larger level. You see, Joe, Adam (who helped with the research), and I are writing an article for the journal Timely Interventions. This is allowing me to develop intellectually as a young scholar and learn how to present my ideas in a compelling fashion.

While all these things are uplifting, this is not the only relationship I have with nuclear technology. When my great grandmother immigrated to the United States in the early twentieth century. She found herself in Boulder City, west of Hoover Dam. At the time, the area provided various construction work, which was the profession of my great grandfather. My grandma chose to stay in the region and when the test site opened in the 1950s, it provided many jobs for people in that community. My grandma would begin working there in the 1960s and stay until its closing in the 90s. Now my grandma suffers from many health problems: heart, back, etc. Doctors are pretty sure the result is extended exposure to radiation at the test site. Looking back, I reminded every time I see her the sacrifices she made, being an immigrant in the U.S., for my father and our family to enjoy a prosperous life.

My experience has been both positive and negative with regards to nuclear technology. However, it is nuclear technology that has allowed me to develop as an individual for good and bad.
