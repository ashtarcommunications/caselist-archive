Observation 1: Declaratory Policy’s Relation to War.
This year’s resolution asks if the United States federal government should reduce its nuclear stockpile or reduce the role and/or missions of nuclear weapons.
As we have researched this resolution we have learned that when it comes to nuclear policy, what is said is just as important as what is done. Indeed many affirmatives this year fiat a change in "declaratory" nuclear policy. In other words, language is our policy. Recognizing the importance language in nuclear politics is essential to nuclear policy making.
Harold H Saunders, White House National Security Council from 1961-1974 and State Department from 1974-81, 1990 ("An Historic Challenge to Rethink how Nations Relate." The Psycodynamics of International Relationships. Vol1: Concepts and Theories.)
“Reorganizing our thinking about… been neglected too often.”

Discussing the language behind policy framing is key to understanding how policy proposals are received, passed and implemented.
Frameworks Institute Viewed 2009 (The Frameworks Institute is a non-profit think tank dedicated to advancing the nonprofit sector's communications capacity by identifying, translating and modeling relevant scholarly research for framing the public discourse about social problems. http://www.frameworksinstitute.org/sfa.html)
“But what many social policy practitioners… must ask and answer.”

And, while researching nuclear missions, one stands out to us as having particular rhetorical significance. In strategy circles it is called the “OVERAWE” mission—the size of our nuclear weapons stockpile is to AWE rivals, specifically China, to intimidate and dissuade them from challenging U.S. primacy. This mission has no physical deployment, or any military official especially responsible for carrying it out, indeed this is a mission that is entirely rhetorically—defined by what policy makers SAY the mission and role of our nuclear weapons are.
Oelrich, Federation of American Scientists, January 2005 (Missions for Nuclear Weapons after the Cold War)
“Two of the missions that this study… of specifically nuclear use.”

The overawe mission is specifically and rhetorically targeted at China out of paranoia of their rise to international status. It is based on absurdly flawed scholarship of how China makes policy.
Oelrich, Federation of American Scientists, January 2005 (Missions for Nuclear Weapons after the Cold War)
“The overawe mission is distinct… with a shift in approach to sizing.”

The demonizing imagery surrounding the “Overawe” mission is typical of the congressional rhetoric regarding China. Unsubstantiated paranoia regarding Chinese proliferation is embodied in racist, hateful and threatening rants on Capital Hill and in Washington as a whole. This China demonization is unproductive and destroys productive policy making with regards to China, therefore increasing the likelihood of mistrust and conflict.
Stanley Lubman 2004 (“The dragon as demon: images of China on Capitol Hill.” Journal of Contemporary China. August 2004.
"The article first establishes… think they are a threat to the entire world.”

Mainstream IR literature which fuels this cycle of China demonization in Washington suffer from flawed assumptions masquerading as neutral reality. The rhetoric of the “Chinese threat” argument encourages bad policymaking that ultimately makes the threat “real.”
Chengxin Pan 2004 (“The china therat in American self-imagination: the discursive construction of other as power politics” Alternatives V.29)
“While U.S. China scholars argue… to make a contribution.”

And, this mode of discourse leads to problematic attempts like “Overawe” that attempt to control China and dictate Chinese actions. It paralyzes productive debate on U.S. policy making.
Chengxin Pan 2004 (“The china therat in American self-imagination: the discursive construction of other as power politics” Alternatives V.29)
