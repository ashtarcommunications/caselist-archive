We do a genealogy of nuclear weapons.

We think this is necessary to be able to fully interrogate nuclear weapons policy and develop new understandings of the bomb and how it effects our lives. We think that most interrogations highlight certain histories over others and that this form of interrogation privileges certain knowledge which excludes certain perspectives and only re-entrenches a lot of harms like colonial mindsets and crisis-driven politics (these are some of the "advantages" so to speak). We think that by allowing for the cultural-historical overlay new tangible understanding of nuclear weapons can be gained which is better for us to better come to a place where we could do something about them.

We don't defend that there is any spill over outside of the room or that there any out of round impacts from the decision of debate.

We think that the most important standard and way to evaluate is through education.

The role of the ballot is an endorsement of the genealogical process.
