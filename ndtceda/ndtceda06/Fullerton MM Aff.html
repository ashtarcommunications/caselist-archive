Milliken Aff


Part I: The Poem: The poem we use explains a version of the creation story found in the Aztec Calendar.

A few hundred years ago, I would have been massacred in the telling of this story,
Just like I could be excommunicated by my Church,

Expelled from the Christian school that calls me the heretic, the blasphemer, the disbeliever.
But worse still,
I would be lashed, and internal and external lashing that gapes my soul and physically opens up the wounds that are carried by my father, my brother, and my daughter.

“Here is the oral account of what is known ….end shall come”.[1]


Part II: Mental Colonization:

At the center of the Aztec calendar is a human face. Most Mexicanos who look into the piercing eyes of the Sun Stone see something they know but have difficulty articulating. The language of this ancient text is unknown to them, deprived of its use for nearly four hundred years. [Mexicanos] are expressing a dilemma articulated by those who have been colonized and have not recovered their history, a history confined in museums and used in academic interpretations by a society that uses it for its own objectification rather than as an active avenue of knowledge. Part of what Mexicanos are saying when reluctantly accepting a historic tie to the face of the Tonal Machiotl (Aztec calendar) is “It’s my face but it looks foreign to me. Part of me is not understandable to my psyche.” Another implication is racial in nature. The face in the calendar is an indigenous face, but how can it be mine if I am no longer indigenous? These questions reflect an internal conflict affected by five centuries of European colonization. Reclaiming this face would be an act against the mental colonization during three hundred years under the colonizer, under whiteness. (257)

Part III: Whiteness and Milliken v. Bradley.

The mechanisms of whiteness that legitimize cultural destruction occur through power relations that privilege a certain identity. Such hegemonic tactics do not directly appear discriminatory, nor do they seem to target and punish bodies of color, but they lie hidden in the structures that make it. For example, in Milliken v. Bradley, the Court held that integration remedies could extend across district lines only where districts had purposely engaged in a policy of segregation. This decision reinforced the existing trend of "white flight" from cities to suburban school districts. The Court implicitly ignored de facto segregation that resulted from voluntary residential segregation.

This logic permeates the educational and cultural system where institutions protect the choices and cultural values of those who embody whiteness, while marginalizing the social existence of entire communities like Latinos and Mexicanos. As a result we have justified a hyper segregation where we elevate one culture—and as a result one curriculum—over the other—A curriculum that is based on Western values, English-only norms, Christian traditional values, and western scientific explanations for creation. De facto racism permeates western curriculum under the guise of “neutral standards” and “meritocracy” as it prohibits and condemns the use of Spanish by punishing students with detention, slapping hands with a ruler or being paddled and as it marginalizes alternate pedagogical possibilities, like the use of stories and readings that uphold values that are not relevant to western culture. Those who hold political and economic power portray the European image as universal and superior, disregarding our own educational contributions to math, literature, philosophy and the creation story. Carlos Aceves states that “This is detrimental for two reasons. It caused in the people a kind of cultural psychosis and inferiority complex by looking and living a brown life but having to pretend to be white” (260). We are taught to assimilate and conform to Western educational mechanisms.

The erasure of our bodies through the erasure of our cultural becoming becomes a violent act to our psyches, making us self-destructive. We are taught to believe that our bodies, our histories are not worthy sources of knowledge, even though it is what makes us. We are taught to hate that part of ourselves that will deny us success in the dominant culture. In fact, I may not be literally lashed when I dwell upon understanding my roots through the Sun Stone, but the suppression of such knowledge is a way to mummify our identity. As long as it remains mummified, we will fall prey to the psyche of dependency and low self-esteem. We begin to be embarrassed for who we are when we speak Spanglish or embrace some of our cultural traditions, like wearing guaraches or proudly saying that we are Mexican, this makes us humiliate all those who wear their identity proudly. We secretly become the hands that whip our people because that is the only way we can gain power. We whip ourselves every time we look at the Sun Stone and laugh at its meaning.

But, it is the Sun Stone that makes us. Unless we understand this ancient document and what lies in that stern face in relation to our respective lives, then we cannot achieve our own liberation. Carlos Acevedes contends that by presenting the face of the Sun Stone in the classroom it allows students to recognize their own fluidity within their own particular existence in relation to the creation story. As Acevedes states “My identification …kinship with Creation” (258).

This mythic pedagogy carried itself into the Xinachtli Project, a project created in the 1990’s by Chicana/o activists who sought to address the loss of ancestral culture in schools. The term Xinachtli is a Nahuatl word that means “seed”, within the educational context it describes the seed of culture being nurtured to grow. The goal of the program is to make Mexicanos owners of their culture[2]. As students learn to use the dates of the calendar and understand its story the sun stone becomes alive and empowering to students who critically affirm it instead of just viewing it as some static objectified artifact.

The Xinachtli Project uses teaching methods that are rooted in indigenous traditions such as using the Thahtokan or Speaking circle where students can share their experiences. The setting of the circle reminds students that ideas spoken in the circle are not “universal” truths, creating a sense of mutual respect within students. Other projects consist of placing memorable incidents and feelings in a box and setting them on fire where students are explained that traumatic emotions are released in the smoke while good emotional experiences are enhanced by the fire and sahred with the universe. The use of indigenous culture via the mythic process allows students to find empowerment within themselves and to actively use their culture as part of the critical thinking process. Without this we cannot dismantle the imposed identity of colonialism or the defacto racist tendencies that elevate a Western curriculum over our identities, but rather we would be perpetuating the internal mental colonialism under an indigenous title. Without interaction based on mutual respect and free of the burden of oppressed-oppressor relationship, people condemn themselves to a schizophrenic cultural mode (262).
Debate should be about the liberation of the oppressed. Brenda and I believe that Miliken v. Bradley in relation to our social location gives us an opportunity to denounce the way that de facto and de jure discrimination functions through systems of oppression. Nullifying the dichotomy of segregation versus integration in Miliken v. Bradley allows us to realize that systems of oppression take a larger psychological and detrimental effect in the everyday curriculum of the classroom rather than on governmental policies. We can learn from the myth of the sun stone and attempt to dismantle cultural hierarchies by adopting the concept of the fifth Sun and its people, la raza cosmica, the cosmic race, an identity that challenges binary category constructions, from race to nationality to gender to sexuality to language. The ballot becomes a stand in solidarity with adopting this mythic pedagogy and the transgressions taken by the Xinachtli project to create a space for ourselves and others.
Brenda and I take the following memories and burn the hatred that we have had towards ourselves, others and our culture; we allow the fire to embrace our souls, we willingly give this part of ourselves as a sign of representing Xinachtli—a moment of infinite possibilities.

The story of La Malinche – the woman who betrayed Mexico—who would later be called La Llorna – the Crying woman –begins in 1521 with the arrival of Herman Cortez and Catholicism. The Aztecs, believing Cortez to be their lost god Quetzalcoatle sent a peaceful welcoming with figs with 20 other women as guides. Cortes did not receive the women with the same purpose as they were sent. He assigned the women to certain men, who raped them for no reason. Cortes took Malintzin, a 14 year-old, as his concubine. Malintzin’s multilingual talents became essential to mobilize indigenous groups against the Aztecs. When Malintzin gave birth to Martin, the first mestizo child, she was considered a traitor to Mexico. Even though more indigenous men sided with Cortez, it was Malintzin who took blame and became the outcast. When Cortez was leaving for Spain he wanted to take his son. It is said that the worst thing that could happen in Aztec culture was to die away from your land. It meant that your spirit would be cast into eternal damnation. Malintzin, seeing the genocide that Cortez inflicted on her people, took her child to the lake where she killed the child.


She cried out of horror and pain to see her child float away and to see her people raped and massacred. As a result she drowned herself in the river. When she reached the gates of heaven, God told her she couldn’t enter until the soul of her child and the others were found. She is thus found wandering in the lakes, oceans and rivers in our world crying for her children.

The story of La Llorona has been inflicted upon Latino culture since the time of colonization. Our ancestors have handed it down in order to enforce Catholic values, in order to keep chains over our bodies and minds, strongly encouraging maternal duty. The pain of La Llorna inflicts scars on the bodies and souls of everyone, including ourselves. Her screech remains equally terrifying; the screech of psychological agony repeats itself in the minds and dream of all those women who betrayed their role as a mother. She is the ghost that wanders between our conscious and sexuality condemning us every time we masturbate, every time we orgasm, every time women caress other women, every time we refuse to get married, every time we don’t want to have children, every time we abort them and kill them, every time we choose our independence. La Llorona reminds us of her destiny becoming our own, becoming La Chingada – the fucked, the whores, and the bad mothers that will be refused entrance from the heavens and condemned by God. Through her story we are raised not to love ourselves; we are taught to give no meaning to our lives. The guilt that has been inflicted on us has allowed us to let others do violence against us; it has allowed us to do violence against others. We are taught not to trust each other because we don’t deserve better, we selfishly want others to be haunted by the ghost of La Llorona and so we continue to tell her story preventing us from ever uniting, understand one another.

Caught in a guilt laden, criminalized, dehumanized position as the ‘evil’ mothers that we have become the core of social disintegration, a dominant culture has taken over us for centuries. The accomplishments and miracles of La Llorona shall be recognized for having the courage to sacrifice her children for the greater good, for being capable of using mourning as a weapon to redeem the pains of guilt, and for carrying the burden of colonization.

Thus we declare and define Blessed Malintzin Tenapal, also known as La Malinche and La Llorona, as enrolled among the saints, decreeing that she is to be veneraged in the whole church of one of the saints. (Towards her image of her): In the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit…Amen.
