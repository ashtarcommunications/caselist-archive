Part I- The Genealogy of Warfare
History can trace back to the dawn of time that humans have been engaged in warfare for many, many years. Looking back at the ages of St. Augustine there has been a transgression to how we fight. Many scholars for almost every single war ask themselves why we must fight these dreadful battles. Is it for political reasons? Economic? The honor of the church? However, the million-dollar question we have yet to ask ourselves is how can we justify mass murder for an arbitrary cause? What makes certain types of warfare “lawful” or “unlawful”?
What we define to be a “just war” worth fighting is nothing but a scapegoat to claim that we are the good people in this fight. Rather not just blatantly, explain the reasons why the enemy is ultimately wrong, we condensed them to a point where they are a disembodiement from their status of life. This condenscending act toward the other is what builds hatred within ourselves for us to strike at they or we precieve to be as the “the enemy”. Analyzing to what could be the root cause of this we look no further than Westphalia thought. According to Hartigan, is a normative foundation to that of the time of St. Augustine:
“If one’s cause was just it meant that one’s enemies had not only committed a crime but a sin as well, and such should be suitably punished.”
However, can we really say that war can be justified within our moral system? The idealogical system we utilize to disembody the other in order for us to categorize them to be the enemy who we must take action and fight. Though we CONTINUE, to use this ideological paradigm. For us to justify the mass killings of almost millions of people, a “war ethic” is that we use which permits us to inflict BLOODSHED amongst others.
As a result, we have now come to an acceptance to what is lawful and unlawful warfare. Coming into terms to what this normative paradigm causes the legitmization of what we define to be a “the innocent killing of others.”
Part II- The Status Quo of Things
The Supreme Court decision on Ex Parte Quirin defines an “unlawful combatant” as those who during a time of war pass surreptitiously from enemy territory into our own, discarding their uniforms upon entry, for the commission of hostile acts involving destruction of life or property. Post-campaign, searching for those who are against the democratic system and us. According to Slavoj Zizek he states:
“When Donald Rumsfeld designated the imprisoned Taliban fighters ‘unlawful combatants’ (as opposed to ‘regular’ prisoners of war), he did not simply mean that their criminal terrorist activity placed them outside the law: when an American citizen commits a crime, even one as serious as murder, he remains a ‘lawful criminal’.”
Zizek describes that this war the Bush Administration is engaged in is part of a “sociological matrix”. Capturing those who we define as the enemy and transporting them to areas where they are caught captured and stripped from their human status of life . What we observe here is what Michael Foucault would describe to what legitimizes the very atrocities of life to occur . Let us take “the bomb” as an example. Because of justifying to what is lawful combat, we find ways out of these paradigms to justify what we are doing is the right thing. Country X does not like our democratic system therefore we should terminate them through nuclear weaponry! That will teach them! So what then do? Do we continue to accept such an ideology and continue to find different methodologies to entangle ourselves from the very double helix that we created? We, the Affirmative, just say no!
Thus, we affirm:
That the United States Supreme Court should overrule Ex Parte Quirin on the basis that the terminology of “unlawful” and “lawful” combatants does not exist.
Part III- The Conclusion
This action towards the Supreme Court is what Nietzsche describes to be “self-overcoming”. Daniel W. Conway helps interprets Nietzsche’s theory of self-overcoming to be:
“…because the promise of one’s “next” self arises only in the practice of one’s refusal of one’s current self, the project of moral perfectionism is both risky and indeterminate, Those who set out on the path of self-overcoming are obliged to raze their old “homes” before new ones are yet in sight. Yet is it precisely the prospect of this “homelessness” that confers upon moral perfectionism it peculiar urgency and attraction…”
When we come into terms that war is nothing but a gaping hole that must be left in it’s own realm, only then we a start a new path to progressing. After all, is it not true that we drive ourselves to become better as a person? If it is moral perfectionism that we are striving for, then it requires taking large leaps. It is what Conway previously describes that we must raze our old homes before we build brand new ones. Being “homeless” is what opens our eyes to the deeds we have done in the 2’
past we inflicted on our old houses and realize what NOT to do when we build our new ones. “The genius must not fear to enter into the most hostile relationship with the existing forms and order is he wants to bring to light the higher order and truth that dwells within him.”
