Round after round we return to framing politics as a relationship between multiple people mediated by the centrally important figure of the law. How does this law come into being? What sustains our practices of the law and their relationship to legal violence?

Politics appears when we see the world in terms of interactions between different people. Any interaction comes out of an initial moment of confrontation where two individuals recognize each other as subjects. What we really want is to be subjects - that is, recognized by (an)other. But to be subjects we have to be subjected to a common condition - call this 3rd figure “the law”. We make the law intervene so that we have a consistent mechanism to relate to others. The law is supposed to do away with the anxiety that the other may not be knowable and controlling and replace this anxiety with a clear image of friend or enemy that we can at least integrate into our depiction of reality.

We think that the affirmative operates to insert the law in such a way that it can supplement equality by mediating our relationships with others in terms of identity and citizenship.

Peter Gabel. May 1984
[ A Critique of Rights: The Phenomenology of Rights-Consciousness and the Pact of the Withdrawn Selves., 62 Tex. L. Rev. 1563 ]
Although the law has a positive dimension to which I will return later, in its negative
An idealized object begins to stand in for the actual other. ....Loving the other begins to define politics, but this type of love treats the other as an object, and politics turns back on itself in narcissism, producing the desire to sacrifice freedom for order.

Wendy Brown, Prof @ UC Berkeley, 2006
[Regulating Aversion: Tolerance in the Age of Identity and Empire]


The law brings us together by alienating us, producing quietude, passivity and submission while enabling state violence....
The affirmative representations of politics reduces the political to identity and the negotiation between isolated, “free”, “autonomous” individuals. A politics of ontology and predictability takes over, treating others as objects, like marionettes on a stage.

Wendy Brown, Prof @ UC Berkeley, 2006
[Regulating Aversion: Tolerance in the Age of Identity and Empire]
The affirmative expresses an attempt to regulate social violence, but this search for control produces isolated individuals (call them “subjects”) whose anxiety can only be resolved through surveillance of each other and themselves....
