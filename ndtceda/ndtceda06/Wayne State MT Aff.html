In the US Supreme Court’s decision in Milliken v Bradley it was held that rulings which proposed a multi-district solution to school segregation were based on “erroneous standards” which were “unsupported by evidence” asserting that outlining districts certainly could not be responsible for discrimination that existed, that still exists, within the Detroit Public School system. But what is it that defines these districts? Is the boundary between Detroit Public Schools and the Grosse Pointe or Birmingham School systems really just a line in the sand, or is it a boundary that has been established over the concepts of agency? The decision in Milliken attempts to wash away the central questions of participation, discrimination and access to life itself.
Driving into Detroit from any direction is an experience, it’s almost surreal as you notice a slow transition from wealth to despair. Burned out buildings, rows of blight exponentially increase as you make your journey to the core of the city. This journey, this historical path towards poverty and discrimination shapes our very existence in Detroit. The Supreme Court’s decision in Milliken v. Bradley ignores the power of education, culture and societal history in shaping our understandings of ourselves and of Others. But the discourse of the Supreme Court, the blight that exists around our home does not have to be the story of Detroit. A city that experiences discrimination does not have to be separated from the larger questions of ontological boundaries which establish unequal access and distribution of education. Rejecting the separation of Detroit from the world around it allows us to inhabit cracks and fissures in the dominant discourse of Detroit, of our very agency.

Bleiker, School of Political Science, University of Queensland, Brisbane, 2003
(Roland, Contemporary Political Theory page proquest gjm)

Despite their power to frame the world, … more abstract insights into the question of Being?
Detroit is portrayed as decaying, destroyed, dilapidated and depressing, but there are signs of life within this cityscape. Everyday mobile subjectivities have created pockets of resistance, from street art to performance, even act as simple as seeing beauty in a landscape that has been separated and forgotten by forces larger then itself. Vote affirmative to engage in a politics of everyday life. The 1AC has created a space for you to inhabit and resist the separation and linearity of the supreme courts decision in Milliken v. Bradley – you should engage in this act of resistance because our politics – our space for activism – is a temporal embrace of the concept of politics. Affirm agency, affirm Detroit even if only for this moment even if only in the signing of the ballot.

Bleiker, School of Political Science, University of Queensland, Brisbane, 2003
(Roland, Contemporary Political Theory page proquest GJM)

More layers of abstraction must be removed … public transcripts is torn apart (Scott, 1990, 19-20, 183-227).

How do we inhabit the space left by the dominant discourse? Should we call on the United States Supreme Court to overrule its decision in Milliken v. Bradley? We ultimately say no, the call to the state to engage in revolution for our agency is a project which always fails. Constant simulation is destructive to the cornerstones of our existence. We instead view the 1AC, this very moment, as a political act which refuses the state, and instead inhabits a discursive space which refuses to be co-opted our return to the Real in the form of violent martyrdom
