We live in an era filled with the greatest amount of violence in history. We are constantly confronted with loss and vulnerability. But, who can we grieve for? What constitutes a grievable life? These questions appeal to something beyond the minutiae of technical policy-making, but rather speak to the very condition of life itself. At every moment of everyday, we are vulnerable, we are exposed to the other. This vulnerability threatens us to fall apart. CLS

The 1AC frames the use of the court as the bulwark against oppression, the guarantor of rights and privileges entitled to and individual or class of people. This desire to deploy the law as arbiter of claims covers over the lack of foundation inherent in law. Our fear of disorder creates the desire to establish order by any means possible. The 1AC’s claims to garner advantages are nothing but a post-hoc rationalization of the desire for order. It’s like pretending to have a chicken because you need the eggs.
Harrison and Mashburn – 1989 (Jeff and Amy, Prof. of Law @ U. of Florida and Practicing Lawyer, “ESSAY: JEAN-LUC GODARD AND CRITICAL LEGAL STUDIES (BECAUSE WE NEED THE EGGS”, Michigan Law Review, 87)
“The central problem… most of us need the eggs.”

Some desires seem harmless enough, like the desire to work out in the morning or eat your breakfast. However, denying the lack of foundation at the heart of law makes it seem natural and inevitable, even though the law is wholly indeterminate, whimsical, and unstable. Legitimating the law in this fashion solidifies existing power relations, making them seem invulnerable to change and guaranteeing the perpetuation of inequity.
Harrison and Mashburn – 1989 (Jeff and Amy, Prof. of Law @ U. of Florida and Practicing Lawyer, “ESSAY: JEAN-LUC GODARD AND CRITICAL LEGAL STUDIES (BECAUSE WE NEED THE EGGS”, Michigan Law Review, 87)
