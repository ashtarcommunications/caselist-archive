1AC
Gender-motivated violence is a crisis that affects various individuals differently, for some individuals escaping gender-motivated violence may be easy but for others it is a struggle that is reinforced by culture. The Asian American Community is barely making its way into literature surrounding gender-motivated violence and its induction to the literature is alarming. Women within Asian/Asian-American communities experience various forms of violence such as psychological, like husbands threatening their wives with a butcher knife, or sexual aggression which consists of a man forcing his wife to have sex against her will. When women accept these acts of violence as the norm of their roles, they also begin to internalize patriarchy that is manifested through the acts of violence. The violence is internalized with this acceptance. With each act of violence, her worth as a human bring is diminished as it is intertwined with each act of acceptance. This internalized violence is compounded with the fact that at a young age an Asian woman is taught to be submissive to the male in the family; this submission normalizes the violence that is inflicted on women. The violence becomes magnified when the silence of one generation allows for the furthering of violence in the next. The combination of these factors only leads to a cycle of violence, where “…relatively minor forms of physical violence serve only to reinforce the ongoing strategy of intimidation, isolation and control that extends to all areas of a woman's life, including sexuality; material necessities; relations with family, children and friends; and work." It is in the escalation of violence that death becomes a possibility which perpetuates a femicidal culture where women become expandable and disposable. This violence is circulating through the community and it struggles for visibility. It struggles for visibility because the Asian American Community is cloaked in a silence.
The Asian culture dictates that a woman is always subservient to the male within the family. A woman is obedient to her father, then to her husband, and finally to her son. This is the role that an Asian woman is socialized to accept. Another component to this structure is the collectivist attitude the family is built around. Within the Asian culture, unlike Western culture, there is a great emphasis on a collectivist approach to social relations. According to Alice G. Yick “The emphasis on family and its hierarchical structure provides a context for understanding domestic violence in Chinese immigrant families. When the family is the foundation of social relations and provides a sense of identity and belonging, victims may be reluctant to disclose incidents of abuse for fear of being ostracized for having betrayed the family…” For women within the Asian/Asian American community silence is induced by fear, silence that keeps the violence unopposed and justified. Yick refers to this way of life as “Saving Face,” a woman must forget in order to avoid any disgrace the family may endure from such disclosure. For “…many Chinese American women, to expose their husbands’ abuse would mean “loss of face”-disgrace and loss or respect, for the entire family and ancestral lineage.” This act of saving face “a woman is to deny her own well-being and make personal sacrifices for the sake of the family honor and well-being. These “obligations” construct a double oppression for Asian/Asian American women where choices become limited. But how far must a woman “save face” when her life hangs in the balance?
In September of 1986, Dong Lu Chen and his wife, Jian Wan Chen immigrated from China to New York. While Dong worked as a dishwasher in Maryland, Jian stayed with their three children in New York. During this time, problems began to occur within the marriage such as Jian refusing to have sex with Dong on one occasion which lead to Dong to believe his wife was having an affair. Once Jian admitted to the affair, Dong took a claw hammer and smashed into Jian’s head eight times. Dong’s attorney argued that “Chen lacked the requisite state of mind for murder and involuntary manslaughter because [his] culture made it reasonable for him to perceive and to respond to the situation in a violent way…” Orden would further his argument that this murder would have been prevented in China because the community would have stopped Dong. This strategy was the “cultural defense.” This defense would prompt the presiding judge to conclude that “Chen was the product of his culture. . . . The culture was never an excuse, but it is something that made him crack more easily. That was the factor, the cracking factor.” Chen would be convicted of second-degree manslaughter: reckless homicide without intent, this sentence would allow Chen to walk out of jail as a free man.
U.S. v. Chen exemplifies the moment when culture becomes monolithic and determines our agency as people. Culture should not be seen as totalizing and impermeable. It is possible to transgress the restrictions that Asian culture places on women. Even though Jian was killed by her husband, her act of resisting her socialized role proves that women within the Asian culture are not paralyzed by these restrictions. Her transgression shows that culture, especially Asian culture, is not homogeneous where every individual follows strictly within their roles, it is heterogeneous and varied. This frame of culture should be adopted as a strategy to counteract the monolithic nature of culture. By adopting this strategy on the discursive level, it becomes possible to oppose culture’s oppressive components.
Our methodology views the body as a site for political resistance. In 1955, Emmett Till was killed for allegedly flirting with a white woman. What is significant about Emmett Till was that his body would symbolize resistance to the racism that was apparent in the country at the time. His body became that symbol when “By moving Emmett Till’s corpse from a muddy river bottom in the Mississippi Delta to a public exhibition in urban Chicago, Mamie Till Bradley transformed her son from a victim of white racism to an unforgettable symbol that mobilized a generation of activists. It was a “public exhibition” when Emmett’s mother refused to have the mortician fix Emmett’s face and had the casket open for all to see a body inflicted with racial violence. This act allowed witnesses, in a kind of visual communion, to consume the body of Emmett Till and be transformed by doing so. According to Harold and DeLuca, “that regimes create bodies in pain to make real their power and to anchor their ideological belief systems.” This ideology holds true for the body of an Asian/Asian American woman whose body is beaten when she tries to transcend these “ideological belief systems.” The act of violence becomes the strategy of keeping the woman in her position. Harold and DeLuca continue with their analysis that the body that has had violence inflicted upon it forces individuals to come to terms with the violence. Instead of being submerged in silence, the body of the Asian/Asian American women needs to be seen. Harold and DeLuca add that, “The image, like the open casket in Chicago, became a gathering place, a temporary nexus around which people could link themselves to each other in a new network, thus reconfiguring their agency in powerful ways.” The body of a woman who has been inflicted with violence can act as a symbol and mobilize others to fight against gender violence. This round acts as our open casket.
The resolution serves as a site that induces a focused discussion on the gender violence that happens within the Asian/Asian American community. The power of the Supreme Court is not entrapped within the nine robes that sit on the bench nor is it inscribed into a piece of paper, but is within the hands of the people. The Supreme Court is a symbol of power that can be used by the oppressed to create political possibilities for their liberation. The decision of U.S v. Morrison placed women on the margins of economies of power, we overrule by moving gender motivated violence to the center of power via a display of the body in violence. Debate should be about the liberation of the oppressed. Education should be accessible and relevant to the social realities of the oppressed. Theory must not be separated from action; our framework is the best for debate because it forces us to come to grips with the bounds of our agency and the privilege and use it for the liberation of the oppressed. We engage in praxis through our methodology of opening the casket and displaying the body in violence.

Fullerton MM 1AC Summary


We begin with the story of La Malinche, a woman taken as a concubine under Cortez, during the time of Aztec colination. Out of guilt from “betraying her culture” she killed her son, and ended up committing suicide. When going to Heaven, God excluded her from entering, telling her that she was to find the soul of her child.

The story of La Llorona has been passed down in our culture as a way of forcing guilt onto women, making them dislocated and psychologically traumatized. Latina women become psychologically displaced in most of their everyday actions and cultural encounters.

We connect this experience to how the Supreme Court, the Catholic Church, and society has produced the same form of alienation by not understanding the contextual reasons why women have abortions. We believe Planned Parenthood v. Casey becomes a lynchpin where conservative and women’s movements framework continue to essentialize, misunderstand the needs of womyn. Still, norms degrade women and further inscribe internalized racism, sexism, homophobia, etc.

Our recognition of this cultural phenomenon allows us to call for a cultural mourning—a process of transgression that changes the way women are forced into guilt, and the Supreme Court’s current view of women as inferior.

Mourning stems from the act of canonization—we, therefore, canonize La Malinche, La Llorona as an empowering symbol within our culture for liberation from psychological denigration.

The story of La Malinche – the woman who betrayed Mexico—who would later be called La Llorna – the Crying woman –begins in 1521 with the arrival of Herman Cortez and Catholicism. The Aztecs, believing Cortez to be their lost god Quetzalcoatle sent a peaceful welcoming with figs with 20 other women as guides. Cortes did not receive the women with the same purpose as they were sent. He assigned the women to certain men, who raped them for no reason. Cortes took Malintzin, a 14 year-old, as his concubine. Malintzin’s multilingual talents became essential to mobilize indigenous groups against the Aztecs. When Malintzin gave birth to Martin, the first mestizo child, she was considered a traitor to Mexico. Even though more indigenous men sided with Cortez, it was Malintzin who took blame and became the outcast. When Cortez was leaving for Spain he wanted to take his son. It is said that the worst thing that could happen in Aztec culture was to die away from your land. It meant that your spirit would be cast into eternal damnation. Malintzin, seeing the genocide that Cortez inflicted on her people, took her child to the lake where she killed the child.

She cried out of horror and pain to see her child float away and to see her people raped and massacred. As a result she drowned herself in the river. When she reached the gates of heaven, God told her she couldn’t enter until the soul of her child and the others were found. She is thus found wandering in the lakes, oceans and rivers in our world crying for her children.

The story of La Llorona has been inflicted upon Latino culture since the time of colonization. Our ancestors have handed it down in order to enforce Catholic values, in order to keep chains over our bodies and minds, strongly encouraging maternal duty. The pain of La Llorna inflicts scars on the bodies and souls of everyone, including ourselves. Her screech remains equally terrifying; the screech of psychological agony repeats itself in the minds and dream of all those women who betrayed their role as a mother. She is the ghost that wanders between our conscious and sexuality condemning us every time we masturbate, every time we orgasm, every time women caress other women, every time we refuse to get married, every time we don’t want to have children, every time we abort them and kill them, every time we choose our independence. La Llorona reminds us of her destiny becoming our own, becoming La Chingada – the fucked, the whores, and the bad mothers that will be refused entrance from the heavens and condemned by God. Through her story we are raised not to love ourselves; we are taught to give no meaning to our lives. The guilt that has been inflicted on us has allowed us to let others do violence against us; it has allowed us to do violence against others. We are taught not to trust each other because we don’t deserve better, we selfishly want others to be haunted by the ghost of La Llorona and so we continue to tell her story preventing us from ever uniting, understand one another.

And yet, for centuries Latina women have transgressed their gender role by practicing abortion as a means to escape socio-economic problems, psychological terror, and in order to protect themselves or their child. Yet for too long the women’s movement and conservatives have exhibited whiteness and ignored the cultural dimensions that abortion carries to Latina women. The woman’s movement having claimed victory in Roe v Wade ignored that the only women available to choose were women of privilege. Conservatives believe that they protect Latino populations by protecting the fetus and upholding Catholic values. Planned Parenthood v Casey has become the linchpin of both of these frameworks. Women with language barriers who have to go through the informed consent process are not only heavily disadvantaged in terms of communication, but are forced to expose and re-explain their decision for an abortion ignoring that his process already creates internal psychological trauma. Despite the promise to make abortion accessible the current system ignores and dismisses the psychological are not women of color, they are not familiar with cultural norms and instead essentialize Latina cultures into one. Simply, the neutral programs are not accessible to womyn who don’t speak English fluently and attempt to use scientific explanations to prove how an abortion affects a group of cells and not a child. But for man Latina women it doesn’t matter if we were raped, if we are too poor, if we want to protect ourselves, we are carried on with an insurmountable cultural guilt. Caught in a guilt laden, criminalized, dehumanized position as the ‘evil’ mothers that we have become the core of social disintegration, a dominant culture has taken over us for centuries. Colonization drives us, producing inward-hate, and therefore, internalized racism, sexism, homophobia!

But…our identity and psychological dilemma is also what gives us meaning and possibilities of subverting the cultural norms imposed on us. According to Kathleen Borgan our stories of cultural haunting, like La Llorona, suggests that ethnicity is a function of memory, it is an attempt for cultures to conjure up ghosts and learn from ancestral ghost, to recreate identities into culture. La Llorona has become the identity given to Latina women who have made the choice to abort. Regardless, the church, making them sinners who will burn for eternity, condemns their decision. As the sinners and the condemned, we recognize that La Llorona was never given a proper burial or given forgiveness. La Llorona is the cultural ghost that has become our destiny. We live with this psychological oppression because our culture has betrayed and silenced our women. Our culture’s Christianity has become the new Cortez. As Gloria Anzaldua speaks in Boderlands “It was Not me sold out my people, but they sold me…the worst kind of betrayal lies in making us believe that the Indian woman in us is the betrayer…For 300 years she was invisible, she was not heard. Brenda and I believe that we can transform this cultural haunting by canonizing La Llorona. Canonization can grant us a cultural transgression of the haunting that has promulgated a deeply entrenched psychological dislocation and trauma. Through this we can transform the powerful image of Christianity in Latino culture as a tool of empowerment for women. Making Malintzin, La Llorona, a saint allows us to use her wailings as a way to properly mourn our guilt as well as to mourn the children that have been aborted . As Anzaldua continues “The Aztecs female rites of mourning were rites of defiance of protesting the cultural changes which disrupted the equality and balance between female and male, and protesting their demotion to a lesser status, their denigration.” We believe debate should be about the liberation of the oppressed. The ballot represents an of solidarity with canonizing La Llorona as a possibility of transgression.
