Our advocacy is beneficial for a couple of reasons:

First, our advocacy strives to ensure that equality and acceptance
become founding principals of debate. We think that the best way to
achieve this is not to debate over which styles are intrinsically
“better” or “worse” for underrepresented groups, but rather to encourage
an ethic of argumentative tolerance that transcends the boundaries and
limitations that particular groups seek to place on debate.

Second, you can think of our advocacy as inclusive of Towson’s, but
without the baggage of having to shut down political spaces and debate
practices that are deemed to be “intrinsically incompatible” with the
purpose of furthering tolerance. Our advocacy doesn’t jettison any
component of debate, and instead prefers to leave open the spaces of
political activism that Towson’s advocacy eliminates, like engagement
with the State or certain debate-related practices like evidence focus.
Any attempt to “do both” Towson and Michigan’s advocacies would be
incompatible and fail because Towson’s advocacy starts with the argument
that we should throw out certain practices – we say that there should
/never/ be a point where we make the decision to simply exclude a
component of debate.
