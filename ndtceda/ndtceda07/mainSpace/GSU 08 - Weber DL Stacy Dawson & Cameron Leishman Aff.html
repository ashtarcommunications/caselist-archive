Observation One: Idle Hands

First,
current political discussions about agriculture take place in a world framed and defined by human power. Agricultural products, naturally occurring parts of creation, are converted into another cog in the machine of human manufacture where raw materials are converted into processed goods by humans for the consumption of humans.
Brewer in 2007 (Joe [Staff, Rockridge Institute] “The Frame, The Farm, The Harm”, July 27, 2007, **http://www.rockridgenation. org/blog/archive/2007/07/27/ the-frame-the-farm-the-harm**)

And, this definition of the world in terms of human manufacture and control is rooted in a theological problem. Categories like politics, nature and the economy have been carved out of the unity of God’s creation to stand as naturalized objects by secularism. This power over the entire world as objects we control hijacks the Christian truth of a covenantal and peaceful relationship between God, humans and nature, transforming human dominion into the right to exert power over nature in whatever way we choose.
Smith in 2004 (James KA [Prof Philo @ Villanova] Introducing Radical Orthodoxy, 100-101)

And, the subsidization of CAFOs is emblematic of the results of human freedom from God’s covenant. Farms are transformed into sunless hells filled with miserable, violent and traumatized animals awaiting a mechanical death. Subsidies are crucial. Providing the underlying possibility for the new “Science” of raising livestock.
Scully in 2006 (Matthew [Fmr. Advisor, Governor of Arizona, “A Sunless Hell” Arizona Republic, February 19)

And, this economic rationality justifies every atrocity. The earth and all its creatures become expendable trash. The final triumph of neoliberalism is a world where all human institutions, foreign policy, the military, science and finance are fused into a single totalitarian entity engaged in constant war, ecocide, racism, patriarchal violence and destruction in order to guarantee growth. The very possibility of human freedom and choice will be eradicated as we are enslaved by the economic and consumer demands of this monster.
Goodchild in 2004 (Philip [Prof, Philosophy of Religion; University of Nottingham] Debt, Epistemology and Ecotheology, Ecotheology 9.2)
OBSERVATION TWO LITURGY AS POLITICS AND POLITICS AS LITURGY

We begin with a doxological praise of God’s mercy and power we pray the Magnificat
(Luke 1:46-55)

Plan: The United States Federal Government should substantially reduce its agricultural support by eliminating all domestic direct and indirect agricultural subsidies for Concentrated Animal Feeding Operations. Funding and enforcement guaranteed.

And, we offer plan up to God trusting that he will reverse the injustices and sufferings of this world. Rather than claiming absolute security we embrace our vulnerability and weakness. We are able to abandon liberalism’s economic and security obsessed vision of the world for one that privileges justice for the oppressed in all places and all times.
Jones in 2004 (Patricia [Director, CAFOD] “Turning the World Upside Down” May 9, 2004
__**http://www.ely.anglican.org/ parishes/camgsm/sermons/ S2004e/pj1_sermon.html**__)

And, this approach of Christian witness and hope in God’s providence is the only way to navigate the inevitability of human violence towards each other and creation. The current responses either justify violence because of it’s inevitability or feed into the false theology of human control and manufacture by believing that we can solve violence through a change in ideology or a comprehensive policy approach.
Williams in 2005 (Scott [Prof Philo and Ethics @ Waynesbury College] Christian Century, May 31, 2005, __**http://www.religion-online. org/showarticle.asp?title=3215**__ )

And, our witness to God’s absolute dominion over the world and history doesn’t require or lead to a withdrawal from politics, nor does it lead to the idolatrous worship of human agency that secular policymaking is plagued by. It frees us to radically engage politics in every forum and with every means at our disposal freed from any fear of death or consequence.
Stringfellow in 64 (William, Freedom in Obedience, pp. 38-39)

Last, Only liturgy can rupture the false universalism of the liberal project and replace it with a vision of all humanity’s universal journey towards our ultimate home in God. This allows local spaces and cultures to be seen as continuous with orthodox faith without violently destroying them. Liturgical enactment allows us to transform life into a pilgrimage where each moment is sacred. For us, each tournament destination becomes the Holy Land of our enactment and each round is a bead on the rosary. Every time we enact Christ’s hope and demand we change the world.
Pickstock in 98 (Catherine [Prof Theology @ Emmanuel College, Cambridge] “Liturgy and Modernity” Telos, Fall, 1998)
