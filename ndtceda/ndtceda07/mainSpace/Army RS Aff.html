Part two:

1. An explicit nuclear no-first-use policy would resolve the inherent contradictions of US nuclear posture and enable a global shift away from the dominant nuclearist mindset


Mendelsohn 06 (former member of the US SALT and START delegations, deputy director of the Arms Control Association, and prof. At GWU, “Delegitimizing nuclear weapons,” Issues in Science & Technology, March, http://findarticles.com/p/articles/mi_qa3622/is_200604/ai_n17175669/pg_1)

2. A no first-use policy is the most effective first step in normalizing relations with Iran, and would set an international precedent against nuclear first use


Sagan 06 (Scott D., Professor of Political Science and Director of the Center for International Security and Cooperation at Stanford University, “How to Keep the Bomb from Iran,” Foreign Affairs, vol. 85, issue 5, Sept/Oct 06, p. 48-49)

3. The idea of the “nuclear advantage” is embedded in government policy. We must dispel this illusion in order to be free to see the elimination of nuclear weapons as in the ultimate best interest of the world. This creates the possibility for true disarmament and an ultimate return to sanity


Lifton and Falk 82 (Robert, Professor of Psychiatry and Psychology at John Jay College and CUNY, and Rihard, Professor of International Law and Practice, Politics, and International Affairs at Princeton, Indefensible Weapons)

4. An official no-first-use policy would be easy to implement and supervise, would undermine imperialist power projection through nuclear hegemony, and would force entrenched military planners to reconsider the “importance” of nuclear weapons in strategic planning – it is the most direct and effective way to challenge nuclearism


Lifton and Falk 82 (Robert, Professor of Psychiatry and Psychology at John Jay College and CUNY, and Rihard, Professor of International Law and Practice, Politics, and International Affairs at Princeton, Indefensible Weapons)



5. To start, the United States must take realistic steps to break out of the habits and practices and move past the Cold War ideology of enormous arsenals, nuclear threats, and aggressive deterrence. The United States government must reconsider its nuclear policies and accept that nuclear weapons are morally indefensible. Most importantly, it is up to individuals debating and supporting the necessary practical steps required to break the global addiction to these dangerous practices that are key to creating a post-nuclearist world.

Butler 96 [General Lee Butler USAF Retd. October 3, 1996. “Chaining the Nuclear Beast” in a speech given by General Butler at the State of the World Forum]

Butler 99 [General Lee Butler USAF Retd. March 11, 1999. “Are Nuclear Weapons necessary?” in INESAP]
