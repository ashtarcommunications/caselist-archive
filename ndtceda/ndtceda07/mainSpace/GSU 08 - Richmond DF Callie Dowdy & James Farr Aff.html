Contention One: The Topic

1. The United States' status quo policy of
licensing the operation of concentrated animal
feeding operations exists under a strict
environmental regulatory regime. This regulatory
regime inscribes calculable ways to manage the
earth and its inhabitants via standards and
previsions outlined by the Clean Water Act. All
of this occurs despite the EPA's blatant
admission that almost half of our water supply
is poisonous.
EPA, 2008. [Environmental Protection Agency,
National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System
Permit Regulation and Effluent Limitation
Guidelines and Standards for Concentrated Animal
Feeding Operations (CAFOs); Final Rule," Federal
Register, Vol. 68, No. 29, February 12, p.
7176.]

"Today’s final rule revises and clarifies …
range of tools to assist small and medium-size
AFOs."

2. The EPA's ceaseless revision of failed policy
is an element of management like none else -
guilt as management technology. They assume a
technological enframing of the environment that
can only ensure the continued domination of the
technological will over the earth. This will has
obscured the problem and solution alike – they
are merely coordinates in an endless circulation
of standing reserve in which everything can be
planned or replaced.
Ladelle McWhorter, 1992, forthcoming. [my
philosophy professor at the University of
Richmond, "Guilt as Management Technology: A
Call to Heideggerian Reflection," in Heidegger
and the Earth, 2nd edition, University of
Toronto Press, actually 2009, pp. 16-31.]

"Guilt is a standard defense …people have become
resources, human resources, personnel to be
managed, or populations to be controlled."


3. The focal point of this managerial control
oscillates between a man/animal distinction on
which the very foundation of what it means to be
human is constructed. Our refusal to think
animality in its "essence" - a calculative
mastery of animality - has produced our modern
condition of nihilism.
Jeremy Bendik-Keymer, 2004. [Professor of
Philosophy at Colorado College, "Openness and
Meaning," in Humanities and Social Sciences,
February 2004, p. 1-3.]

"Western metaphysics has predicated what it is
to be human…might be an example of the kind of
non-technological use-relation he has in mind."


Contention Two: Resolved

Resolved: The United States Federal Government
should revoke all permits for Concentrated
Animal Feeding Operations.

4. This is not a typical plan of action, our
will to be implemented or enacted—rather our
resolve represents an ontological openness to
thinking animality in its essence. Resolution is
not to be found in decision or action, rather it
lies in opening oneself, in thought, toward the
expanse of being.
Jeremy Bendik-Keymer, 2004. [Professor of
Philosophy at Colorado College, "Openness and
Meaning," in Humanities and Social Sciences,
February 2004, p. 4.]

"Indeed, how could we, of any imagination,
accept factory farming…what we lose when we let
ourselves live callous lives."


5. Our affirmative does not simply "do nothing."
This thinking the resolution, rather than
willing the resolution, is an invitation of
paradox - a simultaneous acting-but-not-acting.
Only within this paradox can we inaugurate a
manner of thought that evades technology and the
destruction that it entails. Thinking takes on a
new direction in the absence of a guiding
will—its trajectory is outward into the openness
of Being. Only such a fundamental openness can
orient us appropriately toward Being and
animality.
Ladelle McWhorter, 1992, forthcoming. [my
philosophy professor at the University of
Richmond, "Guilt as Management Technology: A
Call to Heideggerian Reflection," in Heidegger
and the Earth, 2nd edition, University of
Toronto Press, actually 2009, pp. 16-31.]

"Heidegger's work is a call to
reflect…directions of which we may never be able
to dream."
