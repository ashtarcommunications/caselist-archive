The tree was the only citrus tree in the garden that had a fruit on it. Gabriel had taken some pains to plant the tree to mark the Tropic of Cancer. A line. An imaginary line girdling the globe and separating North from South at the northernmost latitude reached by the overhead sun. Rafaela didn’t think much about Gabriel’s fascination with an imaginary line, but she knew instinctively the importance of the surviving orange. In the days when it was a blossom of soft petals, she noticed a line—finer than the thread of a spiderweb—pulled with delicate tautness. Perhaps it was something like a laser beam or light passing through an optic fiber. When the baby orange appeared, it seemed to grasp that line as its parent, if a line could be a parent. And when the salty wind blew west from the sea rocking it back and forth like a small cradle, the curious line—now running through the growing orange—rocked back and forth with it like a lullaby. The orange fell irresistibly from a height of two meters, rolling down a small slope to a neutral place between the ownership and the highway. An old man carrying his suitcase along the highway toward the bus stop came upon the orange. He stopped, opened his suitcase, and put the orange in one corner. As he boarded the bus, the strands of the line extended from the orange, reaching out of the suitcase and slipping across the bus, through the windows and across the land. The bus pulled away from stop, starting its journey from the small city just north of Mazatlan to the US-Mexico border. On either side of the bus, the landscape was continually familiar, as if they were moving but not moving. Tired from five hundred years of travel, the old man closed his eyes for a long moment. He could see again the woman who sold him the nopales in the plaza,
Haitian farmers burning and slashing cane,
workers stirring molasses into white gold,
Guatemalans loading trucks with crates of bananas and corn,

He saw the mother in Idaho peeling a banana for her child,
lines of laborers gripping soiled paychecks at the local bank.
Everybody’s labor got occupied in the industry of draining their homeland of its natural wealth.
In exchange, they got progress,
technology, loans, and loaded guns.
Trade balances and stock market figures.
Negatives and positives.
A 28 billion dollar trade deficit?
Devaluate the peso.
A miracle!
No more debt for the country. Instead
personal debt for all its people.[1]
This exploitation depends on the naturalized divide between the Global North and the Global South. The North continually protects its own position, claiming that these protections benefit everyone. This dynamic is epitomized in the relationship between the United States and Mexico, in which an interlocking system of free trade and corn subsidies enables American agribusiness to flood Mexico with underpriced corn. Stripped of their livelihood and forced off their land, millions of displaced Mexican farmers migrate to the US, where they are further exploited as day laborers. Michael Pollan[2] in 2004 explains:

It waited with seismic sensors and thermal imaging,

They held the border to his throat like a great knife: What is your name? How old are you? Where were you born? Where is your vista? Your passport? Are you carrying any fruit or vegetables?
The old man tried to respond, but his voice was swallowed up by the waves of floating paper money: a graceful movement of free capital carried across by hidden and cheap labor. Hundreds of thousands of the unemployed surged forward. Then came the corn and the sugar cane, the music and its rhythms, the halls of Moctezuma and all 40,000 Aztecs slain, the burned and strangled body of the Incan king Atahualpa, the spirit of ideologies thought to be dead and of the dead themselves. Everything and everybody got in lines—citizens and aliens—the great undocumented foment, the Third World War, the gliding wings of a dream. Mi casa es su casa. Mi tierra es su tierra. Mi mundo es su mundo.
