Karen Halverson Cross, Professor of Law John Marshall Law School, 1-3-06 King Cotton, Developing Countries and the ‘Peace Clause’: The WTO’s US Cotton Subsidies Decision. Journal of International Economic Law 2006. http://jiel.oxfordjournals.org/cgi/content/full/9/1/14

Because a basic familiarity with the measures at issue is necessary to understanding the decision in US Cotton Subsidies, set forth below is a description of the programs at issue in the dispute31:
1. Marketing loan payments
Farmers use cotton crops as collateral for a loan from the US Department of Agriculture (USDA)’s Commodity Credit Corporation (CCC).32 When the world price for cotton falls below a certain threshold (the ‘loan rate’),33 the borrower can repay the loan at the lower price and retain the difference.
2. Direct payments34
Direct payments are an example of decoupled income support, in the sense that the level of subsidy is (at least in principle) unrelated either to market price fluctuations or to levels of production. As such, these payments are viewed as less trade-distorting than other types of subsidies.35 The 2002 Farm Bill set direct payments for upland cotton at 6.67 cents per pound.36 Payments generally are pegged to fixed, ‘historical’ production levels.37 Although recipients of direct payments are not obligated to plant cotton, the 2002 Farm Bill prohibits recipients from planting fruit, vegetables, or wild rice.38
3. Counter-cyclical payments and other emergency assistance
Counter-cyclical payments were established by the 2002 Farm Bill, for the same producers who receive direct payments.39 Payments are designed to protect cotton farmers against a decline in cotton prices and so are triggered when the market price for cotton falls below a target price.40 Although the payments are linked to market price fluctuations, they are not linked to current production levels. As with the direct payments program, counter-cyclical payments are made on the basis of historical cotton production levels.41 In addition to counter-cyclical payments, during the period at issue in US Cotton Subsidies, Congress authorized ad hoc emergency payments to producers to offset low commodity prices, including payments to cottonseed producers42 and ‘market loss assistance’ payments.43
4. Crop insurance
In order to protect farmers against natural disasters, Congress enacted the Federal Crop Insurance Act,44 pursuant to which farmers may obtain insurance against crop losses caused by natural disaster.45 Insurance is provided through private insurers at subsidized rates – the USDA’s Risk Management Agency pays over 50% of the premiums and makes additional payments to the private insurers to cover administrative costs.46 In addition, any losses in excess of premiums paid are borne by the government.47
5. Export credit guarantees
Federal legislation requires the CCC to make available on an annual basis at least $5.5 billion in export credit guarantees,48 which are government guarantees on the repayment of credit extended by private lenders to finance exporter sales of cotton and other agricultural commodities. The purpose of export credit guarantee programs49 is to encourage exports of agricultural products to foreign countries where financing may not be available. Before the US Cotton Subsidies decision, the US government consistently took the position that WTO subsidy rules do not apply to export credit guarantee programs.50
6. ‘Step 2’ payments
‘Step 2’ is the unofficial name for the Upland Cotton User Marketing Certificate Program, one of several competitiveness programs designed to assist US farmers when agricultural commodity prices are not internationally competitive.51 The Step 2 program encourages buyers (such as yarn and textile manufacturers and cotton exporters) to purchase domestically produced cotton. The program provides a subsidy payment for documented sales by exporters or purchases by users of US-grown cotton when the lowest price for US cotton exceeds a benchmark price for Northern European cotton over a consecutive four-week period.52 Between 1995 and 2003, the government spent over $2 billion in payments under this program, $1.2 billion of which accrued to only fourteen cotton mills and cotton exporters.53
Aggregate levels of support to US cotton producers under these and other programs peaked during the years at issue in the US Cotton Subsidies dispute.
