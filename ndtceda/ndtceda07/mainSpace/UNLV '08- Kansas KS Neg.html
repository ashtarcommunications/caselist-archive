Resolutionality/ Framework**

A. interpretation-
1. the affirmative must defend a literal interpretation of the resolution- the usfg should decrease its agricultural subsidies
2. the affirmative must defend the united states federal government- this is the central government in washington dc
3. the affirmative must defend the resolution as the ending point of their advocacy- they cannot start from the resolution and come to a different conclusion
b. voting issue- the question of the resolution is a prior question that is necessary for debate
c. reasons to prefer-
1. predictable ground- the resolution provides a predictable set of arguments that allows negatives to be prepared to engage the affirmative in debate about the merits of the resolution, which allows better clash- that’s the biggest impact because it’s the only form of education unique to debate
2. switch-side debate is good- each team is allowed to argue both sides of the resolution- this allows us to best understand all issues
3. balance of arguments- division of ground between the aff and neg is inevitable- allowing the aff to make this division ensures they will always disadvantage the negative. the resolution is written to ensure a balance of arguments for both sides and provides a neutral distinction for aff and neg ground
4. limits- allowing the affiramtive to expand their advocacy outside of a literal interpretation of the resolution allows an infinite amount of advocacies that leaves the negative unprepared
5. extra-rez- if the affirmative claims advantages or reasons to vote that are not results of usfg action this is extra-resolutional. this is unpredictable and destroys negative ground while allowing an infinite amount of aff advocacies that the neg can’t leverage offense against- that’s an independent voter for fairness
