Observation 1: Property and Rationality

A. Western conceptions of property have devolved into a rationalized process of transaction and production. What was once a moral bond among community members has been reduced to a logic of “use value,” stripped of its former ethic.
Bryan, 2000 [Bradley, Property as Ontology: On Aboriginal and English Understandings of Ownership, The Canadian Journal of Law & Jurisprudence - 13 Can. J.L. & Juris. 3, Lexis]

B. This rationalistic understanding of property excludes all other views. The language of transaction can only comprehend truth claims that fit its model. Thus, it requires all assertions be couched in its language the language of transaction.
Bryan, 2000 [Bradley, Property as Ontology: On Aboriginal and English Understandings of Ownership, The Canadian Journal of Law & Jurisprudence - 13 Can. J.L. & Juris. 3, Lexis]

C. Our conception of property signifies our ontology – it organizes the way that we relate to each other and the world.
Bryan, 2000 [Bradley, Property as Ontology: On Aboriginal and English Understandings of Ownership, The Canadian Journal of Law & Jurisprudence - 13 Can. J.L. & Juris. 3, Lexis]

D. This language of rationalization that forms the foundation of Western property is the
essence of technology. It enframes the world and humanity in the form of standing reserve,
inventory to be stored for later use and transaction.
Bryan, 2000 [Bradley, Property as Ontology: On Aboriginal and English Understandings of Ownership, The Canadian Journal of Law &
Jurisprudence - 13 Can. J.L. & Juris. 3, Lexis]

E. The enframing of being is more dangerous than the complete annihilation of the earth. Even if we survive a nuclear war there will be no ontological clearing for us to exist in.
Zimmerman, 1994 [Michael E. Zimmerman, Chair of Philosophy at Tulane University, Contesting earth’s future : radical ecology and postmodernity, 1994 pps. 119-20]

Advocacy: Thus, we advocate the use of poetics to reject the essence of technology present in Western property law.

Observation 2: A New Ontology

A. Poetics can reconfigure existing language and go beyond the status quo enframing of being. Poets and philosophers are the unrecognized legislators of the world.
Jenkins 6 (Martin Pathways School of Philosophy, “Heidegger, Technology, and Our Future,” Philosophy Pathways, Issue 123, http://www.philosophos.com/philosophy_article_137.html)

B. The dominance of technology can easily co-opt any attempt to stand outside the system. The poetic is the only way to resist technological thought.
Foltz 95 (Bruce V. Foltz, Inhabiting the Earth: Heidegger, Environmental Ethics, and the
Metaphysics of Nature, 1995, p. 166.)


C. Ontology is the ground of all human understanding. The question of being is essential to real practical and political thought, in addition to authentic freedom.
Cropsey 87 (Josepth, Professor of Philosophy at the University of Chicago, “History of Political Philosophy,” 891)

Observation 3: Debate as Technology


We are guilty of two bad assumptions about the nature of language. First, that its only purpose is to communicate facts, and second that our use of technical prosaic language is the norm and that poetry and metaphor are abnormal.
Polt, 1998 [Richard, Heidegger: An Introduction, Routledge, 1998]

Finally, our argument is that we should apply Heidegger’s critique of technological thinking to debate itself. In the face of topicality, in the face of framework, we ask you to do nothing. It is only when we have reached the paradox of understanding that it is our systems that have created the problems they were designed to solve that we can begin to open up new possibilities. We do not have a succinct alternative to the status quo interpretation of debate, and that is our point. The better interpretation can only be dreamed after we have rejected technology’s grip on our language and thinking.
McWhorter 92 (Ladelle, Professor of Philosophy and Women's Studies Department of Philosophy University of Richmond, “Guilt as Management Technology: A Call to Heideggerian Reflection,” Heidegger and the Earth, 1-9)
