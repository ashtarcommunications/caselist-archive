The First Fight

Human activity is characterized by negation: When I eat an apple, I negate the apple in its current form in order to create something else - energy for myself. When I build a house, I must negate a forest in it's current form to transform it into something else. However, this negation isn't enough to sustain us. This can't explain why people engage in such wholesale destruction and negation - like when we kill and die for country, honor, or love. Human behavior is unique in our desire for the recognition of others; we want respect, we want love, and we want adoration. Go back to the beginning of civilization and you will find The First Fight, a fight to the death between two humans for pure recognition.

Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel. "Autonomy and Dependence of Self-Consciousness: Mastery and Slavery." Phenomenology of Spirit. Originally published in 1807. Reproduced in Introduction to the Reading of Hegel: Lectures on The Phenomenology of Spirit. By Alexandre Kojeve. Assembled by Raymond Queneau. Edited by Allan Bloom. Translated from French by James H. Nichols. 1980. Pages 3-9.


The First Fight gives rise to a Master and a Slave. The Slave was unable to overcome their given-reality and risk death, they were a slave to their material reality. Thus they yielded to the master. But by laboring under the fear of death they overcome it, freeing themselves from nature, from their material reality, and from their master.

Alexandre Kojeve. Originally produced 1933-1939. Introduction to the Reading of Hegel: Lectures on The Phenomenology of Spirit. Assembled by Raymond Queneau. Edited by Allan Bloom. Translated from French by James H. Nichols. 1980. Pages 46-49.


Next, Something has Gone Terribly Wrong.

Nietzsche saw that this is not how the slave responded. Rather than overcome the fear of death, they merely moved the object of the question from this world to another. They gave up hope of overcoming the master, accepted their position as slaves, embraced their weakness, for morality and salvation in the next world. This is the invention of slave morality, manifested as heaven in Christian philosophy or the ideal world in Platonic philosophy.

Adrian Del Caro. Grounding the Nietzsche Rhetoric of Earth. 2004. Page 366-367.


Next is Negated Negating Negativity and The Last Man.

These "good and just" slaves seek to preserve. All human action is destruction and creation, but the slave, deluded into thinking they know what is moral and good, don't allow destruction. They can not accept change. They have what they think is moral, and that must be preserved at all cost. No longer can we overcome ourselves or the world; this can only result in the degeneration of human beings into the last man.

Hugo Reinert (Cambridge University) and Erik S. Reinert (The Other Canon Foundation, Norway and Tallinn University of Technology). "Creative Destruction in Economics: Nietzsche, Sombart, Schumpeter." While the negative posits abstract values outside human beings and then asks us to lay down our lives for them, we demand precisely the opposite: You must lay down your life for nothing, you must die for nothing. You must engage in that First Fight to the death and risk dying. We may die, that is a definite possibility, but only when we've done this can we live life.
